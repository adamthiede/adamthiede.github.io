On Church Politics

[link—standalone]

Occasionally I will find myself in a situation where I have to defend something stupid the Catholic Church has done. This is not my job and I am not good at it. Usually it comes up with a family member or two who is really into Church Politics, or some friends who are non-practicing, non-denominational, or fallen-away Christians.

St. Peter, the man Jesus chose to lead his Church, denied the Lord three times on the day of his death; Jesus told him a few hours before he was going to do it, and Peter still did it! The Church is made of flawed humans, and they (we) make many mistakes, but that doesn't make the teachings any less true or God any less real. If we want to go all Cancel Culture on anyone who does anything wrong even once, and disregard all the good things he or she does, that leaves us in a pretty sorry state. Might as well throw all art and science out the window.

The impact most people will have on Church politics is vanishingly small. Nobody I know lives in Vatican City or Rome (Except for my cousin about 20 years ago). All we can do, as "normal" folks, Americans, half a world away from all this stuff, is to pray for the Church and its leaders, and try to find a good parish to go to, and support it. Be a friend to your Priest! We will make a greater impact at home, weathering the storms of life, and being good examples to those around us.

On the flip side, writing vicious articles about dead men who can't defend themselves or spreading around the latest gossip about which Cardinal did that, and what the Pope did wrong this time, or the scandals happening in the Church... we already know. It's not news! This organization is really messed up sometimes! But the errors of men don't mean we need God less. No one argues that the Church instructs, approves of, and endorses these awful behaviors. They obviously condemn it. And to air the dirty laundry out in public all the time would only dishearten those weaker in faith. Much in the same way parents don't let their young children know of all their struggles.

So, Church Politicking - please don't. It does not help you, or me, or anyone. Pray for the Church. Attend the Sacraments. Live well. That's the best we can do.

Turning The Cheek and Tech Independence

[link—standalone]

In Matthew 5, Jesus commands us not to take retaliatory action against evildoers.

Engaging in violence with our enemies would not only continue to allow them to damage their own souls, but would bring us down as well. If we are commanded to be perfect, this is not perfect action. Going the extra mile (even literally, in the case of one of Jesus' examples!) for those who bear us ill will is an act of service. Kindness and love inspire others to kindness and love, as hate and violence only perpetuate the same.

There is no better way to defuse an aggressive situation than by de-escalation. That is what Jesus is asking of us. He is not asking us to encourage or fan the flames of evil behavior. It was hard for me to pick this up at first. Why would the Lord ask us to encourage violence and abuse? He is not - love is willing the good of the other, and loving one's enemies means enabling good behavior, or defusing bad behavior. Like the Bishop in Les Misérables who told the police he had given to Jean Valjean the items that Valjean had stolen, our enemy can commit no crime if we freely give. This act of love shocked Jean Valjean, and inspired him to truly become a better man. Critically, these acts of selfless love do not take part in the sin of our enemies. They do not enable. They do not make us co-conspirators, leading the other to continue to sin. Love brings others out of sin and into freedom.

On Technology: when a large corporation abuses its users, the employees who take part in this are complicit. I worked at Oracle and helped build a product that spied on people. This was wrong. Those who use the products put forth by these companies are enabling their evil and should cease doing so. An act of love, in this case, is spending one's own money and time to help others break out of dependence on these companies. (Self-hosting email or XMPP or Nextcloud comes to mind.) De-escalation, for this situation, is just a peaceful refusal to use any of this bad technology. It is important to meet people where they are, and offer an olive branch, a compromise, with the understanding that the eventual goal is to disable all this abusive technology entirely by drying up its pool of users.

Standing against evil should not be about raising the sword and harming our enemies. Evil exists and attempts to corrupt each one of us, and the best way to stand against it is to love others into goodness through sacrifice and gentleness. This is a way I know how to help others. I know I am pushy or passionate at times, and could stand to improve. When I fail, dear reader, help me get back on the right track. I'll do what I can to help you, too.

OpenBSD Wireguard

[link—standalone]

I've been attempting to use OpenBSD both as my main server OS and main desktop OS for a number of years now. It's a coherent, understandable, simple system, and I really like it. There are plenty of issues it has that Linux does not, mostly due to the popularity differences, but many of these problems are trivial and easy enough to work around. (Some are not, like vmd not supporting multi-core virtual machines!) But one of the easier things is Wireguard.

Wireguard is pretty popular, very simple to set up, and it's been in OpenBSD for a few years now, so it's pretty stable. I hand-rolled one server's setup not too long ago, but just now have put together some scripts for it.

So anyway, to start the new year off right, here's some shell scripts! They aren't heavily tested but at least the new.sh script works great for me. Wireguard was the only thing I found on OpenBSD that wasn't super simple and understandable with only a single man page. (That's fair - it's not exactly part of the main code base!)
