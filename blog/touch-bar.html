Touch Bar

[link—standalone]

In 2016, the Apple Computer company unveiled the "touch bar" Mac laptops. If you don't know about them, look at your keyboard, and imagine a thin long screen replacing row of keys that Escape lives on. Instead of physical keys, you have a touch screen that dynamically switches to the appropriate context of whatever program you're running.

The presentation heralded this as the next big revolutionary innovation in laptop computers, on par with laptop trackpads, WiFi, flash storage, and high-resolution displays. A screen running on a second computer in your computer, that replaces Escape and the F1-F12 keys, is on par with putting WiFi in laptops.

The idea of a more dynamic keyboard was hardly revolutionary or unique, and Apple's wasn't even the best version. Real implementations happened almost a decade earlier with the 2008, with the Optimus Maximus keyboard and its successors. The Original Razer Blade from 2012 made it popular by integrating it with games and useful software. They were both hard to miss if you paid attention to tech at the time. So hardly an innovative idea, and not one that was useful enough to drive mass consumption, until Apple did it. (Note that neither of these examples removed the tactility of the keys, or took away an escape key.)

So today at 5 AM, trying to troubleshoot a work alert on my Touch Bar equipped computer, I was unable to decrease the searing brightness of the screen because the Touch Bar had crashed. Because of course since the Touch Bar is its own separate computer, it can crash independently of the main computer, and doesn't have the foresight to restart itself. It remained dark save for the occasional inexplicable flash of bright white. Only a series of three reboots and deeply nerdy hold-these-keys-for-seven-seconds chords restarted the Touch Bar. While I worked to clear the alert, I was thankful past me had remapped caps lock to escape, because of course this has happened before.

If it doesn't have to be a computer, it really shouldn't be a computer. This goes for speakers, microwaves, clocks, thermostats, trash cans, and apparently keyboards too. Because if it's a computer, it will fail in unpredictable ways, and frustrate or harm users. Just like my Touch Bar.
