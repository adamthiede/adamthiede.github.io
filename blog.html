Discrete Tools

[link—standalone]

I carry a Leatherman with me everywhere I go. It's nice to have a slightly-more-capable pocketknife on hand, in a pinch. In my garage, I use my "real" tools, since the Leatherman makes for a poor knife, lousy pliers, and a hardly serviceable screwdriver. But it does have them all.

In contrast to the above multi-tool, I also own a lot of small, cheap devices that I only really use for one thing each. While the wastefulness doesn't make me feel great, and the amount of space things take up can be a burden, I belive it makes for a healthier lifestyle. Allow me to explain.

When I'm reading a book on my e-reader device, a stray message, email, or update will not distract me, because that thing doesn't have that functionality. Listening to music or podcasts on my media player is the same - no potential distractions, because the device only does one thing. No matter how far smartphone cameras have come in the past few years, even a decade-old SLR will knock their socks off if the conditions are less than perfect - and you can't miss the perfect shot due to an incoming phone call. Turning off your alarm in the morning shouldn't also be an on-ramp right into the wailing and grinding of teeth that is social media. You deserve time to wake up.

A dedicated device forces you to be deliberate about your choices. While integration is nice, and I marveled at the utility of early smartphones as pocket computers, it just turns out they're bad at pictures, reading, gaming, playing music, watching videos, and email, but they can be bad at all of those things at once. They're built for distraction and task-thrashing and end up hurting more than they help. Cramming everything you do in a smartphone is pretty magical, but also a pretty dumb idea. I could pry open that case with the mini screwdriver on my multi-tool, but I'd end up hurting myself or the object. Instead, I reach for a little plastic pry tool that is built to do the job right. A competent contractor carriers around a fully loaded tool kit, full of individual tools, not just a tiny foldable all-in-one.

Dear Reader, I encourage you to examine what in your life is a Leatherman, and how that functionality can be broken out into task-specific objects. Think about how you can improve your life by finding single-task objects or units to replace a catch-all. Chances are, the nice set of individual tools you find will not only be higher quality, produce better results, and be more pleasant to use, but also keep you focused on the task at hand, and in the end, make you happier.

How To Defeat Apple's Wrongthink Hash-Matching

[link—standalone]

Time to be scared, and time to make a change. Apple's just announced they are going to plant a database of pattern-matches of child abuse material on your phone, and use that to scan all your photos to make sure you don't have any. Pretty neat, right? Anyone downloads child abuse material on their iPhone, it blurs it out, and the cops show up at their house in 15 minutes flat. Sounds like the right thing to do.

Well, it's unfortunately not. You can't build a system like they have without now enabling governments to say "hey, also insert these hashes of VPN software or Tiananman Square Tank Man photos", and not being able to deny them, because of course Apple won't. This sets a precedent, too - trustworthy encrypted messaging programs like Signal may now also be strong-armed into adding this scanning database functionality to their app in order to stay on the app store.

There's a laundry list of other professionals and their complaints laid out in this open letter.

Issuing a small update to this post, but leaving the original bits intact.
Apparently they aren't using direct cryptographic hashing, but a different way to detect smaller patterns called perceptual hashing. Apple's announcement has a link to a PDF full of technical details which mentions perceptual hashing by name (the actual announcment doesn't), and this site has a decent explanation of why it's also not that great. My method below will not work to change perceptual hashes. But maybe rotating or flipping the image will? Changing the color profile? Applying a noise filter? Who knows. Probably pretty easy to automate those, too. Bad guys already know how to evade this stuff, and they're probably not storing their stash on iPhones. This is just going to wind up with regular folks getting false-positives. Anyway, update over.

The worst thing about this is that it's comically easy to defeat hash-based matching. Here's how!

$ md5sum image1.png
e8d55c2ae485226ac88fa9520a6a787d image1.png
$ echo " " | tee -a image1.png
$ md5sum image1.png
26bc96b4f8d9cc070c5ea4df969a1152 image1.png

An explanation - I took the unique hash of an image and altered it by adding a single space to the end of the data. You can add multiple spaces, or add some other data that doesn't matter to the structure of the image format you're not using, or open it in MS Paint and color a single pixel, or take a screenshot and share the screenshot. There are so many different ways to alter the hash of an image, and many can be done without losing quality. I could even do this to my whole Pictures folder at once, using a simple one-liner:
find ~/Pictures -type f -iname '*.png' -iname '*.jpg' -exec echo ' ' | tee -a {} \;

How long until it's used for pirated music or old Game Boy ROMs? How about things like youtube-dl? How about VPN software or Tor Browser? Maybe a few years down the line, it scans for not-woke-enough memes. If you own guns it reports on you to Big Brother. If you go to Church, it tells you you're doing wrongthink and prevents you from getting directions to go there again. (They literally say "at-risk thoughts and behavior" in the screenshot at the bottom of their page.)

Apple is an evil company doing evil things, and telling us to "think of the children!" You should throw your iPhone in a lake, buy a Pixel 4a or 3a in cash, install CalyxOS or GrapheneOS, and only use Free Software. Email me. I'll help you do it.

Linux on the Asus L210

[link—standalone]

I went through a bunch of pain at work, got paid overtime, and bought a new toy. That toy is the Asus L210 laptop. There's a pretty decent video review here, which describes feautures, specs, and performance.

Two main reasons I bought this machine: size and silence.

Size: This machine is tiny. It has an 11" screen and reminds me a lot of my netbooks - a Dell Mini 9 and Acer Aspire One. In fact, it's a little bigger than the netbooks, but that just means it's got a wider keyboard, which is much more comfortable to type on. It is orders of magnitude thinner and lighter, making it a nice travel machine, and a great upgrade from the old 32-bit systems.

Silence: No fan. No spinning hard drive. Low-power CPU and motherboard by Asus, so almost no coil whine. No matter what I do this machine won't make noise.

I have to mention upgradability - usually you don't get that on these tiny budget machines.
The Wi-Fi card is some Realtek piece of junk that's not in Linux yet (but does have a driver you can manually install). It is swappable and there doesn't appear to be a whitelist. I put in a several-year-old Intel card I had laying around and it works great. You can't swap the RAM or CPU or eMMC, but there is an NVMe slot. I left Windows 10 on the internal eMMC, and put a WD Blue drive in the NVMe slot for a seamless dual-boot.

Linux: Fedora 34 works great. Arch and Ubuntu 20.04 live ISOs boot and function properly as well. The GPU has great hardware decoding capability so mpv and vlc will play back video without high CPU usage. The system sleeps/wakes great, headphones with an inline mic (TRRS) work well and the mic is detected, brightness is controllable, the hotkeys work great, and the system boots with the Fn-lock on - F keys are F keys, not media keys, but there is an indicator light and you can turn it off with Fn+Esc.

Windows 10: it came with Windows 10 in S mode and a whole ton of bloatware. I wiped and reinstalled with a clean, official Windows 10 Home download from Microsoft. It activated itself and installed all the updates and drivers just fine. It performs decently but not as well as Linux. Windows needs more than 4GB RAM. It plays Halo Combat Evolved at a high framerate though.

BIOS: The BIOS looks like a slightly stripped down version of an Asus motherboard BIOS. It's shockingly full featured and well designed for a cheap laptop. You can update the BIOS using a tool in the BIOS. The updates from Asus's website can be loaded from a FAT32 USB stick (or your ESP if you're feeling dangerous). This is a great feature for non-Windows users - no hard Windows requirement to update the BIOS. No LVFS, but you can't win 'em all.

Hardware: It's cheap but it all works. USB-C does not charge the laptop, nor will it oputput display. Bummer, but expected. The keyboard is not backlit, but superb for this size. The layout is as expected for laptops. The power button is above backspace like many modern machines, but it is a stiffer press than the rest of the keyboard. The trackpad has a numpad on it that only works in Windows. That's fine, a touch numpad totally misses the point anyway. The enter key has a yellow border on it. Reminds me of the old Thinkpad keyboards' blue enter key. The stock Wi-Fi chip issues aside, it only has one antenna, so Wi-Fi no matter what card you put in is going to be slow, but acceptable.

Conclusion

All in all, a great little machine for the money and surprisingly capable. If you spend a few bucks, you can get a Linux-compatible Wi-Fi card. And for $50 on top of that, a nice NVMe SSD. For around $300 total this is a fantastic portable computer to run Linux on. I recommend it if it's a companion to a more powerful desktop or server, where you can offload more resource-intensive tasks.

I very much don't recommend this laptop if you're going to run Windows, or Linux with a more resource-heavy desktop environment, and a bunch of electron apps. 4GB RAM is just not enough, constantly swapping to the eMMC is going to make things perceptibly slow, and there's no upgradability. I run sway with a lot of terminal/CLI programs, and that setup works very well, mainly because of its lightweight nature and low resource usage.

Better Doesn't Mean Easy

[link—standalone]

Most of my life, I've been in pursuit of bettering myself at the expense of my time and effort, because my whole life I've been young and full of energy. This is the way young people operate - we sacrifice our time, energy, and effort to achieve high-minded ideals. It's a good thing to do - it's how people end up changing the world. As people get older, they convince themselves they're OK with mediocrity, or not meeting their goals, because they get tired, lazy, or stuck in their ways.

This post is about the value of work. Exercise, or physical labor in general, is something we all acknowledge is good for us, but it's hard. Going for a run, lifting weights, building something, or spending a weekend doing yard work, is not easy. By definition you're expending a lot of energy, tearing your muscle fibers, getting sweaty, and feeling pain. It's not very convenient, but it's indisputably good, and nobody can help you do it. You can't have someone do the work for you - at some point, they cross the line between spotting your bench press, and just lifting the bar for you.

Convenience maximalists always end up getting played for a fool by charlatans. This isn't unique to any industry. People know to haggle on prices with car salesmen, hire an independent inspector before buying a home, look at ingredient lists before buying food, and read contracts before signing them. This is all common sense so far.

Complex problems often require complex or specialized tools. Many musical instruments are complicated tools, difficult to master, and require ongoing maintenance. Shop tools are powerful, but dangerous if used improperly. Both video-editing software and CAD software are incredibly complex and deep tools, but the complexity is required for the tasks they perform. In any of these cases, hiding the complexity or making a more simplified version of the thing would not make it more accessible, because the task itself isn't simple in the first place. It would dumb down the tool such that it either was missing critical functionality, or couldn't perform the task at all.

People shouldn't shy away from hard things. And of course, my angle of attack is about computers. Many people use computers for critical things in their life, things they consider incredibly valuable, but since they don't really know how to use computers, they end up relying on those who would abuse them, or otherwise take advantage of their laziness.

Case and point: Internet Service Providers (ISP) and renting modems. When you pay Comcast or Time Warner or whoever for Internet service, they dark-pattern you into renting a garbage-tier modem/router/wifi combo box, and you end up paying orders of magnitude more than it's worth. It's a Leatherman: it does all the things, it's kinda bad at all of them, but it does do all the things. In ISP-provided boxes, the settings are limited, they're often full of unpatched security holes, and the ISP has full control over the device. Anyone with any sense buys a modem, then a separate router that they have full control over, and configures it for maximum security and independence. While the upfront cost is higher, and there is about an hour of research involved, many people won't do it, because it's not easy enough.

I could go on and on here - password managers, VPN services, companies saying "we value your privacy", cloud backup systems, streaming services, antivirus... the list of computer scams ranges far and wide. Most people don't even realize they're getting scammed.

That's because even the most respectable and upstanding of individuals have become soft, whiny smooth brains when it comes to computers. They throw their hands up and decide that since the problem is "too hard" it's not worth solving. This is not the world I want to create. When I imagine a future, I imagine driven, independent individuals learning and creating, not pacified idiots tapping away at social media on their iPhones.

There's nothing wrong with letting your doctor handle your medicines or your mechanic handle your engine maintenance. But in the modern world, especially with computers, convenient things are often harmful - to stay safe, stay sane, and get something truly better, you just have to lift the bar yourself.

If we want the world to get better, we have to envision the better that we want, and work hard for it.

Touch Bar

[link—standalone]

In 2016, the Apple Computer company unveiled the "touch bar" Mac laptops. If you don't know about them, look at your keyboard, and imagine a thin long screen replacing row of keys that Escape lives on. Instead of physical keys, you have a touch screen that dynamically switches to the appropriate context of whatever program you're running.

The presentation heralded this as the next big revolutionary innovation in laptop computers, on par with laptop trackpads, WiFi, flash storage, and high-resolution displays. A screen running on a second computer in your computer, that replaces Escape and the F1-F12 keys, is on par with putting WiFi in laptops.

The idea of a more dynamic keyboard was hardly revolutionary or unique, and Apple's wasn't even the best version. Real implementations happened almost a decade earlier with the 2008, with the Optimus Maximus keyboard and its successors. The Original Razer Blade from 2012 made it popular by integrating it with games and useful software. They were both hard to miss if you paid attention to tech at the time. So hardly an innovative idea, and not one that was useful enough to drive mass consumption, until Apple did it. (Note that neither of these examples removed the tactility of the keys, or took away an escape key.)

So today at 5 AM, trying to troubleshoot a work alert on my Touch Bar equipped computer, I was unable to decrease the searing brightness of the screen because the Touch Bar had crashed. Because of course since the Touch Bar is its own separate computer, it can crash independently of the main computer, and doesn't have the foresight to restart itself. It remained dark save for the occasional inexplicable flash of bright white. Only a series of three reboots and deeply nerdy hold-these-keys-for-seven-seconds chords restarted the Touch Bar. While I worked to clear the alert, I was thankful past me had remapped caps lock to escape, because of course this has happened before.

If it doesn't have to be a computer, it really shouldn't be a computer. This goes for speakers, microwaves, clocks, thermostats, trash cans, and apparently keyboards too. Because if it's a computer, it will fail in unpredictable ways, and frustrate or harm users. Just like my Touch Bar.

Days Off and Arch Installer

[link—standalone]

I had a few days off to take a short vacation with my dad. It was a lovely time, most of it. Didn't get good sleep but it was worth it.

Since I have an extra day off, I did some mulling over Arch Linux. There's a new official installer, and it's kind of neat. Does quite a bit wrong, but a few things right:

  • Does a bunch of boring stuff quickly and gets it out of the way
  • Integrates drive encryption
  • Offers to disable the root account and add a user to sudoers
  • Offers a lot of flexibility of desktop and drive choices
  • systemd-boot instead of grub by default
However it's not perfect. Even though there is an installer, it doesn't give you a "ready to go" system out of the box - you still have to know what to do, or read the wiki. Printing won't work unless you enable cups. The network is broken until you enable networkmanager. You should install a firewall and you probably don't need the gnome-extras packages.

2 things:

  1. systemd is truly eating everything. I do quite like systemd-networkd, systemd-resolvd, systemd-boot, though; they make everything easier and more consistent. Linux is not about choice, and Arch doesn't offer a non-systemd choice, but you do have the choice to use other network and boot managers. You truly can make systemd just an init system, but when it's good at other things too, why not?
  2. At critical moments in my life I've used Arch Linux on my primary machine, because I had the free time and I do love Arch. But when things break and I don't have time to fix them, I usually give up and use Fedora where I have no problems. It's like a slightly safer, slightly slower Arch that breaks far less frequently.
Anyway. Arch is cool, there's an installer now, and you probably still shouldn't use it if you don't have the free time for it being Arch every now and again. If you have a day off, though, and want to spend it tweaking Arch, have fun.

A Short Review of CalyxOS

[link—standalone]

Smartphones are pretty awful. By default they required accounts with creepy companies to do anything useful, and track the heck out of users (even if they are told not to). Using LineageOS without Google Apps is decent way to use a smartphone without the anti-features, but requires a supported device, and requires leaving the bootloader unlocked, which is seen as a vulnerability. Android One devices, once stripped of the usual garbage Google apps via adb, run a clean enough version of Android, and programs like NetGuard can be used to only allow trusted apps to have network access, but it prevents the use of other VPN apps, like WireGuard or OpenVPN.

Enter CalyxOS - an Android OS based on mainline AOSP, but with a number of privacy and usability tweaks thrown in. In Calyx, users can disable network access entirely for apps in the regular permissions manager. F-Droid, an "Open Source Apps Only" app store, is included. So is "Aurora Store", a way to get Google Play apps without using the Play Store or using a Google account - it even lists known trackers in a report in each app's description. CalyxOS' installation allows and suggests re-locking the bootloader. MicroG, an open-source re-implementation of Google's services, allows apps that require it to function, while remaining dormant for those who don't. It also gets timely security updates. The Pixel 2, no longer supported by Google's OS updates, is still supported by CalyxOS, so it also breathes life into perfecly fine phones that have fallen off the hilariously short support lifecycle.

The main downsides are limited device support and the command-line based installer, which limits adoption for "normals" (but was a plus for me). Also, a small subset of proprietary apps (that shouldn't be used in the first place, tsk tsk) don't work quite right without tweaking. The CalyxOS User Communities are a helpful resource - most of the app compatibility problems have already been solved and a search will dig up solutions in minutes. Plexus is a good resource for this as well.

Overall, CalyxOS provides a highly usable Android experience, promotes Open Source software first, doesn't reduce the regular Android security model, and is made with user interests first. There's no reason not to use it on supported devices, if you have one, and if you don't, the Pixel 3a can be purchased for less than $200 these days. Give it a go if you care about your privacy and the long-term usability of your smartphone.

Pragmatism

[link—standalone]

- Inspired by Bring Your Own Client by Geoffrey Litt -


For a number of years I've been interested in "minimal" cell phones - alternatives to the duopoly of iOS and Android, like the Light Phone and similar devices. There is a draw there for me - I don't like using phones, I tend to carry a laptop wherever I go, I made people use Signal so I could text from my laptop, I'm not a fan of the way phones operate, and all I really need in that case is a data modem for the laptop. For me, this is an ideal way to operate and live my life - I get messages when I want to, the phone is effectively a data modem, and maybe it plays music, too. I would prefer not to communicate with it unless I'm under duress, since unlike real computers, it's not a device that can really be made secure without a lot of effort and modification. But nobody else is going to allow me to work like this.

If I want to own a stick-shift car, I can do that. If I want to have a left-handed soldering iron, I can do that, right-handers be damned. If I want to use a terminal-only Arch Linux setup and check my email in an esoteric command-line email app, I'm free to do that. Doesn't matter that you use Outlook on Windows or Gmail on an Android phone. We can still exchange emails.

But it all falls down when it comes to phones, and phone apps, which is just the way people work now. I don't really like Signal that much. It's the best thing I've ever done for my social circle, but it requires a phone number, depends on a smartphone app, and the desktop app is electron. The Signal people refuse to make their program federated or accept third-party clients because it would slow down their pace of innovation, or reflect poorly on their service. I get it, makes sense. They want to be free to innovate and quickly push new features to everyone.

With smartphones, smartphone apps, and things revolving around the smartphone sphere, I can't bring my own client. There is lock-in to one of the two awful platforms, with very few acceptable ways to alleviate the privacy and security concerns. The endless consumerism means I have to purchase a new phone every few years, even when I don't want to, because the "old" one is abandoned and dangerously insecure. I can't make the type of decisions I would want to - independent choices based on my personal values of Freedom, minimalism, privacy, and decentralization. This bothers me quite a bit.

I want to use cool technical stuff. Stuff that's more open, secure, private, interesting, and technically superior. I want to put my values first, much like many people refuse to buy products from China or only eat organic food. But the fact that people don't care about tech means I can't since I still have to talk to them. I tried it years ago, before Signal was a great solution, and people just ended up SMS or iMessaging my wife, using her as a middleman. That also puts a huge burden on her - she's not my secretary, and if my objective is "secure, private communication", this completely flaunts that. It makes my efforts useless.

What I really want is to bring my own client, everywhere, for everything. I want to bring my own weirdo phone that isn't like everyone else's iPhone or Android. I want to bring my own OS, my own desktop interface, my own software and hardware. I mandate that everything I use respect my freedom and privacy. I mandate that I can use all my software anonymously and without reservation. But as long as others are not willing to step up and do something a little more difficult, invest a bit of time into digital well-being, I can't do what I want, and it's mainstream Android phones and Electron apps for me. Pragmatism. I have to take what I can get from the things that work in ways I don't like. What other industry or area is like this? Is there another situation in which others can make a decision for you, and you're locked into something you don't like because of their poor choices?

Raspberry Pis and Spades

[link—standalone]

Raspberry Pis are great little computers. They work well, don't break the bank, and there's a lot of accessories and tutorials for beginners out there to get started. They can be a desktop, server, or portable system. There's no end to what you can do with them. People have used them for all sorts of things over the years.

Recently the Raspberry Pi Foundation automatically, silently, added a Microsoft repo to the operating system the provide, Raspberry Pi OS. They're proud of the fact that VSCode is now available for the Pis, but others were not so happy about it, claiming it was a "secret" addition and it enables Microsoft telemetry. As always, the truth is somewhere in the middle, and it's kind of boring. Yes, they added a repo to Raspberry Pi OS. Yes, you can disable it pretty easily. No, Microsoft's repo isn't going to replace your network drivers with spyware (although the capability is now there). They should not have added it on "Lite" (no desktop, console-only) installations of the OS or on hardware like the Pi Zero, because they wouldn't be able to run it anyway.

But let's call a spade a spade: the Raspberry Pi's website is all bout how you can use these computers to teach kids to code. They offer simple tools and tutorials to help get complete beginners started using computers. They're supposed to be easy coding/computer on-ramps for educational purposes. They're not meant to be cheap servers for experienced Linux users, which is how I see them most often used in my circles!

There should be no anger here. Just a realization that we, the admins, the people who noticed the new repo, were never the target market for these devices. We've been skating by for a while using these things as cheap "lite" home servers, running pi-hole, VPNs, and media servers, many of us not realizing we were shoving a round peg into a square hole.

The short-term solution for people like me is clear. If you must use a Raspberry Pi, at least don't use Raspberry Pi OS - it's almost Debian, but not quite. Debian does make official images for the Raspberry Pis, and do does Ubuntu. Pick your poison. They both work great. Their policies are clear on what software they include. The Raspberry Pi images are supported in the same way their x86 OS is. Using Raspberry Pi OS and pretending it's Debian is clearly not going to work for much longer, for the people that just want Debian.

Long-term, if what we want is a server, let's use server hardware and server OSs. We should be buying NUCs, PCEngines boards, Pine64 Single-Board Computers, or building our own home servers - things that were created with "power users" in mind. Not toys built for kids. Personally I've transitioned running all my Pi's network-critical systems into containers or VMs on my NAS, and the Pi is now a toy, or a game server. I'm using it for fun, to play, and to learn. (It also now runs Ubuntu, because I can expect consistency there, not unexpected changes I don't appreciate just to make it easier for kids to use.) Raspberry Pis are great, but let's call a spade a spade. They're not for us, and never were. We shouldn't expect them to be.

Rolling or Static

[link—standalone]

Software updates happen. Your operating system and its programs get updates all the time, and you should install those updates. Sometimes they provide bug fixes, security patches, or new features. Sometimes these changes are "breaking changes", or paradigm shifts that break workflows or existing configurations.

Rolling release is a model that supposes you take all the updates as they come, immediately, with little modification. This is like installing a program (say, a specific release of Docker, LibreOffice, or GIMP) and keeping it updated regularly as the creator pushes out new releases. Simple enough to understand - you get the changes as they come and update frequently but on no particular schedule. Rolling releases fit with the saying "every update is a security update". When software interacts in interesting ways, sometimes a simple bug can turn into a massive security hole, so it is important to keep systems and software patched.

A good example of a rolling release operating system is Arch Linux - packages arrive in the repositories as soon as they're released, and Arch doesn't have any particular release schedule. You just install once, and update forever. But you must update frequently or you'll be without security patches. Sometimes the software doesn't work too well together - it's not tested or specifically modified to integrate well with the rest of the system.

Static release is a model built for the enterprise, but beneficial to anyone who doesn't like surprises. You pick a base version of a program (LibreOffice 6.4 for example), and if there are any bug fixes or security patches that affect that particular version, you apply them. New versions come out, but being cautious, you save your major version upgrades for all your software for a specific interval. Example: every year in April, you go through the list of software you use, see if there are any new releases, and if those new versions have introduced breaking changes to your workflow. You then integrate the new software if you desire, apply fixes to the old software if you must keep that version, and carry on.

Static release is a harder model to understand. It requires a lot of work. Thankfully many people do that work for you. If you use a Linux-based operating system, you can get a number of static release systems. Red Hat Enterprise Linux releases a new version of their operating system every 4 years or so. That release is supported for ten years. You can continue using the same stuff you were using in 2014 (RHEL7) with the same software it came with, only with regular security fixes "backported" to the versions that were chosen at the time of release. Specific versions are chosen and tested against each other to make sure they work well. They bundle an old version of the Linux kernel, old versions of desktop programs, and old versions of server software. But this means users and businesses don't get surprised by random changes, and can rely on the system being stable and secure for a long time. They can plan their own software or infrastrucutre around it, since it doesn't change out from under them. Some long-term support releases of operating systems, like Ubuntu, are more frequent - they release every 2 years on the dot, and enterprises (and stability-concerned users) can count on this schedule. Every 2 years in May or June they can upgrade to the new version, and plan to do all their troubleshooting then, as opposed to random times throughout the year, when they're trying to get work done.

Some operating systems use a Semi-rolling model. Fedora, the "upstream" of RHEL (what RHEL is based on, before making significant changes) releases a new version every 6 months. These releases keep some desktop and server software at a single version for those six months, only applying bug fixes. But other software is updated as the updates come - the Linux kernel follows upstream releases closely, while GNOME, PHP, Python, gcc, and other programming tools and server software promise to only change every 6 months. This gets benefits of both sides - most of your stuff is as up to date as possible, but critical software doesn't change out from under you. You know every 6 months you'll have to examine the major upgrades for breaking changes, but the changes between bi-yearly release are usually minor. The reason for these cycles is so that the project can pick a date to do major changes and have milestones.

Rolling release models are not for the faint of heart. They require constant vigilance to make sure upcoming changes don't break things. Every update has the potential to render your desktop or web server unusable. You don't get to pick when things break or when there are major changes. You must update freuquently and that requires a lot of extra CPU usage and data usage, so not great for metered networks.

Static releases can be just as bad - old software won't work with new tools you want to use. When new hardware comes out, the old software won't support it, and you may have to wait months or years before the changes you're waiting for land in the operating system you're using. You get to choose when you do full release upgrades, but often times, it takes days or weeks to iron out all the things that have broken or changed releases. 2 to 5 years is a huge jump in version numbers and having been out of the loop will mean you have a lot of catching up to do.

Both releases are valid models. Home users or enthusiasts are likely more interested in rolling models - newer software as it comes usually causes no issues, fixes bugs, and provides more up to date hardware support. Entereprises, developers, and cautious users stick to static releases - less surprise, nothing changing out from under you in the middle of a project, and a solid long-term support strategy. Semi-rolling, in my opinion, gives you the best of both worlds without the downsides. But in the end it's up to preferences, goals, and needs of the individual.

Modern Cars

[link—standalone]

I went car shopping recently. In the latest episode of "they don't make 'em like they used to", I level complaints at modern automobiles.

  • Many cars have built-in touchscreens that replace functionality that used to be buttons and knobs. It is easier and safer to memorize where your buttons and knobs are, and not have to look at them to change volume, temperature, and other system features about your car. Cars like Teslas, where the entire control system is a big touchscreen, are more dangerous than my decade-old Toyota Camry - I know where all the knobs are, can find them by feel, and can adjust them without looking. (How is using a car touchscreen different than looking at your smartphone while driving, an activity illegal in many states?)
  • Newer cars often include computers with wireless connectivity. Obviously integrating a computer with opaque software that the user doesn't control is bad, but what's even worse is the security implications - often these systems ship with serious security vulnerabilities and never get fixed, allowing bad actors remote control of the vehicle or tracking of its location. The more complex the system, the more chances for exploitation.
  • Most vehicle's models are quite a bit larger than the same model that was shipping 10 or 15 years ago, with no increase in functionality.
  • Some new safety featues, like backup cameras or adaptive cruise control, are functional but not yet reliable, and are expensive to repair when damaged. It's a "false sense of security" thing. People are less likely to look around their car when they have a backup camera. You've also likely seen news of Tesla owners crashing their vehicles because "full self driving" features aren't exactly full self driving yet.
  • Personal complaint - CD drives are all but gone. You now have to look at a touchscreen to select music from an Internet service, or via smartphone integration. It's a global waste of energy and brings me back to the first point - it's easier to manipulate a CD case by feel than a touchscreen.

Cars are more safe than ever. They're more energy efficient and have a bunch of great new features. But not all of the features are good, and we should be discerning about what we as consumers accept. More safety and security - fantastic! More Internet connectivity, "smart" features, and controls transformed from buttons into touchscreens - get it out of here!

Protocols, Not Platforms

[link—standalone]

Over the past year I've been asked by family members and friends about platforms, apps, and services. "Should I use this app?" "Is this social media site secure?" "What do you think of this email provider?"

These are the wrong questions to ask.

The reason these questions are being asked is that people are getting more concerned with security, privacy, and longevity of the systems that they use. If a system is not secure and private, they're afraid they can't express their opinions or talk candidly to friends and family via the service. They've also noticed that services they use go away sometimes, or are offline for long periods of time. People don't want to have to constantly switch from SMS to iMessage to WhatsApp to Viber to Telegram to Signal, because change is hard, and shifting your whole social group to use a different system is a very difficult long-term effort.

The right question to ask is, "What tools can we use that leverage open, extensible, secure, protocols, ensuring longevity and privacy?

To understand what I'm talking about, you need to think about email. This is the extensible open platform people have the most experience with. You can send an email from your @gmail.com account, via the iPhone's mail app, to your friend's @yahoo.com address, who checks his email in Microsoft Outlook on a Windows PC. Which app you use doesn't matter here - you could use any number of email programs ("clients") to interface with your account. Gmail and Yahoo mail are just two fish in the sea of email servers. Email the protocol has been around for decades, and will work for decades more, even if Gmail and Yahoo go away. You can even run an email server in your house on an old spare computer, or on a rented cloud virtual machine, for less than $5 a month. This is the power of an extensible, open, decentralized protocol.

Focusing on protocols (the method and format of the data transmission) instead of platforms (who is holding and transmitting your data) puts the power in your hands. It also puts the responsibility in your hands. You can choose an email provider, or go about the herculean process to host email yourself. Hosting your data and services yourself requires a fair amount of knowledge, but it also makes you self-reliant, resilient to censorship, and insulated from the constant churn of platforms that exist, and then suddenly don't a few years later. Your server's downtime (for updates, etc.) happens on your terms, not Google's or Amazon's.

Extensible, open software I recommend:

  • Matrix Protocol for secure communication. Encryption built-in, many different clients available, servers are easy to host, and most importantly, it's open-source and respects your freedom.
  • XMPP/Jabber is the universal messaging standard. You've probably used it before without knowing it. Layering OMEMO or OTR encryption on top of XMPP is like a better version of Signal. Check here for a list of servers that support all the appropriate extensions required to run a secure XMPP conversation, or just start your own.
  • ActivityPub Protocol and its implementations Mastodon and Pleroma. Decentralized DIY social media. No algorithms, no censorship or oversight. Each server can choose its own rules.
  • PeerTube is DIY YouTube. It also implements ActivityPub, so people can comment on videos with their Pleroma or Mastodon accounts.
  • NextCloud is DIY Dropbox, with integrated contacts/calendars. Calendar and Contact syncing is done over the open DAV protocols, CalDAV and CardDAV, and comes for free with many email setups.
  • MailInABox if you must self-host your own email. Easy to set up, and keep secure. Also sets up a small Nextcloud instance.
  • This list contains more resources I didn't cover. Most of these things will integrate well with each other.

Signal had an outage the other day that lasted for over 24 hours. Messages were unreliable or didn't go through, and forget about calls. This happened because Signal is a single entity, and the only way you can use Signal is through their single point of failure - them. Decentralized protocols federate the responsibility to users, so if your server is down, your parents can still talk to their friends on different servers.

A couple guidelines to live by:

  • Open Source is a must.
  • If it requires a smartphone app, it's not worth your time.
  • Client and Server should have multiple implementations that run on many operating systems - you should be able to host the service, and interact with it, from any computer.

In a world where you can't trust surveillance-addicted megacorps to respect your privacy, and you can't trust that scrappy moralizing startups won't either fail or get acquired by the megacorps, the only reasonable choice is to trust individuals. The best individuals to trust are yourself, your family, and your friends - if every family hosted their own services, outages would be a thing of the past, privacy would be table stakes, surveillance would be much more difficult, and we'd all have control of our data. So do some research, and continue to ask the right questions.
