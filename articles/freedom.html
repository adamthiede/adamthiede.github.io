The Value of Freedom

It's not what you think.

When I talk about Freedom, and why it's the Most Important Value to me, I don't think people really understand what I mean, and therefore may be confused about why I hold this value so close. Freedom does not mean license - they are very different. While there are many definitions of freedom in various dictionaries, I am mostly avoiding those, since this is such an overburdened word and such a large concept. I will focus on the distinction between two types of Freedom, and why one is true Freedom, and the other is false.

To frame this essay, here are some things to know about me: I am a Catholic. There is absolute truth and natural law, created by a loving and intelligent God whose will we cannot fully comprehend. I am a GNU/Linux user, a homeowner, and a home server owner. Software Freedom, self-hosting, and Doing-It-Yourself are important to me, at the cost of consuming new products and following trends. My values are more important than instant gratification. I try to apply my values to everything I do.

Freedom To

Freedom To is essentially license. License is unrestricted total freedom. Do whatever you want, no regard for morals, the thoughts and feelings of others, religious beliefs, laws, tradition, forethought, or general good sense. Actions taken with license are not always bad, but usually inevitably license is used to hurt or take advantage of others. No restrictions, internal or external, give one the Freedom To do whatever one wants.

This total lack of rules and restrictions only works if everyone in its sphere of influence shares the same exact set of values and morals. Like Communism and Anarchy, it seems to work well on paper, and on very small scale. In reality, no one should have this license. We live in a world with other people, and obviously, one's freedom only extends up to where it infringes upon the rights of others.

This kind of freedom is a false freedom. It does not make one free. In the Catholic perspective, sin (bad deeds that go against the laws of God) will make one more susceptible to further sin. Sin is an affliction that warps one's ability to make correct and moral judgements. This is a rational line of thought too - a drug addiction or a bad habit is hard to break. Bad deeds are permissible with Freedom To, because there are no rules. Sin and addiction make one slaves to the sin itself, and the changes it effects on one's well-being (physical, mental, or spiritual). Sin digs a hole that is hard to climb out of, but easy and comforting to keep digging.

Examples of Freedom To are the aforementioned drug addictions, the hedonistic lifestyle of the "sexual liberation", allowing children to make choices they shouldn't (kids are dumb!), and encouraging instant gratification over learning and self-actualization. Things like using social media because it feels good, instead of sitting down and taking the purposeful time to compose a letter (or email), are included. Habitually fondling a smartphone instead of bringing a notebook and pen to write down one's thoughts, or a good book to read and ponder, are similar.

Freedom From

Freedom From is true freedom. It is like a set of wings. It's keys to the shackles. It's discipline, learning, and fulfillment. In true Freedom, one is not free to do whatever one desires. Desires are not inherently bad, but some desires are bad, and these need to be recognized, faced, and curbed with discipline and healthy habits. Freedom From is the ability to filter the good from the bad and choose the good, with help from religion and philosophy. Freedom From has restrictions, but they are restrictions imposed to make further freedoms available!

The chains of sin and slavery of bad habits will bind one to a life of misery. Moralizing one's choices and living a life of discipline keep one free of regrets. A life of Freedom To will lead to short-term pleasure, but eventually results in a broken-down body, crippling addictions, and ultimately a lack of fulfillment. Freedom From frees one from all these things. Restrictions are not imposed arbitrarily, but for one's own good. Most adults can remember that a parent's discipline felt bad in the moment, but resulted in character building that had a butterfly effect of benefits for adult life. "Eat your vegetables," "don't stay up too late," and "wear a helmet when you ride your bicycle," are all good examples of things that may not be fun, or pleasurable, or ideal now, but build good habits or prepare a young man (who may not be able to make rational or moral decisions) for a successful, happy, and fulfilling adult life, where he not only treats himself well, but holds others to this high standard as well.

Immediate and Long Term Effects

"Anti-fun" is a phrase detractors might use to describe the Freedom From philosophy. That's only kind of unfair -

Freedom From the distractions, restrictions, and impositions that result from improper or amoral behavior are consequences of a well-informed, careful, and disciplined life. Successful men and women don't chase every new pleasure and every latest trend. They plan, think, and then act. Freedom From is fundamentally long-term, and Freedom To is for the now.

In work, a lifestyle guided by Freedom From prioritizes learning before doing. Freedom To prioritizes getting things done fast. Almost always the speedy, immediate way is the wrong choice. Good things take time to get right, and while cutting corners might work, for now, it creates a false sense of security and bigger mess to clean up later. Hurried work makes for double the effort, in the end. Of course that doesn't make hurrying always bad! Sometimes one must rush out a quick solution to avoid long term consequences, then implement the real fix later. (Implementing the real fix later is something that a Freedom To-er won't understand - the work's already been done, why redo?)
