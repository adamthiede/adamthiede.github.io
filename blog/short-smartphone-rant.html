Short Smartphone Rant

[link—standalone]

Here's a fun thing for you to test: try playing 2 simultaneous audio streams on Android or iOS.

Having trouble? That (and other countless things) are surprisingly limited on mobile devices in comparison to a real PC.

The pinephone and many other devices (thanks to postmarketOS) can be full desktop computers. One can choose to run a full desktop environment like GNOME and XFCE, but most prefer to run Phosh, a mobile-optimized/stripped-down GNOME, or SXMO, a mobile-optimized gesture-based tiling window manager setup. The pinephone has an optional keyboard attachment - it's a bit chunky, but still pocketable. It's a real computer. I have full access! I can delete the kernel and wipe the modem if I want. I can uninstall any software I want and replace it with whatever I want. I can build apps for the device on the device. I can dock it to USB-C and get display output.

If we accept that smartphones are the norm, sealed-in batteries, no SD card slot, no headphone jack, no hardware killswitches, arbitrarily limited features, proprietary drivers, brutally short software lifecycles, and every other of the dozen+ ways to violate user freedom that smartphones have pioneered, we will continue to suffocate computing from the bottom up. Look at how hard it is to replace batteries on modern laptops. Didn't use to be that way. Same with RAM and SSDs - they're becoming built-in. This is a nightmare for upgradability and long-term repairability/usability. The best way to help the environment is to upgrade your stuff, not throw it away. Extend its life for years and years. That's why "reduce" and "reuse" come BEFORE "recycle".

This new wave of mini PCs with open hardware and software is setting things right. They are way better than smartphones, even if they're "worse". They're worse at being proprietary sealed-in trash-waiting-to-happen environment-destroyers that funnel you towards just consuming content on five different approved websites. They're probably a bit worse at being telephones and televisions, but those aren't what we want. They're better at being computers for computer hackers, which is what we want.

by Adam Thiede
