A Short Review of CalyxOS

[link—standalone]

Smartphones are pretty awful. By default they required accounts with creepy companies to do anything useful, and track the heck out of users (even if they are told not to). Using LineageOS without Google Apps is decent way to use a smartphone without the anti-features, but requires a supported device, and requires leaving the bootloader unlocked, which is seen as a vulnerability. Android One devices, once stripped of the usual garbage Google apps via adb, run a clean enough version of Android, and programs like NetGuard can be used to only allow trusted apps to have network access, but it prevents the use of other VPN apps, like WireGuard or OpenVPN.

Enter CalyxOS - an Android OS based on mainline AOSP, but with a number of privacy and usability tweaks thrown in. In Calyx, users can disable network access entirely for apps in the regular permissions manager. F-Droid, an "Open Source Apps Only" app store, is included. So is "Aurora Store", a way to get Google Play apps without using the Play Store or using a Google account - it even lists known trackers in a report in each app's description. CalyxOS' installation allows and suggests re-locking the bootloader. MicroG, an open-source re-implementation of Google's services, allows apps that require it to function, while remaining dormant for those who don't. It also gets timely security updates. The Pixel 2, no longer supported by Google's OS updates, is still supported by CalyxOS, so it also breathes life into perfecly fine phones that have fallen off the hilariously short support lifecycle.

The main downsides are limited device support and the command-line based installer, which limits adoption for "normals" (but was a plus for me). Also, a small subset of proprietary apps (that shouldn't be used in the first place, tsk tsk) don't work quite right without tweaking. The CalyxOS User Communities are a helpful resource - most of the app compatibility problems have already been solved and a search will dig up solutions in minutes. Plexus is a good resource for this as well.

Overall, CalyxOS provides a highly usable Android experience, promotes Open Source software first, doesn't reduce the regular Android security model, and is made with user interests first. There's no reason not to use it on supported devices, if you have one, and if you don't, the Pixel 3a can be purchased for less than $200 these days. Give it a go if you care about your privacy and the long-term usability of your smartphone.

by Adam Thiede
