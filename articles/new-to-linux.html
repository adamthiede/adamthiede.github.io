So You've Heard About Linux

You've seen the light - you want more control and freedom out of your computing experience. You've had it with no customization, forced updates, settings changing on their own, and paying for the privilege of being spied on. You've got adequate backups, a fresh USB stick with your distribution of choice, a printed-out copy of your favorite installation guide, and grit and determination! But there are a lot of choices out there, and you don't want to have to reinstall your system every week when you inevitably make mistakes. What's the best course of action for the new user who wants a stable, reliable system that will run effectively for the life of their computer?

The worst thing you can do is expect to not change most of your habits. It's like going vegan - you can't just have the same exact meals minus the animal bits. You have to find suitable replacements that fulfill your nutritional requirements. The way Linux systems work differs greatly from Windows and macOS in a couple key ways.

Is Linux for You?

Although it's free for everyone, Linux might not be right for you. You might not be ready to make a transition. This isn't gatekeeping, or me being an arrogant know-it-all - I'm honestly trying to save you some suffering here. Here's a few ways to know that Linux is not for you:

  • You use proprietary software that is not available on Linux, and are not willing to find an alternative or workaround.
  • You have specific hardware that Linux either doesn't work well on, or is a huge chore to get working. This includes most recent Apple computers, Microsoft Surface devices, Chromebooks, and laptops with switchable "Optimus" graphics.
  • Your computer is a work- or school-owned computer - seriously, don't do this on a computer you don't own.
  • The main thing you use your computer for is playing specific video games that aren't available on Linux, and you are not willing to play different games.
  • You want a "problem-free" experience.

Making major changes in your life doesn't come without struggle. The problems you have right now on Windows or macOS don't exist to you, because you've probably been working around them for so long you've forgotten that they're there. You'll have to deal with new issues, new layouts, and new programs, but rest assured you'll get used to them in time too. Change is a constant of life. You got used to your new shoes, new car, new phone, and new home. You will be fine.

Picking an Operating System

If you're used to macOS or Windows, you'll be surprised at how much you can customize, theme, and tweak the Linux desktop. Take a look at Xfce's screenshot gallery and FVWM's screenshot gallery. Xfce's had a long history, and you can see all the different ways people have modified and customized it to suit their needs. FVWM has an even longer history, and it's fun to see all the late 90s and early 2000s fonts and desktop setups. This is a fun example to look at, because it's a teaser of the infinite potential you are about to unlock. If this doesn't appeal to you, there's no problem sticking with something standard.

There are many different flavors of Linux, called "distributions". They all have different reasons for existing, but few should truly be considered. At the end of the day you end up with something that runs more-or-less the same no matter what you pick. You'll probably end up trying a couple out.

I can't recommend Ubuntu highly enough. It's solid, simple, gets great support, and is a fantastic starting place for the new Linux user. It isn't any lighter on system resources than Windows is, though, so pick one of the official flavors that offers a lighter-weight interface, like Xubuntu or Ubuntu Mate. Both still look great, and offer an experience tailored for older or less powerful hardware.

If you're a more technically inclined individual, and you think computers are fun to play with, I'd recommend Debian. It's the oldest still-active Linux system, the gold standard for quality and software availability, and the system that Ubuntu's based on. Debian is faster and lighter-weight than Ubuntu, is highly stable and secure, and still produces releases for old 32-bit PCs (10 years old or older).

The easiest way to get Linux is to buy a pre-installed computer from system76 or another vendor like them. If you don't have that kind of money, a spare old computer is fine.

Installing Software

When you set up your computer, you need programs on it. You don't get them the same you would on Windows and macOS, which is typically going to the vendor's website, downloading an installer, and running it to deploy the program to your system. On most Linux distributions, the programs you install will come from a centralized repository maintained by your operating system's creator, much like the "app store" model. There's nothing stopping you from installing things from outside that repository, but you should not do that. Programs from the repositories or "repos" get updated with the rest of the system, ensuring security and cohesive operation. They're all built to work together, so every program you install from the repositories should work great with the rest of the system, and automatically install anything it needs to function. No more grabbing old dot-net packages off Microsoft's website, or finding the X11 installer from your macOS CD.

The Debian project has a great document on how not to break your operating system. Linux systems will give you a lot of power, but also a lot of chances to break your system. The training wheels are off - be careful.

Most Linux users should be comfortable installing, updating, and removing their system and software from the command line. This tutorial does a pretty good job of explaining it. You should familiarize yourself with some basic commands, because they will come in handy.

Sometimes your repositories will not have a program you want to use. This is usually because the operating system vendors can't package it due to licensing issues (patent-encumbered software required to play specific media files), design philosophy (the distribution vendor won't package non-free software), or just lack of Linux support by the program's creator. In this case, you can either build the software from source yourself, find someone else who has done it and provides binaries, or find an acceptable alternative. Examples would be using Gedit instead of Notepad++ or GIMP instead of Photoshop.

Yes. I know. This is not what people want to hear. But you've made it this far. If you're going to sit down at your fresh, free Linux system, and junk it up with a bunch of proprietary spyware, what did switching even get you? Why would you install Steam and Spotify and Zoom, when you spent all that effort to gain your Freedom? Going from Windows to Linux and using the same proprietary software as before is like going from 100% spyware to 90% spyware. It basically makes no difference. If you're bothering to do this, spend the effort to find the Free alternative. No, it will likely not be as "good" of a product (GIMP vs. Photoshop), but when the other one spies on you, and requires you to use an operating system that spies on you, isn't it kind of a non-starter?

Ongoing Maintenance

Your computer, like your car, needs ongoing maintenance. Fortunately on Linux this is mostly just periodic updates, backups, and keeping an eye on filesystem usage. Your system will come with most of these tools pre-installed. Many systems have options to configure automatic updates, but these won't reboot you in the middle of your work. Frequently a reboot is required to complete an update, but the reboots don't take any longer than a regular reboot. You're in control.

Tools you may be used to on the other platforms, like antivirus, registry cleaners, program uninstallers, disk defragmenters - they all have no place here. Linux takes care of that stuff for you. Unless you go about actively destroying your computer, chances are it'll work better for longer than Windows. macOS is already pretty good in this department too, but you at least won't see regressions.

Command Line Comfort

Linux evangelists fall into two camps: the "don't make the beginners think about the command line because it's scary!" camp, and the "beginners should gain familiarity with the command line before even seeing a graphical interface!" camp. Both are dumb and wrong - as usual the truth lies somewhere in the middle. The command line is just another tool, like the graphical file manager, web browser, and mouse/keyboard. I bet it took you a long time to memorize all the keys on the keyboard, right? It's probably really hard to find what you're looking for in Microsoft Excel's constantly shifting menu system. The command line is just another one of these things you can use to make your computer do what you want. It's not inherently difficult, but it doesn't pull any punches and there are no "undo" buttons.

The majority of Linux systems are servers. It's a fantastic server operating system, and is probably running as the backbone for most of the websites you're looking at, or backend services your apps use. The way system administrators interact with these systems is the command line. It requires a basic amount of computer literacy, but chances are, you already know enough to get started.

The command line may seem daunting at first, but think of it like Siri or Alexa or Google Assistant. You have to know the right phrases, in the right way, and you can make the computer do anything by invoking them correctly. If you say them in a different language or don't appropriately enunciate, the computer won't understand. You can create shortcuts and aliases that string multiple commands together, or set a schedule on which things are automatically run.

This is the way Unix and Linux computers were designed to be used - a text prompt, where you invoke programs that output text, and that output can be used as the input to another program. Concatenating a bunch of programs, automating activities, parsing output, and batching tasks is referred to as scripting, and it's an incredibly efficient way to use your computer. The command line is hard to learn, but the possibilities are endless. While it may not be the easiest way to use your computer, when you get to a certain skill level, it is most certainly the fastest.

The command line experience on Ubuntu is entirely optional. You can install the system, install apps, keep it safe and secure, and do regular maintenance without ever opening "Terminal". But you'll be worse off for it. I encourage you to give it a shot especially if you have an older or lower-spec machine. Command-line-interface apps ("CLI" or "TUI") are much easier for computers to execute and will save you RAM and battery life. You can have news readers, audio players, chat apps, spreadsheet programs, and even web browsers in your terminal.
