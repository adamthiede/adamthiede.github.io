Today, Facebook, an advertising/malware distribution/surveillance company from California announced it would no longer allow white nationalists (essentially Neo-Nazis as we know them in the States) on their social media platform.

<a href="https://newsroom.fb.com/news/2019/03/standing-against-hate/">Link here</a> to their post.

Clearly this specific, isolated move is good. We can all agree on that. Because of their absolute control over their communication platform, they have the power to do this. 

I hate to be the bearer of bad news - the doom prophet - but sooner or later, something slightly controversial that *you* believe in will also be banned from a platform you use. Be it firearm rights, abortion, religion, conspiracy theories, or encryption/data privacy rights, the ability to express one's voice publicly without being beholden to a Silicon Valley Overloard, is going away. The machine learning algorithms are going to mistake a joke in private message (or group message) between friends as inciting violence and your account will get deleted. Even worse, the police could show up at your door and arrest you for it.

Bruce Schneier wrote about <a href="https://hbr.org/2013/06/you-have-no-control-over-s">digital feudalism</a> back in 2013. It only seems more relevant now! People are trusing cloud-connected computers to protect their privacy and security, but it all comes down to trusting the companies who have absolute control over them. In the six intervening years since this article's publishing, there's been no reigning in of the power of these monsters. Apple, Google, Amazon, Facebook, and Microsoft have only grown more monstrous and less responsible - less trustworthy.

Taking power away from these companies and putting it in the hands of the users is the most important thing we, as digital citizens, can do. Federation and decentralization is how the Internet was born, and it's the natural way of things. Think of how many social networks and chat/IM systems have come and gone - email, venerable, simple, stoic email, has outlived them all, and shows no signs of stopping. Its nature as a decentralized system makes it more useful and powerful than any other, and its interoperability makes it a first step for everyone on the web.

We need more systems that respect the users and their freedoms, and less that try to take them away. When will people learn that it's dangerous to continue trusting these companies? The only reason they can make moves like this is because of their absolute control, and so many people don't understand that there are other, better choices out there.
