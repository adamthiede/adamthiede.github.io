In Praise of ath9k

[link—standalone]

Do you have a favorite device driver? Or maybe a favorite pice of hardware? I do! My favorite hardware is the set of devices supported by Linux's ath9k (or OpenBSD's athn driver).

The devices in question are all Qualcomm Atheros wireless cards. They were in vogue from about 2011-2014 and shipped in a lot of consumer hardware. Most higher end notebooks shipped with Intel Wi-Fi chips, and the lower end ones shipped with Realtek or Broadcom cards. Atheros has less market share than the larger players in the "laptop Wi-Fi chip" space. This makes it somewhat complicated to find these cards in a reliable way, especially given that they mostly shipped in the mini-PCIe form factor, not M.2/NGFF. Most laptops these days also expect the Wi-Fi and Bluetooth chipsets to be on the same card in a combo pack, and those are also difficult to obtain reliably, since older systems with these cards frequently had a separate bluetooth module. Not only that, if you do find one with Bluetooth, it's likely it's older, and won't have modern Bluetooth features. Most of these cards also only have 2.4GHz bands for Wi-Fi and won't see 5GHz networks.

Despite the drawbacks, I go out of my way to find these cards. I've ordered a few on the Internet in various form factors and have them in every laptop I care about. Every machine I repair for a friend gets checked, and if it's got the Holy Grail of Wi-Fi, I ask to trade them for an Intel or other one of my spare cards, and since they run Windows it's usually an upgrade for them. In my extensive laptop experience, there is no Wi-Fi card that has worked better than the ath9k cards in terms of reliability, stability, connection speed, and versatility. Sometimes Intel cards dump their firmware all over the floor and leave a mess in dmesg and a reboot is necessary to clean up. Newer Qualcomm Atheros cards (ath10k) or Realtek cards either take a long time to connect or are single-band cards that can only handle one connection at a time, and in Realtek's case, that's if there's a driver at all! Sometimes their cards are such obscure one-offs that they don't land in the Linux kernel at all.

In honor of this driver and these wonderful cards, these blob-free bastions of Freedom, I made a website dedicated to these wireless cards and their driver. Enjoy. Go get yourself an Atheros card and bless your laptop with it to find out what you've been missing.

by Adam Thiede
