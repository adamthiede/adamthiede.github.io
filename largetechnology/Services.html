Internet Services are why we use computers.

Avoiding Big Tech in your network services portfolio means liberating yourself from dependence on proprietary systems. The solution is decentralization - decentralized systems can't be deplatformed because they exist everywhere and nowhere. The technology is complicated, but you know you've found a solution when its backbone is hosted by thousands of volunteers worldwide, instead of one big company's data center.

If you cannot easily read the privacy policy for a Network Service, or if all of its components are not open source, do not trust it. Some services like Telegram claim to be open source and protect your privacy, but neither of those is true. Do your research!

Email

The elephant in the room is email. Most people use gmail, hotmail, or yahoo. A good email provider will not look at your email, won't delete your account, and doesn't use your account for 500 different things. A not-progressive-enough YouTube comment shouldn't mean you lose access to your bills.
For email, choose Fastmail or mailbox.org. I refuse to recomment ProtonMail because it makes you dependent on their special app - you can't use ProtonMail with any old email app you like, so it's not actually email, and doesn't conform to the email standards. If you cannot come up with a good reason to use ProtonMail over Fastmail or Mailbox.org, do not use it.
You can host your own email easily with Mail-In-A-Box, but this is only for highly technical people.

Really, though, no one should be communicating over email with any expectation of privacy. It cannot be encrypted well enough to matter, and most of the people you'll communicate with will likely be using Big Tech mail anyway. Far better communcation methods are outlined below.

Social Media

Most people should not use social media. It's cancer for your brain. But there are valid use cases for small family or neighborhood bubbles, so I'll recommend a few:
Pleroma and Mastodon are both ActivityPub networks. They work well and can communicate with each other, and PeerTube (mentioned below). Imagine if you could leave a comment on a Facebook post and a YouTube with your Twitter account. Just one account for all three services. Not mindblowing enough? Throw Pixelfed, an Instagram clone, into the mix too. They all interoperate. Many people run Mastodon servers, so you can find one to your liking, or run your own. It's like email - there are many choices, but only one protocal, so everyone can communicate regardless of where they are.

Other Communication, Entertainment, and Business Needs

Replace Google Hangouts, Skype, iMessage, Facetime, Whatsapp, and others with Signal, the Matrix protocol, or Jami. These are all a little different, but none require technical expertise to use and understand. No more than 10 minutes of reading, maybe. Signal isn't a great option, since it is centralized, but it's easy to use for even the least technical on your contacts list, so that's an easy win.

For entertainment, be discerning with your dollar. Avoid subscribing to music and video services, since they don't pay creators anything and don't let you own your media. Use things like Bandcamp, or just buy DVDs in a store. Streaming services in general are a bad deal, and only give Big Tech more data about what your habits are. Buy the things you like. Own them, and support the creators.
If streaming is the only option, replace YouTube with PeerTube, which is software that lets you make your own YouTube.

Many people use Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Slack for businesses. Instead, use Mattermost and Rocket Chat for text chat, Jitsi for video calls, and GitLab or Gitea for team collaboration. Big Tech hardly has a monopoly on business technology. Hosting an open source platform yourself ensures there's no spyware and no fear of capricious account deletions for wrongthing, like you have with Big Tech.
