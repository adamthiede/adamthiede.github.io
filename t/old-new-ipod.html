Finally after 13 years, it's 'brand new' again. I added three things to my old, busted 5th Generation iPod ("Video"). -The slow, failing hard drive has been replaced with an SD card in an iFlash adapter. I used this microSD card - it comes with a full SD adapter and I just left that in. -The battery, which was completely dead, has been replaced. -The headphone jack, which only worked out of the left ear, has been replaced with a brand new one. iFixit has a series of really detailed guides focused on old iPods. This is a black iPod, but interestingly enough, the black iPod "Video" models had white trim - the headphone jack and dock connector plastic were white originally, just like the white model. The only differences were the part number, front faceplate, and click wheel. I would've liked to get a black headphone jack, but those did not come with a hold switch, and were out of stock at the time. After the repairs, I connected it to iTunes (on a Windows PC - Windows-format iPods can be read and written to by both PCs and Macs, but Macintosh-format iPods can only be used with Macs) and restored the firmware. There are only a few noticable differences since this repair. Every so often the volume control becomese non-responsive, but I think that was happening beforehand. The iPod is much lighter than before. I went on a jog with it, and - pleasant surprise - there is no more skipping and pausing like it did before. Despite being mostly recommendable and safe some dangers in this process include breaking the cables attached to the battery and headphone jack, cracking the side plastic clips that hold the iPod together, and denting the rear metal case. This is one of the easier iPods to open up, but chances are if you've got one as old as mine that has been in use for the 13 years, the plastic will be old and brittle, and you'll run the risk of the iPod not coming back together correctly. Read through the iFixit guides completely before even attempting to pry your iPod open, so you know what you're in for. Mine's held together with electrical tape right now, because some of the case did crack apart, and I'm exploring better solutions. Maybe I'll have to re-form the metal case and push it in to be a bit tighter. But for right now, I'll enjoy the repairs I've made. I've unearthed an older pair of the terrible iPod headphones and am listening to my ancient collection of middle-school jams. Sounds like nostalgia!
