How do you know what you're sending is secure? I got asked recently about iMessage, and if it's 'secure'. I can't give a real answer to that. Apple says it's secure and most people tend to believe them. <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2014/02/27/apple-explains-exactly-how-secure-imessage-really-is/">Here</a> is a 2013 article from TechCrunch on it.

Apple wants you to trust them. They <a href="https://www.apple.com/privacy/approach-to-privacy/">explain their approach to privacy</a> and <a href="https://www.apple.com/privacy/manage-your-privacy/">give you tools to manage it</a>, but again, that requires you to trust what they say. Those switches could do anything. If you trust them, and belive that their servers aren't decrypting your messages and storing them in plaintext, then good for you, you can treat iMessage as a secure messaging platform... if you turn off location services, and if you turn off iMessage backup, and if you turn off iCloud, and if you don't worry about someone tracing your phone number to you, and... the list goes on.

They say that they don't <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man-in-the-middle_attack">man-in-the-middle</a> all your messages. They say that they don't store information that you don't want them to. "You have control over how your photos...and other data are shared between your devices and iCloud. Individual iCloud services can be turned on or off."

But beliveing all this requires TRUST.

How do you trust opaque services? How do you make sure they're acting in your best interest?

You can't.

In order to trust a service, you have to be able to see their back-end policies, configurations, settings, access controls, and most importantly, the code that runs it. There is absolutely no way to trust the privacy of a system that isn't open-source. It can be independently audited for security, and for a system like Apple's, I imagine it's quite secure - it's a very big target.

No matter what, in the end, you can't consider their systems private because they don't show you how they work. It's a closed system, and for all the toggles and preferences they let you have, you really have no control over anything you give to them, and no way to find out if they're abusing it.

What can you do? Is there a private system out there? Sure. You can use PGP encrpted email (or for that matter, PGP-encrypt ANY text you send over any method), you can use Signal, or you can write letters with personalized ciphers. You can choose to not communicate over networks you don't trust (i.e. any network outside your home). Another good option is using XMPP/Jabber - it's a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_(information_technology)">federated</a> system, like email, so you can use any provider you like, and communicate with anyone else on any other provider, and you can roll your own. There are also many encryption methods that can be applied to XMPP, and it's not tied to a phone number at all.

You must be able to personally audit systems that you trust with private information. If your providers don't allow you to look at what they're running, it's probably because they don't want you to find out.
