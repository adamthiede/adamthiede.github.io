Apple and Privacy

 or, Actions Speak Louder Than Words

I've had it with Apple's privacy bait. This is a systematic takedown of how awful they are as a company and how they're lying about something I'm deeply knowledgeable about.

Back in 2013 I bought an Apple MacBook Air. It was the biggest improvement in computing I'd ever seen, and I really enjoyed using it. I use a MacBook Pro and iPhone for (and provided by) work. My position here does not come from unfounded anger-bait or ignorance, but from disappointment and misplaced trust.

Privacy is a value that is focused on the individual. It is a human-centric goal. Focusing on privacy puts the needs of the individual over profit. Apple is talking big about privacy, but they can't back it up. At every turn, they put profit over people, and marketing will follow whatever trend will put them ahead.

While no one can actually prove unfounded fears about what Apple does with data behind the scenes, using their visible activities as indicators of their overall character allows us to safely assume the worst - they don't care about your privacy, they just want your money, they intentionally break old products to get you to buy more, and they spy on everything you do. Factual accouonting of their behavior is laid out below as evidence.

Hardware

Apple as a company used to make great computers - high quality, versatile, durable, with exceptionally long lifespans. I have a 2013 MacBook Pro in my closet that (aside from the shattered screen) looks and performs impressively well for its age, especially when lined up next to 2013-era Thinkpads.

In 2016, they removed a standard port from their phones in the name of "courage" and then 30 minutes later in the same presentation sold you a $170 solution to a problem they created. [1] From 2016 until 2020, they released a series of fundamentally broken and unrepairable computers with horrible keyboards [2] - the 2019 model had a recall program in place for its keyboards before it was even on store shelves. [3]

If you toss a normal laptop down the stairs and it's broken, you unscrew the bottom and pull out the hard drive or SSD to recover your data on to a new computer. Apple removed the ability to do this, but left a (real name) "lifeboat" connector[4] on the motherboard to ensure that in the event of a broken computer, data could be recovered by technicians. They removed that after one year[5] and now their storage is just soldered on the board. If I toss my MacBook Pro and Thinkpad down the stairs and they both are destroyed, I can get recover the data from one of them, but not the other.

Using a replacement battery in an iPhone will reduce its performance if the battery is not blessed by special Apple technicians.[6] If you take two new iPhones and swap their batteries (or screens or touch ID sensors), they will complain that the battery is not "official", and in the case of Touch ID, refuse to function.[7] Apple goes to great lengths to make sure you only get your devices serviced by them, even if independent repair shops can do it better and cheaper, and even do things Apple won't do.

Features nobody asked for (Touch Bar) are lauded as revolutionary while features people loved and relied upon (USB ports, SD card readers, MagSafe) are cast aside. These computers also aren't capable of dealing with the heat they generate and either overheat or sound like a jet engine or both.[8] They intentionally obscure specifications on their website and make it difficult to compare their components to equivalent PC laptops. They rebadge chips with different names, even though they have the same specifications and feature set, to make it intentionally confusing[9].

Creating and using proprietary ports like Lightning ensures that Apple controls the market for chargers, docks, and other accessories for their iPhones and iPads. No one can make Lightning (or before that, 30-pin dock connector) devices without Apple's blessing, and a tax. Continuing to push these proprietary connectors makes the experience worse for everyone, all the while making Apple a quick buck through the MFI program.

Software

That's just the hardware - the software is an even sadder story. For the longest time they offered network services but sucked at it, and had to do all the processing of data on your iPhone, so it got hot and slow when you had it charging and it was processing your photos or emails. They pivoted their incompetence to a feature - "privacy". On-device processing became a feature because they couldn't figure out how to do it in the cloud[10].

Apple's devices don't work well without the Internet. Applications in macOS will not launch unless your computer can contact an Apple Internet service to determine if the program is allowed. This information is transmitted in clear text so anyone on your network or on any of the computers in the network between you and Apple's server can see who you are and what app you're launching.[11] Apple's built-in apps on the mac also bypassed the VPN in their latest release of macOS.[12] This has subsequently been fixed, after public outcry. This is a very big deal.

Initial setup of an iPhone uses dark patterns to trick you into using iCloud and other features that don't respect your privacy. All your photos taken are uploaded to their servers, and they give you a very very tiny 5GB of storage, then nag you about paying them money for more when you inevitably run out.[13]

If you use Siri, contractors (not Apple employees even) listen to the recordings. You can and probably have triggered Siri by accident.[14]

There are ads all over their operating systems with no clear way to disable them - you can't play music on your phone without getting an ad for their monthly subscription service every so often.[15] Sending messages with Siri puts an add on your message on the recipient's device.[16]

They tout privacy but don't give you a way to turn off features that compromise your privacy, or turn them back on after a time. There is no way to enable an application-level firewall or persistent system-wide VPN in the style of NetGuard on Android.

Apple controls software distribution on their platforms. It is impossible to install another App Store on your iPhone, and impossible to install another app without jumping through a bunch of hoops that either allow third parties full access to your device, or require you to have a Mac and reinstall the app from the Mac every 6 days.[17]

Operating systems that Apple controls cannot be manually patched. You cannot apply fixes yourself - you have to wait until their fix is available. You cannot turn off affected services on their operating systems to mitigate the flaws.

Consumerism and the Environment

The blame for consumerism doesn't lay entirely in Apple's hands. People that continue to get sucked in to buy the latest barely-measurably-different products are also to blame. They're not thinking clearly because they've been tricked by advertising. Apple's advertising is consistently full of lies or half-truths.

Apple is not interested in you. They are interested in selling expensive devices, and a lot of them, with high margins. They need cheap labor. Their devices are built in China, a country infamous for human rights abuse. The conditions in Apple's factories are so poor that they installed nets around the building to catch the not-insignificant number of people who try to kill themselves by jumping out of the windows.[18]

The rare earth metals that are in these devices are often mined by underpaid workers in abusive working conditions. The process of obtaining these minerals only increases in difficulty, and we are destroying the planet to get more.[19] Apple devices are discarded because of the forced upgrade cycle, and the demand for these minerals increases. Non-replaceable, dangerous[20] lithium batteries go into trash cans. Gold and copper used in the circuit boards goes into trash cans. Factories churn out more devices every year because Apple has created demand for yearly cell phone upgrades. This was not the case 15 years ago - people kept their devices until they were unfit for duty, which was often anywhere from three to five years. Replacement batteries were easy to obtain.

Apple products are more expensive and less functional than many competing alternatives, but they drive the market. Sometimes it's in good and interesting ways, like ditching floppy drives, adopting USB ports, and pushing the envelope on Thunderbolt, which makes non-upgradable laptops a little more upgradable. Many times it's for the worse though. Wireless connectivity in everything isn't a great idea. Wireless charging is a waste of energy. Bluetooth headphones with sealed-in batteries are not only dangerous but wasteful.[21] Anything with a sealed-in battery has a limited lifespan and poor repairability. We should strive for longevity in our technology, not lock-in and disposability, especially when disposing of these items is bad for our planet.

Privacy

For software to respect your privacy, you must be able to trust the software. If the software is proprietary and closed-source, you have to trust the company who makes it, the distribution channels, the governance of said company, all the individual people at that company, and the government of the country where the company resides. This chain of trust isn't something you can achieve with Apple and the United States.

Apple can't be trusted not to spy on you. They claim privacy but don't release the source code for their systems. They regularly cooperate with governments and allow backdoor access to user data.[22] They don't write new software back doors for governments to get into iPhones[23], and that's the very public information you likely know. That's not the problem though. Anything that gets backed up to iCloud is fair game[24] - which is all of almost every iPhone's contents, since they convince users into doing it during setup, and don't allow app downloads without it. And that's really all that needs to be said on Privacy. Their devices may protect your privacy in some cases, but cannot be trusted to maintain that protection and you won't know if or when you are betrayed.

Money

Apple has, multiple times[25][26], harmed software developers because Apple was being greedy. (Those are but two cases in a vast sea of evidence. Peruse the web at your leisure.) In many cases it involves smaller companies whose services are available for purchase outside the app. Apple's policy prevents apps from notifying users that a pay-for service is available in any other way than in-app purchases. Yes, that is as ridiculous as it sounds. In the case of Wordpress, an open-source piece of software with a services component available from a separate division of the company, Apple forced the developer to add a feature to the app so they could take a cut.

That's just the beginning of that rabbit hole. Their rules are overreaching to the point of ridiculousness. The only purpose to these rules appears to be greed. More evidence to this greed is the fact that Amazon and Netflix, among others, are exempt from the in-app purchases rule, likely because of some boardroom deals they've cut out of the public eye. They're currently (2020-2021) engaged in an antitrust suit with Epic Games about it too.

No, being a greedy money-grubber doesn't have much to do with privacy. But when the world's richest company will take every opportunity to put the screws to the little guy for cut of his change, it becomes clear what their motivations are and where their priorities lie.

Conclusions

Apple made good computers. Then they got greedy. Then Privacy became popular and they decided to pivot their weakness into a strength. This really grinds my gears because I care about privacy. It takes effort and lifestyle changes, and is not just buying a new $1000 iPhone.

I'd be content to let them be the richest company in the world, profiting off of increased control, surveillance, and the console-fication of general purpose computers. But, "privacy: that's iPhone"[27]? Now they've made me mad. They come into my house, spitting these lies, and expect me to stand by and let them be? Get off my lawn!
