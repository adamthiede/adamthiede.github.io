Options For Getting Rid Of Your Smartphone

The desire to remove smartphones and other modern technology from one's life is becoming quite popular these days. Many have remarked on the peace this brings them, and on how it improves their lives. I approve, and can offer you help on how to do so. Now, more than ever, in the age of abundant technology, you'd think there would be some easy options available. Well, that's not entirely true. If you would like to live a more focused and personal life, and be less distracted by the spy-brick in your pocket, I will tell you about some ways to do that, and how some of your options that seem good are actually no better off than your distractinng and invasive smartphone.

Your Goals

In doing this, your goal should not be to alienate and cut off personal connections from your life. I don't want to help you do that. Getting rid of your smartphone should enrich your personal connections and decrease distractions.

Some of your options will require self control. Others will require money. Know that this process doesn't come free of compromise. You should have a goal in mind, whether that's peaceful living, removing something that causes you to sin, getting off of social media, or even just the abstract "digital minimalism". Remember that goal, and stay dedicated. It helps to have a partner, so see if your spouse, parent, sibling, cousin, or friend will do it with you.

You Can't Just...

Before we get into it - I have to do my duty as a technologically savvy individual and tell you what you can't do, so you don't try something, get frustrated, and give up.

  • You can't put your SIM card into your Nokia Brick from 2004. The network technology is different now and it will not work at all. Same goes for even your older smartphones - it'll be far more frustrating and less rewarding than you'd expect.
  • You shouldn't buy a flip phone from the store and expect it to be what you're looking for. These newfangled flip phones have high-rez cameras, big screens, YouTube, WhatsApp, and Google Assistant apps. They're basically smartphones with a dial pad. Worse still, you can't control them the same way you can with smartphones, so you won't be able to remove the distracting stuff you don't want.
  • You shouldn't mess with your work phone and its setup. This should go without saying. What you should do is try and keep separate work and personal phones, only use the work one for work, and turn it off when you're not required to use it.
  • You shouldn't do this without warning people! If you frequently use things like WhatsApp, Signal, Telegram, or other smartphone-exclusive communication platforms, don't go dark on your friends and family. Tell them what you're doing and why. (Some of the options below allow you to keep these around.)

Finally, I have to mention HD Voice, VoLTE (Voice Over LTE), and Wi-Fi Calling. Modern devices make phone calls sound crisp and clear, thanks to routing the call over the data network, instead of the "legacy" voice network. In addition, if you have poor cell service in your area, you can even use your Wi-Fi at home to call and text. Consider this as part of your research, and purchase devices that support these features if you need them.

Options

With that out of the way, let's look at what you can do!

Option One: The Purge ($0)

Take your current smartphone and delete everything from it. This can come in the form of a factory reset, or just deleting all the apps you possibley can. Log out of everything, including email. Don't download any apps, unless they're basic communication programs, like WhatsApp or Signal. Have a trusted individual set restrictions up on the device, disabling all functionality but the phone, messaging, and maybe maps. Bring a digital camera, MP3 player, paper notebook, or other tools with you if you want to use them. Your smartphone is now just a phone. On most smartphones, you can also use accessibility options to turn the screen grayscale. Do that too. Use notification settings to ensure the only thing that makes noise at all is the phone, and messages.

iPhone Parental Controls and Restrictions

Android doesn't have the same kind of granular control. Your best bet is to disable apps and have some self-control. Chances are your family members have a somewhat recent iPhone laying around that you can restrict and use.

Option Two: The Minimalist ($100+)

There are a few devices on the market for sale that are geared toward modern non-smartphones. A few decent options are:

  • The light phone is a small phone with a black-and-white screen that is designed to call and text, with some other simple tools. It's spendy, but a very thoughful device. Its display is e-ink (think Kindle or Nook e-readers) so it's also very easy on the eyes. The device is also pleasantly small, compared to today's massive smartphones. It can share its data connection over wifi.
  • Techless sells a sheep in wolf's clothing called the Wisephone. It is a smartphone that they've put a custom interface on to make it do less. It also has some good family-oriented services. Made by Catholics.
  • Gabb Wireless is a full-stack phone and cell service provider. I do not like the restriction to a single carrier and the monthly fee, but this is another option. Unaffiliated with the weird social network (this has an extra "b"). Made by LDS folks. Seems a little too smartphone-y to me, so if you have a problem, this won't solve it.
  • Punkt MP02 is another expensive product, but is very much a simple feature phone. Has a kinda-sometimes-working stripped down Signal app (do research first!). Can share its data connection over wifi and USB.
  • Mudita Pure is like the light phone - simple device, e-ink display, few features, and rather expensive. The operating system is Open Source, so they score massive points there for long-term use and customizability. It has a user-replacable battery. It can share its data connection over USB.

This is a non-exhaustive list. Other devices have games, Internet Browsers, or modern features. Many "dumb" phones you can buy have lot more in common with smartphones than you would expect. Be careful and don't buy something without research.

Option Three: The Incognito Mode ($100+)

This is kind of an out-there option, because it requires not only a purchase of a new device, but also some technical know-how and self-control. It's what I prefer and use, because the technical know-how and money aren't hurdles for me.

What I'd recommend is to purchase a device on the supported list for one of several open source, privacy-respecting alternative operating systems for smartphones. (Or, check your drawers; you may already have an old device supported by LineageOS or PostmarketOS.) I have also written another article that goes into more detail on the subject of usable, privacy friendly smartphones that are free from the clutches of Apple and Google.

Easiest to hardest:

  • Devices running CalyxOS are running Android with Google tracking ripped out. It's a privacy-focused setup, but removing web browsers, cameras, and installing only required programs for texting/calling (like Signal) will get you a minimal, non-distracting device that still lets you communicate securely.
  • LineageOS is also Android, but supports many more devices. The install procedure is more complicated depending on device, but what you end up with is a completely minimal system that doesn't even have an app store by default! You can still use Signal and disable the web browser.
  • PostmarketOS is my favorite - it's Linux for your phone. Using PostmarketOS is a technical challenge for Linux enthusiasts, but once set up, it's a reliable and user-friendly but basic smartphone interface. The draw here is that you can't run phone apps at all on this system, so even if you're tempted, installing apps is impossible (unlike on Calyx or Lineage). It offers the ultimate in personal freedom, respect, and privacy, but also might make you spend more time on the phone because you'll be troubleshooting all the time.

If you think a device like this is the way to go, but don't have the technical know-how, I can set it up and mail it to you. Email me if you're interested, and we can talk options, from old used inexpensive devices, to fast high-quality brand-new ones.

Conclusions

I've written before about my desire for a better communication device. The above options, if done right, will get you a device that give you a bit of your focus and life back. They will mostly respect you and your time. The major problem with "dumb" phones is the lack of security and privacy - none of them ship with the ability to communicate securely. If that's one of your needs, smartphones are really the only way you're going to meet folks where they're at. Like we said before, the goal of this overhaul is not to alienate and cut off communication with anyone. It's to be more intentional and human about communication. This means the Light Phone may not be an option for you, but a stripped-down smartphone (Options 1 or 3) would work great.

For many of us, the distraction, distress, and division a smartphone brings into our lives is a much larger concern than the surveillance of the basic communication we'd be doing over the phone anyway. Don't let perfect be the enemy of good. Even small improvements are still improvements.

If this interests you at all, find a buddy, and commit to a less distracted lifestyle. Don't let yourself get discouraged if you lapse, though. We're all sinners just trying to do our best. You can do it!
