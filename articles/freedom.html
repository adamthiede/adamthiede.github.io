The Value of Freedom

What is Freedom Anyway?

Recently I've been grappling with defining true Freedom. It's talked about a lot these days in many circles and I think a distinction must be made between what is real, and what is fake, and why that matters.

When I talk about Freedom, I don't think people really understand what I mean. Defining terms^1 is an important precursor to any rational conversation, and since "freedom" is such an over-encumbered term, I'd like to make a clear definition of what it is and is not.

Many definitions of freedom are essentially "maximum available activities, minimum restrictions". This is what I call Freedom To. This does not provide true freedom. True freedom is Freedom From, which is freedom from restrictions and burderns imposed in less-visible ways.

Freedom To

Freedom To is essentially license. License is unrestricted total freedom. Do whatever you want, no regard for morals, the thoughts and feelings of others, religious beliefs, laws, tradition, forethought, or general good sense. Actions taken with license are not always bad, but usually inevitably license is used to hurt or take advantage of others. No restrictions, internal or external, give one the Freedom To do whatever one wants.

This total lack of rules and restrictions only works if everyone in its sphere of influence shares the same exact set of values and morals. Like Communism and Anarchy, it seems to work well on paper, and on very small scale. In reality, no one should have this license. We live in a world with other people, and obviously, one's freedom only extends up to where it infringes upon the rights of others.

Freedom To is a false freedom. It does not make one free. In the Catholic perspective, sin (bad deeds that go against the laws of God) will make one more susceptible to further sin. Sin is an affliction that warps one's ability to make correct and moral judgements. This is a rational line of thought too - a drug addiction or a bad habit is hard to break. Bad deeds are permissible with Freedom To, because there are no rules. Sin and addiction make one slaves to the sin itself, and the changes it effects on one's well-being (physical, mental, or spiritual). Sin digs a hole that is hard to climb out of, but easy and comforting to keep digging.

Examples of Freedom To are the aforementioned drug addictions, the hedonistic lifestyle of the "sexual liberation", allowing children to make choices they shouldn't (kids are dumb!), and encouraging instant gratification over learning and self-actualization. Things like using social media because it feels good, instead of sitting down and taking the purposeful time to compose a letter (or email), are included. Habitually fondling a smartphone instead of bringing a notebook and pen to write down one's thoughts, or a good book to read and ponder, are similar.

Freedom From

Freedom From can almost be overlooked, because initially, its definition does not seem to fit. Freedom From is intentionally chosen, self-imposed discipline, learning, and through these restrictions, fulfillment. In true Freedom, one is not free to do whatever one desires. (Desires are not inherently bad, but some desires are bad, and these need to be recognized, faced, and curbed with discipline and healthy habits.) Freedom From is the ability to filter the good from the bad and choose the good, with help from religion and philosophy. Freedom From has restrictions, but they are restrictions imposed to make further freedoms available!

The chains of sin and slavery of bad habits will bind one to a life of misery. Moralizing one's choices and living a life of discipline keep one free of regrets. A life of Freedom To will lead to short-term pleasure, but eventually results in a broken-down body, crippling addictions, and ultimately a lack of fulfillment. Freedom From frees one from all these things. Restrictions are not imposed arbitrarily, but for one's own good. Most adults can remember that a parent's discipline felt bad in the moment, but resulted in character building that had a butterfly effect of benefits for adult life. "Eat your vegetables," "don't stay up too late," and "wear a helmet when you ride your bicycle," are all good examples of things that may not be fun, or pleasurable, or ideal now, but build good habits that prepare a young man for a successful, happy, and fulfilling adult life. where he not only treats himself well, but holds others to this high standard as well.

Freedom and Your Choices

As the saying goes, "Freedom isn't Free." It's hard to achieve Freedom. Revolutionary wars are not fought because they're easy, but for a higher ideal - to achieve liberation from

When one followes a healthy diet and fitness regimen, life becomes easier. Sleep comes easy and waking up is painless; stairs are not a challenge; long walks and impromptu sports games aren't out of the question; and life's length is extended, under the right circumstances. Fitness and a diet are restrictions - one is not free to guzzle soft drinks and eat unlimited candy. But these restrictions enable much greater, higher qualities of Freedom.
