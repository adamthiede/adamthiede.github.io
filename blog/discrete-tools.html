Discrete Tools

[link—standalone]

I carry a Leatherman with me everywhere I go. It's nice to have a slightly-more-capable pocketknife on hand, in a pinch. In my garage, I use my "real" tools, since the Leatherman makes for a poor knife, lousy pliers, and a hardly serviceable screwdriver. But it does have them all.

In contrast to the above multi-tool, I also own a lot of small, cheap devices that I only really use for one thing each. While the wastefulness doesn't make me feel great, and the amount of space things take up can be a burden, I belive it makes for a healthier lifestyle. Allow me to explain.

When I'm reading a book on my e-reader device, a stray message, email, or update will not distract me, because that thing doesn't have that functionality. Listening to music or podcasts on my media player is the same - no potential distractions, because the device only does one thing. No matter how far smartphone cameras have come in the past few years, even a decade-old SLR will knock their socks off if the conditions are less than perfect - and you can't miss the perfect shot due to an incoming phone call. Turning off your alarm in the morning shouldn't also be an on-ramp right into the wailing and grinding of teeth that is social media. You deserve time to wake up.

A dedicated device forces you to be deliberate about your choices. While integration is nice, and I marveled at the utility of early smartphones as pocket computers, it just turns out they're bad at pictures, reading, gaming, playing music, watching videos, and email, but they can be bad at all of those things at once. They're built for distraction and task-thrashing and end up hurting more than they help. Cramming everything you do in a smartphone is pretty magical, but also a pretty dumb idea. I could pry open that case with the mini screwdriver on my multi-tool, but I'd end up hurting myself or the object. Instead, I reach for a little plastic pry tool that is built to do the job right. A competent contractor carriers around a fully loaded tool kit, full of individual tools, not just a tiny foldable all-in-one.

Dear Reader, I encourage you to examine what in your life is a Leatherman, and how that functionality can be broken out into task-specific objects. Think about how you can improve your life by finding single-task objects or units to replace a catch-all. Chances are, the nice set of individual tools you find will not only be higher quality, produce better results, and be more pleasant to use, but also keep you focused on the task at hand, and in the end, make you happier.

by Adam Thiede
