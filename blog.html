Short Smartphone Rant

[link—standalone]

Here's a fun thing for you to test: try playing 2 simultaneous audio streams on Android or iOS.

Having trouble? That (and other countless things) are surprisingly limited on mobile devices in comparison to a real PC.

The pinephone and many other devices (thanks to postmarketOS) can be full desktop computers. One can choose to run a full desktop environment like GNOME and XFCE, but most prefer to run Phosh, a mobile-optimized/stripped-down GNOME, or SXMO, a mobile-optimized gesture-based tiling window manager setup. The pinephone has an optional keyboard attachment - it's a bit chunky, but still pocketable. It's a real computer. I have full access! I can delete the kernel and wipe the modem if I want. I can uninstall any software I want and replace it with whatever I want. I can build apps for the device on the device. I can dock it to USB-C and get display output.

If we accept that smartphones are the norm, sealed-in batteries, no SD card slot, no headphone jack, no hardware killswitches, arbitrarily limited features, proprietary drivers, brutally short software lifecycles, and every other of the dozen+ ways to violate user freedom that smartphones have pioneered, we will continue to suffocate computing from the bottom up. Look at how hard it is to replace batteries on modern laptops. Didn't use to be that way. Same with RAM and SSDs - they're becoming built-in. This is a nightmare for upgradability and long-term repairability/usability. The best way to help the environment is to upgrade your stuff, not throw it away. Extend its life for years and years. That's why "reduce" and "reuse" come BEFORE "recycle".

This new wave of mini PCs with open hardware and software is setting things right. They are way better than smartphones, even if they're "worse". They're worse at being proprietary sealed-in trash-waiting-to-happen environment-destroyers that funnel you towards just consuming content on five different approved websites. They're probably a bit worse at being telephones and televisions, but those aren't what we want. They're better at being computers for computer hackers, which is what we want.

Simple Computing

[link—standalone]

Two articles, one about analogies and the other on everlasting desktops, both got me thinking. This is why I follow blogs, and why I write one (despite the low readership) - it helps me think about things I care about.

Computer literacy among the general population is massively low. My mom used Windows XP to great success, and my dad installed a graphics card+drivers into that same computer after hooking up home broadband and setting up a Wi-Fi router. Now, neither knows the difference between a web browser and a search engine, or an email program and email service provider. It's app this and iPad that - their general computer literacy is completely gone.

It's not just my parents' gradual slide into ignorance - the new generations aren't literate either. I grew up around slow, obtuse, and failure-prone computers. They didn't do everything. They required lots of manual intervention. The Internet wasn't ubiquitous. My cohorts and I had to learn how to use them. There were no hints, no hand-holding. Remember the search function in Windows XP with the little dog? It took ages to run, locked up the whole machine, and didn't even work! Older systems I used didn't even have that.

I've identified two main problems causing this lack of literacy: shifting sands and complicated, black-box computers.

The constantly shifting landscape of modern software and hardware doesn't do anyone favors. Software changes so often that it's hard for users to learn how to use anything before its auto-updates have shape-shifted it into something unrecognizable. Users pick platforms, because that's what they're tricked into, and the rug is ripped out from under them before you can say "pick an open protocol instead of a centralized platform". This churn seems to have many people throwing in the towel, using things in anger that they'd rather not be touching, and feeling stuck with software and services that bubblewrap functionality and baby users. iPads are peak fisher-price computer. No filesystems, no shell access, no installing software, heck, only recently could one even pick a different default web browser! These things masquerade as real computers but abstract away (to the harm of the user) critical functionality for all but the most basic of users. Not only that, all the built-in software changes all the time, but since the user has no control, and you can't go back to the old software, users are forced to re-learn the same basic concepts year after year.

Newer computers might as well be black boxes. Although the lower-end desperate-fool-tier PCs you find at best buy still may have some visible screws, most PCs are getting less repairable than ever. CPUs have gone from socketed and upgradable to soldered on the board. Power sockets, which can get loose over time, used to be easily replaceable 5-dollar parts, but are now USB-C and soldered on the motherboard. Wi-Fi chips are no longer replaceable. And, forget complaining about soldered-on memory; that's so 2012. Apple computers even solder on the main storage now! Consumer-grade computers used to come with manuals, and now you'll be lucky if you can open the computer without dusting off your Security Torx or proprietary pentalobe driver. Of course enthusiasts still can and do build their own rigs, and many enterprise machines (like HP Z-series desktops, costing many thousands of dollars and usually sold per-pallet to engineering firms) have instructions printed on the inside panel or come with Thinkpad-tier assembly manual. But the average user's got a Personal Computer that can't be Personalized. If you want different than what came out of the box on day one - too bad!

Both of these factors seem to have led users to distrust and dislike computers, treating them like simple tools instead of a personal treasure trove of infinite possibility. After all, if every machine you've owned for the last ten years doesn't even really belong to you, then at some point you stop caring about it, and just carry on. It's sad to see so many people despise smartphones and iPads and modern Windows PCs and Macs yet continuing to carry them around.

The better way is a simpler computer. When users have less changing, less to manage, and less surprises, it's easier to understand and get comfortable with the computer. A simple Linux or BSD system with a classic graphical environment like Xfce and some basic programs will actually empower the users instead of babying them. Simple programs that don't change much, with easy configuration and good documentation, will make all the difference. Only when the computer is a helpful tool does it actually become interesting again.

The advertising garbage in Windows and Android and bubblewrapped baby-world of Apple are what frustrate users, not "computers" in general. The constant change and blackbox nature of these modern devices don't make it easy - they make it hard. Abstraction layering doesn't make a hard task easy. It makes a hard task even harder. Actual simplicity is what makes things simple and easy. I'm doing my part and helping people switch to simple, easy, and understandable. It really is making lives better.

Gemini Time!

[link—standalone]

Attention: you should be using Gemini.

I think the indie web space is pretty cool, if you do it right, but the tempatation to make anything but a static website is very strong. Most people don't do it right. Web bloat - JavaScript frameworks that bring in piles of dependencies with security issues, style sheets that only work in Chrome, dynamic content that takes forever to load, tracking, ads, and all sorts of other wastes of bandwidth and electricity - these are all status quo on the modern web. Want to use an older computer? Sorry, your average news or recipe website will bring it to its knees. These sites should just serve text, and maybe an image or two, but they do not.

Why a new protocol?

Essentially, nobody but hyper-nerds avoids web bloat. Web bloat is making us throw away perfectly usable computers, create e-waste, killing our planet with useless energy consumption, giving power to massive surveillance corpos, and recklessly introducing security problems left and right. Web bloat is morally wrong. The Web worked fine without JS and CSS frameworks. It can work that way again. It should. But now that this is the status quo, companies won't give it up - turning what should be text-only sites into interactive web applications that track your usage and location and battery level and heartbeat and GPS location is too tempting to most webmasters, assisted of course by marketing departements. You almost never see web bloat on a regular blog or personal website.

With a new protocol that is intentionally limited, temptation is avoided. Terminal-based browsers are a first-class citizen. Text is downloaded near-instantly. There's no energy waste because 100% of the page's content is, well, actual content, not literal junk that nobody wants.

I've had a gemini capsule (that's what we call websites) set up on this server for a while: (gemini link). You will need a special browser to use it. I like Amfora, but if you must have a GUI, Lagrange is also fantastic.

The Gemini space is like what the web was like in the early days. Before powerful computers, before smartphones. It's beautiful and interesting and full of people - not bloat.

Revisiting

[link—standalone]

A blog post I saw today reminded me that the website it's on exists. This website happens to be a website I heavily referenced when first replacing the BIOS on my Thinkpad X220. It was a little over five years ago.

Much has changed in my life since then. I still have the X220, though it's been replaced (by another Coreboot-powered machine). I still use most of the software I started using around that time (except for X/i3 -> Wayland/Sway), and my usage of stable, predictable console tools has only increased. It started with the X220. I was a Linux user for a long while before I went all in on CLI-only and tiling window managers, and I'm far happier using an environment that's "mine". The meme-worthy "Thinkpad + [core/libre]boot + Arch Linux + tiling window manager" combination is actually quite comfy and Tyler's blog set me on the path.

The whole "I installed Arch Linux and now I really understand how my system works" trope is only kind of untrue. Understanding commands and components that go into building a usable desktop really helped me get a grasp of what I was working with. I had to know about, install, and configure video drivers, console configuration, bootloaders, the initramfs, disk partitioning, text editors, networking, sudo, systemd, audio, and a GUI, to name a few. Configuring these got me much more comfortable with command line editors and tmux. Rescuing a system from a live CD and doing the whole bind mounts of /proc and /sys wasn't useless - it helped me grow. Editing config files to modify my desktop was intially scary, but I am now very comfortable looking at config files, finding documentation, using man, and being able to troublshoot systems. This has not only helped me customize my desktop, but also really influenced the direction of my career and helped me land some sweet jobs.

To think it all started because I saw a thinpad and a cool window manager setup somewhere on the Internet, and thought to myself, "that's really cool! I want that!" I now use software that's stable, predictable, scriptable, and heavily configurable. Most of what I do on my computer is on the command line, in a terminal, and that journey because of some cool screenshots. I'm quite glad I fell for this meme.

Cool desktops don't change, they adapt and shift only when the user needs them to. Now, and for the past few years, that's my desktop, too. And that's pretty cool.
Thanks Tyler!

Ownership

[link—standalone]

I've lived in an apartment for 2 months now, after living in an owned home the last few years. I now know I vastly prefer the latter, as well as running Linux, self-hosting, and not using a smartphone. Here's what I like about ownership.

When I own something, I am in control. For better or for worse, I have both the ownership and responsibility. If something breaks, I can and must fix it. When I rent something, I must wait for the real owner to fix it, and they often take longer and do a worse job than I would. At a job of mine when we hosted our own email server, it almost never went down, and if it did, it was quick to get back up or fail over, because of our high redundancy, low complexity, and complete ownership. O365, AWS, GCP, and others went down a few times, for multiple hours at a time, during that 2 year period. Our uptime was better because we had an owned system that was less complex.

If I want to change something, I can. Simple as that. Our cabinets here don't have handles and my work Macbook doesn't have a competent window manager. I would change both, if I could.

Things aren't changed out from under my feet when I own something. Ownership is stable and predictable. Rent will increase, user interfaces will change, APIs will go away, the owners might decide to shut the place down or turn off the service - I live at the whims of others. While I own the movies on my NAS and hard drive, but I've had streaming or digital "purchases" take my stuff away before. There are plenty of people running KDE3 and GNOME2 (Trinity, MATE) because they forked the old versions they liked and kept them around. Imagine if you could keep, customize, and update Windows 98 or iOS 6 yourself.

Ownership is harder. There's more stuff you have to deal with that most would rather not. But I like stability, privacy, security, and living on my terms, not the terms of some arbitrary master who may not share my values. Unless you own your stuff, use open protocols, and make sure you are in control of as much as you can be, you will spend your whole life getting the rug pulled out from under you. That's frustrating!

Living in this awful apartment has whiplashed me so hard that I want to start hosting my own email.*

*For the non-technical: self-hosting email is not generally considered a good idea on a small scale or individual basis, because the potential risks are too high for something as critical to modern life as email. It is a siren's song for sysadmins, both green and seasoned alike, as controlling one's own email does offer a great deal of control over personal data.

SXMO - Peak Linux User

[link—standalone]

It was only a matter of time.

Back in February, I gave sxmo a serious try for the first time. I didn't quite get it and it didn't really "click" for me. I'd tried it before, because I've tried every postmarketOS interface at some point, but this was the first time I really truly gave it a go.

You know where this story ends. Like everything good in *NIX-land, it has a high initial learning curve, but is more lightweight, customizable, and ultimately, more user-friendly by virtue of being hackable. I use sway on the desktop, and now I'm using it on my phone, too. I don't have to understand how to use two different interfaces, becasue they're the same! It's like the people that use an iPhone and an iPad-as-laptop - it's the same interface between multiple devices.

Tiling window managers can actually make a lot of sense on smaller screens:

  • window decorations usually end up being smaller or nonexistent
  • it's easy to add in customizable launchers
  • scripting is a "first-class citizen", leading to less actions per task (even Apple thinks it's important)
  • window managers are lighter weight than a full "desktop" environment, leading to longer battery life
(This is only kind of a joke.)

A tiling window manager on a phone is peak Linux ricer. It's unironically hilarious. But also, seriously, this is the most fun I've had in a while. Being able to ssh into my phone and control its functions from an ncurses-like interface is incredible. Using the buttons in a different way, contextually, is also really cool. Before sxmo, the keyboard case was only kind of okay, but with sxmo, the keyboard case actually makes a lot of sense. This is a really natural way to use the pinephone - why else would one have this thing but not for hacking around in a totally weird Linux environment?

There are, of course, a few changes I've had to make to the environment, shortcuts, hooks, libraries and programs I've compiled from source and stuffed into my ~/.config directory... but that's what this was designed for. It's why I bought a Linux Phone. I'm really happy to be able to treat my telephone like a computer, instead of an appliance. Every aspect is a hacker's dream come true.

I Reinstalled Fedora Without Losing Data

[link—standalone]

Yesterday I broke NetworkManager, and was reminded of the power of a separate /home partition.

It didn't recognize any devices - WiFi or ethernet. Reinstalling packages from downloads after wiping out configuration didn't seem to work either. I didn't want to spend much time figuring out why, because NetworkManager is a useful tool but highly complex house of cards, and because I'm a tired, busy man these days. I think it had something to do with rfkill, and the hardware WiFi switch on the computer.

Fedora defaults to the following layout (since about 33?):
/boot (1GB ext4)
/efi (512MB fat32, if you are EFI)
/ (rest-of-disk BTRFS volume)
/home (rest-of-disk BTRFS subvolume)
And I used this to my advantage! You can reinstall Fedora on / while leaving /home alone.

How I did it:
In the Fedora installer, I did custom partitioning (but not 'advanced custom'), decrypted my volume, selected to reformat the primary volume as /, and then told it to mount my /home at /home, but not reformat it. I also chose to reformat /boot. (The computer in question is not EFI).

I've never had to do this before, but knew it was theoretically possible. A quick dnf list --installed > packages.txt before reinstall helped me get back up and running quickly. And of course I had a full backup of my data should everything have gone south. At the end of it all, I had reinstalled my OS, reinstalled all my preferred programs, and retained all my data. The whole process took about an hour. Some of my manual packages I will have to rebuild, but I expected that anyway, and should have just put them in copr in the first place.

Lessons here:

  1. Always install with a separate /home partition
  2. Always keep install media around
  3. Keep backups so you're not afraid to try something new
  4. NetworkManager is great when it works but don't make it angry!
The end.

Responsibility, Availability, and Free Software

[link—standalone]

I'm conflicted, and want to ask for advice.

But first, two short anecdotes.

  • When I was in college, I was available and reachable to my roommate. He woke me up late at night. His girlfriend's sister was sick and needed a ride to the hospital. I had a car. Since I was available, right there in his home with him, he asked me, and I gave him my keys. This enabled him to do something good for his girlfriend (now wife), and I was helpful and useful because I was available.
  • Shortly after college, I got a text message late in the evening from a friend. Because I was reachable, and available, I was able to respond to her need quickly and with love and attention. Now we're married.
Because I was reachable and available to my friends, I was able to affect positive change in my life, or help a friend out. I think that it is important to be reachable and available to one's friends.

Some definitions are required for me to be properly understood.
available: being immediately and easily able to freely give of one's time. Example: "Mary has a flexible job and takes every break she can to go out with her friends. She is always available."
reachable: being able to receive and respond to requests in a timely fashion. Example: "Colin answers his phone on the first ring, and responds to emails within the hour. He's very reachable."

I have a full-time job and a family. I have some hobbies and projects that I enjoy. I'm not always available but I should not be difficult to reach. I stay at home most of the time, have a good Internet connection, and have a lot of computers that are frequently on. It shouldn't be hard to get open a communication channel. There are a lot of ways to contact me.

Should I have a preference on how people should contact me with computers?
Should I, as I have in the past, enforce that preference?

But I worry about this a lot. I worry that my extreme preference for Free Software is (funnily enough) excluding people from being able to interact with me. Now, that doesn't make a lot of sense to me, because if I insist on Free Software, no one should be prohibited from contacting me. The only barrier is convenience. If the person on the other end for whatever reason refuses Freedom, I guess there's nothing I can do about it.

However, "don't make perfect the enemy of good" - if something is good enough, I should let it exist and work. I shouldn't put a bunch of silly overly-technical flaming hoops up for people to jump through, just to call me on the phone. It seems to confuse people that I actually do care about how they contact me. I just want it to be safe and reliable - unfortunately that's kind of a hard problem to solve these days.

All this to say, I want to use the weird computer stuff I want to use, because I'm a guy who likes weird computers. However, I use communication solutions that are, by their very nature, available to anyone and everyone, regardless of what they use. I don't put up barriers. I don't force people to do unethical things. It might be slightly more difficult than laying on the couch, eating chips, and channel surfing, but come on, you're better than that anyway.

Anyway. That's about it. I want to be reachable, because I am available. I want to be friendly and helpful and reliable to my friends and family. I believe that I am, and that enforcing my communication channels to be good things, but not necessarily easy, is kind of what I'm here for. Why even use a computer to talk to me if you're not interested in that sort of thing?

When Normal is Not Normal

[link—standalone]

When people define the normal usage of computers and computing technology, they usually mean specific things - smartphones, appliance computers, web browsers, office suites, and the like. Nothing remotely complicated or, in their eyes, abnormal.

But these are computers. Programmable machines that calculate and compute. The coolest thing about computers is that they can be programmed and their behavior changed at will. Even so, that's not on the normie-approved list of what to do with a computer.

The most normal use of a computer is programming the computer.

What else are they here for? They're here for users to make them do arbitrary things, to write code and run it, and to hack and tinker and change the system at will. Using a computer with a half dozen high-rez cameras, a sensor array, multiple wireless communication modules, an enormous color display, and more compute power than existed in the whole world in 1980, all just to look at pictures of food, or view advertisements, or waste enormous amounts of energy on a communication protocol over 20 years old - that is truly odd.

A computer that can't be programmed misses the point of computers. The ability to change what the system does is what makes it so desirable. Using a computer without modifying it is insane behavior. Personal Computers are devices that can be modified to suit the needs of the user. One size does not fit all. It is our differences and uniqueness that make us interesting. We can't all be put in one box. So, reader, if you must use a computer, make it work for you. Program it. Hack it. Tinker and modify.
Use it normally.

Linux Mobile Setup

[link—standalone]

This is a current snapshot of how I set up my mobile Linux system (Pine64 Pinephone, as of February 2022) for future reference and to help me collate all my information in one place. I usually forget something when I upgrade or reinstall the device.

I create my OS image with pmbootstrap. pmbootstrap offers a lot of choices. I choose edge, phosh, and set my locale to en_US.UTF-8, as God intended. I have a 2GB MicroSD card running JumpDrive to install PostmarketOS to the eMMC of the Pinephone with --sdcard=/dev/sda --fde.

After the system boots, I join it to the network, and install the following:
vim git htop rsync mpv ffmpeg urlscan newsboat phosh-antispam vvmplayer dino flatpak gnome-podcasts flatpak numberstation yad seahorse powersupply geary evolution foot dconf-editor yad
Next I add add flathub and my repo to flatpak remotes. I install my builds of Signal, Axolotl, and my Dice program (all detailed on my repo's site) and NhekoReborn, FluffyChat, KeePassXC, and Komikku. I install KeePassXC on from flatpak because it keeps QT libraries off the main system, and I'm installing them with NhekoReborn anyway.

I run the restore outlined in the "upgrade" detailed above from my encrypted "backup" sdcard, which I regularly rsync --delete my home directory to.

On PostmarketOS, I add the following cron entries:

# daytime
30 06 * * * /home/adam/bin/profile-swap restore
30 07 * * * /home/adam/bin/themeswitch.sh light
# nighttime
59 19 * * * /home/adam/bin/profile-swap store
59 19 * * * /home/adam/bin/profile-swap silent
30 19 * * * /home/adam/bin/themeswitch.sh dark
And speaking of cron, I use rc-update to add crond and waked to the default runlevel. Waked ensures the clock program will wake up the phone and sound the alarm when appropriate.

The keyboard case is fun. It's kind of broken and not really ideal - I'd like to to be thinner and larger - but it's awesome to have when on the go. At home I mostly don't use it because I can't plug the phone into my dock when the case is attached - power from 2 sources at once is bad. When using the keyboard I change the scaling from 200% to 150% or 175%. Phosh doesn't work well in landscape at all.

And that's about it. Future self, hope this helps.

Pinephone February 2022

[link—standalone]

How's the Pinephone thing going? Glad you asked, nobody!

I write this from convergence land. Recent updates have transformed this thing from "okay-ish" to "surprisingly usable"! My phone-computer is plugged into my monitor, and I am typing with external perhipherals. It's pretty sweet. There has been an enormous amount of work by many others (and a tiny amount by yours truly!) to get this thing to a workable state. I imagine the Librem 5 or Pinephone Pro would be even better, but this is what I've got. It's still incredible.

My main SIM card lives in the Pinephone most of the time. I occasionally swap back to my Android device, but try to avoid using it otherwise. It is faster, has a working camera, calls sound clear due to HD Voice, and it can run Signal natively, but I don't like it nearly as much. The Pinephone is what I want to use.

On a daily basis, the phone does very few things when undocked - it needs to do calls, messages, music, podcasts, and maybe some email? That's about it. When docked, I treat it like a full computer. The details of my personal software stack are as follows:

  • It runs PostmarketOS Edge with the Phosh UI. I've found this to be the most "feature complete" OS that requires the least amount of hackery on my part. I used to run Arch and that worked fine too, just a little less usable, and I had to compile a lot of packages and features that PostmarketOS just has natively.
  • Chatty is for XMPP (with omemo) and SMS/MMS. It works well!
  • Dino for XMPP as a secondary client.
  • Geary and Evolution for email.
  • NhekoReborn (Flatpak) for Matrix.
  • KeepassXC with scale-to-fit for password management. It is far better than gnome-passwordsafe/"Secrets" due to the insanely high resource usage of the latter.
  • GNOME Podcasts to listen to podcasts.
  • Lollypop is for music.
  • Signal-Desktop flatpak (built from these excellent arm64 signal builds).

The battery life isn't great. I await my keyboard case anxiously. It can't really do the whole "portable hotspot" thing, but that can be easy enough to figure out.

I'm satisfied with this for now. It's not great but it is incredibly cool and useful having a full computer in my pocket. It's what I dreamed smartphones could be, but never were.

Breaking Changes

[link—standalone]

Breaking Changes are always interesting to deal with. Recently I've been trying to get a few bugs fixed in software I used, and build the resulting software. I use GNOME Podcasts on my Pinephone, and it used to have a pretty annoying bug where it would download the album art every time you viewed the media widget in the notification panel or lock screen. This was the byproduct of several bugs, both in phosh, and in gnome-podcasts. The bugs have been fixed, but it's taking a while for them to move down the pipe from git master, to release, to distributions. Sometimes, you just have to take matters into your own hands.

So a-building we will go. I really should get another Raspberry Pi to use as an arm64 build server, but until then I'll continue using the free ARM server on Oracle Cloud's Free Tier. I am not a fan of Oracle the company but they do offer a pretty nice set of resources for free, which includes ARM resources. (4 cores, 24 GB RAM, combine-able or divisible into up to 4 individual systems.) I've got the ARM resources set up just as one server with all the resources running Ubuntu.

In order to get an interactive Arch setup, I run Docker on the system, managed with Distrobox. I use the menci/archlinuxarm base image, and just exec into that. Distrobox automatically shares the home directory transparently with the container, so all the files I need are accesible both from the host system and the Arch Linux ARM container. The setup isn't automated or anything. I just write PKGBUILDs and run makepkg. Pretty primitive, but most of the software I am building has a pretty hacky, beta, manual setup anyway.

Anyway, all this to say, Meson broke on everyone recently! (issue 1, issue 2) GNOME Podcasts, like pretty much all moder GNOME/Linux Desktop software, uses Meson. It's a system I've been meaning to learn for a while but have basically zero understanding of. Browsing the issues, I figured out that I needed to imitate some of the other projects, and remove some declarations Meson's Makefile equivalent in order to get GNOME Podcasts to build:

sed -e "s/i18n.merge_file (.*$/i18n.merge_file (/" -i podcasts-$pkgver/podcasts-gtk/resources/meson.build

And now I'm a happy GNOME Podcast listener. I learned a few new things from this experience. Upstream changes happen super fast; Arch Linux doesn't always have the latest stuff (glibc on Fedora is newer!); and when you're living on the bleeding edge of software releases, waiting on critical issues and hardware support, releases seem to come really slowly!

FOSS is great because I could look at public code and public issues, figure out how to build my Podcast app myself, fix the build, install it, and use it. Thanks to all the GNOME and Meson people who figured this out before I did. I can't get over how cool it is to have a smartphone I can do this with.

Better Might Not Be Ready

[link—standalone]

or, you might not be ready for it

Recently a high-profile Internet Elder called the concept of decentralized financial systems a bad idea, mainly because of energy consumption, and because people who got into it early had a lot of money, and those who didn't care about it had none. You might not understand what I'm talking about - that's okay. This instance isn't important, but what is important is that he misses the point by a mile and a half, and is using his weight to publicly discourage improvement of a potentially better system because it isn't ready yet.

Bitcoin and Monero are good, sound, stable cryptocurrencies that make the world better. Cryptocurrency does consume a lot of energy, sure, but there are a lot of things that do, and good ideas should hardly be the first on the chopping block. The first should be the yearly smartphone and chip cycle - release a new one every 3 or 4 years, and instead of throwing the old stuff away, unlock it and open source the code, so instead of throwing batteries and metals in landfills, we can throw away planned obsolecense! If we're dead-set on decreasing the energy consumption of financial systems, why turn off the good one? How about we turn off MasterCard or Visa instead? Maybe the stock market, and betting on the fate of companies? That churns through electricity, and lots of people get hurt by it, and by the endless purusit of year-over-year growth.

The point is this:
Bitcoin and Monero are better than whatever currency your government's using. They belong to no entity but the people that use them. You can't make more. You can't cheat or steal or lie about what's going on. These systems put the power back into the hands of the people, and take it away from adversarial and oppressive organizations that want to control and change the system on their whims. The Linux Desktop is better than Windows and macOS. It supports mutiple architectures, brings decade-old computers back to business, doesn't exfiltrate your data to the NSA, and generally give the user more choice and customization than the other proprietary systems. It too gives the power to the user, making them responsible, but free.

But they aren't quite ready yet.

Photographers need Photoshop, architects need AutoCAD and SolidWorks, organizations need Active Directory support, and most people just buy a computer in a retail store and use what it comes with. There are still plenty of problems with using a Linux Desktop, though I've been exclusively using it for almost five years, and doing everything but gaming there for about ten. Although it's ready for most people, there are still plenty for whom it's not accessible - either beause of actual accessibility concerns, or just lack of knowledge.

Cryptocurrencies are the same. Just like you don't need 10,000 different choices of Linux Distribution (Hannah Montana Linux, RebeccaBlackOS, Biebian Linux, and not-Linux PonyOS are all real computer operating systems you can use!) you don't need DogeCoin or other stupid meme cryptocurrencies. Not every United State needs to have their own coin, and neither do messaging apps. At some point, we all settled on a standard for communication, and that's email. Eventually one or a few cryptocurrencies will "win" and they'll be the ones people take seriously - seems like Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Monero are all the ones that the DeFi and Privacy people can agree on, so maybe them?

Anyway, point is, maybe the things that are better don't quite hit the mark for being as functionally complete as we want yet. But that doesn't mean that the idea sucks or it's a monstrosity that's destroying lives. The idea is better, but the implementation just needs to be ironed out. Bitcoin consumes a lot of power, but Monero's algorithm is ASIC resistant and can be mined on normal CPUs, making it something that's far more attainable for regular folks. Linux is truly Free (as in freedom), but in order to live in the better brighter freer future, we need more better programs to work on Linux and other FOSS operating systems.

We have a glimpse of the future with us today, in the form of FOSS and Decentralized tech, but we still need to help it along. Get comfortable with it, use it a bunch, figure out what's wrong, and fix it! Giving up and throwing in the towel is wrong. We won't get a better world like that. Sometimes it sucks to be an early adopter, but you don't have to go all in - experiment, play around, report bugs, and get more people thinking about how to fix the problems. It's slow going but Rome wasn't built in a day. No need to use something in production that doesn't suit your needs, but don't denigrate it as a failure when it's clearly built to last. The future's just getting started.
