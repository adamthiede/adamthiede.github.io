Why I Dislike SMS/MMS ("texting")

Because I want to save money, use mostly Free Software, and avoid some spyware, I use "different" phones, and smaller carriers (like Ting, Mint, etc).

Because of this, historically SMS and MMS have not worked reliably for me. I used Ting back when it was a Sprint reseller on devices customized custom Open Source Android, and I had to fiddle a LOT with APN settings. Sometimes things didn't work right.

Eventually I discovered that all standard phone carrier communications (SMS, MMS, calls) were transmitted in cleartext. With publicly-available information and $200 of equipment, anyone could intercept these communications. Not only that, but most carriers store this plaintext data for upwards of five years and give it to whomever asks. Yikes. When I send messages or calls I don't want anyone to be able to read it but me and the person I send it to. Despite all our modern technology this basic, reasonable requirement is incredibly difficult to achieve! (I wonder why.)

MMS also compresses any attachments, so you might as well not send pictures and video, and it only works with mobile data switched on. You can't send arbitrary files (PDFs, Documents, etc.) because of this compression.

Meanwhile, around this time most people started using messaging apps anyway, so moving over to multi-device-capable communication was a big win for me. I never liked typing on phone keyboards. Also, a phone is one device. SMS cannot be done via a computer with a real keyboard in most circumstances. If I leave my phone at home I cannot use SMS at another phone, but I can log into my email or use other Internet-based messaging. Most of these are at least encrypted in transit as well, so not just anyone sitting on the network can read them. Nice!

In summary, SMS and MMS are bereft of features, inherently backdoored, unsafe, single-device-only comms. With the proliferation of smartphones, it's trivial to choose an open, encrypted, multi-device comms program. Even if you don't use a smartphone, you can use XMPP, email, Matrix, or a number of other open, non-smartphone-dependent messaging services.

What to use instead:

  • Email, while unencrypted at rest, is still better. It is transmitted to providers encrypted, and unlike SMS there is at least the option to GPG encrypt data.
  • XMPP is ideal because of its maturity, extensibility, and low resource cost. Matrix is another good option.
  • Signal locks you into chatting with only Apple and Google Smartphone users. WhatsApp, Viber, and others like them are backdoored by Big Tech and also require smartphones. I can't recommend these unless everyone in your life plans to use a smartphone forever. (If you're reading this, that's not a correct assumption, because of me. Hi!)

However!

I've been on record caring a lot about what messaging system I use, or what other people use to contact me. I pushed a lot of people to use different methods of digital communication over the years, and nothing good gained traction. I have had to stop caring as much because I've legitimately lost contact with people for not wanting to use backdoored communication.

It is, in my eyes, incredibly easy to use something good, if you put in time to find it. (Here, I'll help you out: get this and use this.)

But the more I put effort into making my digital life a little saner, the more I seem to push others away. Why? Is everyone so addicted to being spied upon that they immediately reject something kind of difficult, in favor of the easy, low road? (Most of the people in my life are Christians. Isn't that the opposite of what you should do?) I try to fight against bad people doing bad things, in my own little ways. In this realm it mostly means using sane, small tech, contributing to it with my time and talent, and teaching others to use it. It really does improve the lives of those I help out! It just takes effort. It's minimally hard, whereas what you're doing now has inertia.

So, sure, if you insist on texting, I'll text you. I've decided not to be as hard-line and heartless as I once was. But consider this: you are making me compromise my values. And they're not weird, out-there values. (See this paragraph.) They're values that you, if you thought about it, should share.
