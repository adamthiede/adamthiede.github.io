When I see an interesting thread on HN, I end up writing half a blog post in the reply section, then I realize I actually have a blog/website thingy to put them on. This is a response to <a href="https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=19286970">this thread</a> that links to this blog post: <a href="https://debugandrelease.blogspot.com/2019/03/ui-redesigns-are-mostly-waste-of-time.html">https://debugandrelease.blogspot.com/2019/03/ui-redesigns-are-mostly-waste-of-time.html</a>

Here's my reply.

UI Redesigns are not necessarily a waste of time, but I think that backend dev and admins tend to get burnt by the tools they use changing drastically for no perceived reason. If something works, and works well, shouldn't it stay that way, at least until the UI gets overburdened with the functionality that's added in year over year?

This always comes to mind for me: <a href="https://twitter.com/dcseifert/status/101669738566424985">link to a tweet</a>

In recent years my pain with redesigns has always revolved around 1) More whitespace and 2) less information density. The screenshot of "Harris Bank" in the article is dripping with this - the menu items are enormous, and the entire main body portion could be simplified to something 1/4 the size.

Take Windows' redesign of Server 2012 Server Manager, for example. This is the Server Manager in 2008. Admins know it well, and it has a lot of information in a small space. It is fast and configurable, and behaves much like MMC and other very familiar Windows management tools.

<img class="blog-img" src="../i/2008sm.png"></img>

This is Server 2012's redesign of that same program.

<img class="blog-img" src="../i/2012sm.png"></img>

I'll be the first to say that the hate for Windows 8 was undeserved, but this broke a lot of workflows, looks instantly outdated and ugly, and doesn't really contribute to any better functionality. It's slower, more difficult to use, there's more whitespace, and half the dashboard view has this big 'ol useless tutorial section.

Sometimes, redesigns have a purpose, and they can contribute to a much more usable, useful system. But consider the users, not just making a mark on the company or whatever. Following modern trends is a surefire way to make your design look outdated in a couple years.

Redesigning a critical system means that users have to do more work to figure out how to use the system. Often it's out of their control. The <a href="https://www.tomshardware.com/reviews/fedora-16-gnome-3-review,3155-27.html">backlash that came with Gnome3</a> is something often cited as a redesign that was very anti-user. Many people just use Gnome3 because it sees the most work and is the top pick for most major distros, not because they actually like it. The path of least resistance wins again.

Anyway, one thing to take away - be aware of how you're changing systems, and make sure you take everything into account before making a huge change. It's worth talking to the actual end users of the system to find out how your work will impact them, too. Don't be afraid to cancel, roll back, or postpone the work! Operational considerations should come before eye candy.
