Oh boy, it's February already!
Time sure flies when you're doing nothing. This month my <a href="https://clockworkpi.com">ClockworkPi Game Shell</a> arrived. That's what this post is about.

I've owned a few interesting GNU/Linux portables, and been disappointed by pretty much everything. Android is a mess - ancient Linux, sans GNU. The <a href="https://wiki.lineageos.org/devices/xt897">Motorola Photon Q</a> with LineageOS came the closest to what I could want, but being relatively unstable and having non-modular parts was pretty hard to deal with.

The <a href="https://hackaday.com/2018/04/03/is-this-the-end-for-the-c-h-i-p/">CHIP and PocketCHIP</a>, a now defunt Raspberry Pi-like handheld GNU/Linux machine, was both really cool and promising, but incredibly underpowered. This wasn't such a big deal - the display was large, it got frequently updated, and had really good software. With only built-in (non microSD-based) eMMC storage, it became a chore to re-flash when I tweaked the software into oblivion. Its whole premise was that it was useful on the go, but the keyboard was impossible to use. It is essentially too large to be pocketable and too small to use as a laptop replacement. I've dug it up recently and have half a mind to attempt to put a newer OS on it, but I'm just waiting for the eMMC to fail at this point.

Of course, I've owned a few Raspberry Pi's (hasn't everyone?) and have been pleased with them. The Pi 3 performs its home server duties admirably, and the SD card has only died once in about 3 years. Even then, it's trivial to dd one of my backups back on to a new card and I'm up and running again in a snap.
The Pi Zero is a beautifully competent machine as well, despite its low specifications. I've got one that I use for system board flashing (Coreboot, broken BIOS, etc) and other one-off projects. For $5 you can't get a much better system.

But hand-held open-source just keeps getting better! This "ClockworkPi" device is really something - all the software and hardware is open-source, so I could 3D-print myself another case if this one breaks, or hack the launcher's code and add new program icons. The keypad is arduino-compatible, so if I find (or build) another keypad with more buttons, or one with a full QWERTY, I can 3D-print a new top case, wire it up, set up xmodmap, and I'm in business. Furthermore, it's a full quad-core chip, much more powerful than the CHIP or the Pi Zero. The Emulators and the adorable case are just part of the reason I'm happy about it. It's stable, durable, high-quality, and quite fun so far. It even comes with a GPIO output cable. Kudos!  

Even if this becomes abandonware in another year or three (Looking at you, CHIP) it's at least a cheap little computer that's fun to play with and makes me excited about the possibilities of handheld, pocketable, wireless Linux machines. I'd love to ditch the iPhones/Androids for something fully open and as capable as this device. I know every year is the year of the Linux Desktop, but I can't help but think we're getting closer and closer to a fully open/free handheld running some variant of GNU/Linux. The Librem 5 is always worth mentioning, and Pine64 just made a few interesting announcements as well. Those are worth your time and worth investing in, becuase even if you don't really like those devices specifically, vote with your wallet, as they say. Invest in the future of a true, real, fully-free GNU/Linux system in your pocket.
