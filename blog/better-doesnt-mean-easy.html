Better Doesn't Mean Easy

[link—standalone]

Most of my life, I've been in pursuit of bettering myself at the expense of my time and effort, because my whole life I've been young and full of energy. This is the way young people operate - we sacrifice our time, energy, and effort to achieve high-minded ideals. It's a good thing to do - it's how people end up changing the world. As people get older, they convince themselves they're OK with mediocrity, or not meeting their goals, because they get tired, lazy, or stuck in their ways.

This post is about the value of work. Exercise, or physical labor in general, is something we all acknowledge is good for us, but it's hard. Going for a run, lifting weights, building something, or spending a weekend doing yard work, is not easy. By definition you're expending a lot of energy, tearing your muscle fibers, getting sweaty, and feeling pain. It's not very convenient, but it's indisputably good, and nobody can help you do it. You can't have someone do the work for you - at some point, they cross the line between spotting your bench press, and just lifting the bar for you.

Convenience maximalists always end up getting played for a fool by charlatans. This isn't unique to any industry. People know to haggle on prices with car salesmen, hire an independent inspector before buying a home, look at ingredient lists before buying food, and read contracts before signing them. This is all common sense so far.

Complex problems often require complex or specialized tools. Many musical instruments are complicated tools, difficult to master, and require ongoing maintenance. Shop tools are powerful, but dangerous if used improperly. Both video-editing software and CAD software are incredibly complex and deep tools, but the complexity is required for the tasks they perform. In any of these cases, hiding the complexity or making a more simplified version of the thing would not make it more accessible, because the task itself isn't simple in the first place. It would dumb down the tool such that it either was missing critical functionality, or couldn't perform the task at all.

People shouldn't shy away from hard things. And of course, my angle of attack is about computers. Many people use computers for critical things in their life, things they consider incredibly valuable, but since they don't really know how to use computers, they end up relying on those who would abuse them, or otherwise take advantage of their laziness.

Case and point: Internet Service Providers (ISP) and renting modems. When you pay Comcast or Time Warner or whoever for Internet service, they dark-pattern you into renting a garbage-tier modem/router/wifi combo box, and you end up paying orders of magnitude more than it's worth. It's a Leatherman: it does all the things, it's kinda bad at all of them, but it does do all the things. In ISP-provided boxes, the settings are limited, they're often full of unpatched security holes, and the ISP has full control over the device. Anyone with any sense buys a modem, then a separate router that they have full control over, and configures it for maximum security and independence. While the upfront cost is higher, and there is about an hour of research involved, many people won't do it, because it's not easy enough.

I could go on and on here - password managers, VPN services, companies saying "we value your privacy", cloud backup systems, streaming services, antivirus... the list of computer scams ranges far and wide. Most people don't even realize they're getting scammed.

That's because even the most respectable and upstanding of individuals have become soft, whiny smooth brains when it comes to computers. They throw their hands up and decide that since the problem is "too hard" it's not worth solving. This is not the world I want to create. When I imagine a future, I imagine driven, independent individuals learning and creating, not pacified idiots tapping away at social media on their iPhones.

There's nothing wrong with letting your doctor handle your medicines or your mechanic handle your engine maintenance. But in the modern world, especially with computers, convenient things are often harmful - to stay safe, stay sane, and get something truly better, you just have to lift the bar yourself.

If we want the world to get better, we have to envision the better that we want, and work hard for it.
