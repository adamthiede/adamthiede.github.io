These days, I see so many people using their phones while driving. My old man brain still thinks people are either on a call, but usually it's people holding these things and looking at them. Big, bright glass rectangles that absorb every waking moment of someone's time, and they can't be bothered, not even for the safety of grandmothers and children, to not put down their little Facebook dopamine machines for one minute.

Not only is it infuriating to see people using these things while driving, they're not even using them for anything useful. I can forgive someone who's on the phone - holding it to their face, or obviously using the speakerphone. But most people are engaging in text-based communication using a messaging program that has one thousand buttons on the screen, all light-gray-on-white. Not only is using these things incredibly difficult (see <a href="https://blog.fawny.org/2018/10/22/hardtouse/">here</a> and <a href="https://jnd.org/apples_products_are_getting_harder_to_use_because_they_ignore_principles_of_design/">here</a>), but they're only getting worse.

In the days of flip phones, If I got a call while driving, I could open the phone, press the speakerphone button, drop it on my lap, and talk. All without looking at the darn thing. Took me two seconds.

Now I have to grab a massive shiny glass rectangle, figure out where to swipe (sometimes wearing gloves prevents this from even working), look at a glowing screen to tap "speakerphone", and then set it down. It's much worse than it used to be. I find it hard not to answer sometimes - being a parent now, I'm a little more twitchy - but increasingly I'm getting less phone calls, so it's a moot point anyway. More often I get text-based message than a phone call where I can actually here somone's voice. Voice is faster, easier, and more personal. It's easier to encrypt and secure text, sure, but Signal has made things really easy, even for my techno-illiterate family.

All of this to say - phones in the car is a problem, especially since they're basically tiny computers now, and they're not getting any easier to use, just harder. Modern design has made mobile phones burdensome and difficult to interact with, and people use them more often for more and more stuff, so the desire to use them, addictive as they are, just grows stronger. Using them in the car is a natural extension of the original intent of mobile telephones (I'm talking about <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car_phone">car phones</a>, in case that wasn't clear to our younger readers), so it is only natural that one would want to use a phone here.

The gamification of most of our communication through social media has made people desire its presence even more. Look at something like Facebook Messenger (<a href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/messenger/id454638411">screenshots here</a>) - so many bright colors on a white background. So many different interface elements. So many tiny buttons and weird shapes that can change depending on context. Something simpler (<a href="https://9to5mac.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/6/2014/03/messages.png">iMessage, prior to iOS 8</a>) could improve readablity and usefulness, but nobody's after that any more. You're supposed to be using their platforms for longer, with more of your friends, feeding it more of your data, getting locked in further and further until there's no real way to esacpe.

People should stop using their computers in the car, but they won't. People should stop using platforms that disrespect them as a human, and are bad for the world at large, but they won't. There are things that every person should know in order to make the world better - take your medicine and get your vaccines, eat healthy, take the stairs, try to bike or walk instead of driving, don't leave your lights on, don't support dictators that kill people, be nice to others, try to only use platforms that aren't rife with backdoors from untrustworthy companies who mine your personal info for profit, and brush your teeth. You know, basic stuff.

I firmly belive if people understood how bad it was, they'd change. Are we not doing a good enough job? Do people really find it so easy to ignore? Or, the most likely scenario, do people just not care because it's non-physically-representable stuff like personal data and behavior? I think for most people, it's still to hard to understand. We need to do better at telling them WHY these things are bad.
