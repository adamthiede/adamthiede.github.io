Merry Christmas to all.
Ours was pretty great, all things considered.

I received a mystery laptop from my family. Having given them my old 2013 Macbook Air that was no longer in service, they had a laptop that it replaced, and that's been given to me. It's an <a href="https://www.asus.com/Laptops/K50IJ/">Asus K50I</a> - a 15-inch-class beast of a machine from 2009. Almost ten years old. I was very glad to receive it. It had been used as by a now-12-year-old girl. The lid was covered in a somewhat disheveled but tastefully done tape pattern. Not my preference but it didn't look bad or tacky.

The laptop is thick and heavy. Not like my Thinkpad, but thick and heavy in a seemingly useless, unpleasant way. The first order of business was to turn it on and log in. Password was forgotten, but so what - boot to a live Linux CD and do the sethc trick. One "net user" later and we're in.

The machine hadn't been on in over 2 years, so all the versions of installed software were out of date. The BIOS was only one release behind, so I updated that from a USB stick.

It ran an activated, 64-bit version of Windows 7 Home Premium loaded with Asus bloatware. However, the Asus stuff wasn't the weirdest thing on there. There were basically no personal files of note - this was used as a Google Apps machine. In the taskbar were three browsers - Firefox, Chrome, and (guess - what do you think?) Chromium! Bet you didn't see that coming!

Also in the taskbar was a shortcut for Dolphin Emulator. Weird. That's something I did not expect at all.
I went in to uninstall all the Asus garbage, and there was also an instance of a standalone Microsoft SQL Server installation. I will be examining that later! Strange to have on a laptop.

After everything useless was removed from the OS, I went to open it up physically and inspect the hardware. This laptop has a Pentium T4400. It's a 45nm process part, 2 cores, 2.2GHz, 35W, 2009 vintage. Paired with 3GB DDR2 SDRAM (2GB soldered on the board, 1GB in a removable stick) this machine was surprisingly capable. The Hitachi hard drive (320GB) is where I give most of the credit - Hitachi makes immortal disks, and this one performed surprisingly well for all the reboots and uninstalls I did. The wireless card was an "AzureWave" card - it was standard laptop sized, but the PCB extended far beyond the length of a standard card, like it was made to be chopped if it had to be. It was also mostly dust free. Pretty cool.
All in all it was not that bad! Better than expected. I could load a fresh copy of Win10 on this and feel comfortable using it as a desk-based laptop. Nothing is broken on it and it's in decent shape. Ten years old, put together pretty poorly, and still kicking despite all that!
