When Normal is Not Normal

[link—standalone]

When people define the normal usage of computers and computing technology, they usually mean specific things - smartphones, appliance computers, web browsers, office suites, and the like. Nothing remotely complicated or, in their eyes, abnormal.

But these are computers. Programmable machines that calculate and compute. The coolest thing about computers is that they can be programmed and their behavior changed at will. Even so, that's not on the normie-approved list of what to do with a computer.

The most normal use of a computer is programming the computer.

What else are they here for? They're here for users to make them do arbitrary things, to write code and run it, and to hack and tinker and change the system at will. Using a computer with a half dozen high-rez cameras, a sensor array, multiple wireless communication modules, an enormous color display, and more compute power than existed in the whole world in 1980, all just to look at pictures of food, or view advertisements, or waste enormous amounts of energy on a communication protocol over 20 years old - that is truly odd.

A computer that can't be programmed misses the point of computers. The ability to change what the system does is what makes it so desirable. Using a computer without modifying it is insane behavior. Personal Computers are devices that can be modified to suit the needs of the user. One size does not fit all. It is our differences and uniqueness that make us interesting. We can't all be put in one box. So, reader, if you must use a computer, make it work for you. Program it. Hack it. Tinker and modify.
Use it normally.

by Adam Thiede
