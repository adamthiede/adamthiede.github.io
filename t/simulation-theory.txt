<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simulation_hypothesis">The Simulation Hypothesis</a> idea is pretty interesting to me given that I am somewhat knowledgeable about computers, especially Virtualization. Many people think about simulated reality as a Matrix-like system - rooms full of people in coffins or tubes, fed intravenously and brains wired into a collective conscious experience. Like an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_role-playing_game">MMORPG</a> but in reality.

This brings with it the most problems, most notably that people take a lot of energy to maintain, so it is pretty unbelieveable. Second, it begs the question "why". Obviously energy production is a terrible excuse since we'd net negative. Clustered computing is a pretty decent answer, and it does raise quite a few interesting discussion points.

What I'm thinking of, though, is a little more mundane, but pretty sad. This weekend I installed Ubuntu in a virtual machine to test a few things, and of course post-install, as the Desktop variant does, it asked to submit statistics about its installation. It made me chuckle since the machine was wiped only three hours later (testing complete), but it still thought that it was a full-on desktop system. Virtual machines don't usually know they're virtualized - they think they're running on bare hardware, not as a software component on a larger machine. This poor Ubuntu box thought it was going to live a long life on a user's laptop or desktop, and that it would serve them well over the next few years. But it got nuked in less than a day, since I just needed it for a little bit.

So what kind of simulation are we in? Are we a critical infrastructure system, a Windows 2000 install powering something on the power grid? Are we a Red Hat Enterprise Linux 4 box, chugging along unnoticed for years in a corner? Is our simulation more like an IBM Mainframe, running for so long because it has to, or is it more like my Ubuntu VM - just something a kid spins up on his laptop for a few hours to tinker with that gets deleted a few hours later?

Who's running the simulation? Do we assume they care?
