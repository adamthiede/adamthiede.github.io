Ubuntu Derivatives

[link—standalone]

A heavy criticism of a lot of these Ubuntu-based distros is "Ubuntu + a custom PPA", but system76 does actually put in a lot of work into a custom kernel and some neat UI tweaks that make the OS on their hardware really well. They also have a firmware updater for their systems built into the settings program. The whole experience is a lot more refined than Ubuntu, I believe. It's a great OS for their machines, as well as any laptop with hybrid Intel/Nvidia graphics.

To be clear, lots of the Ubuntu-based systems really are just Ubuntu + a custom ppa, or a tweaked Ubuntu full of someone else's software. But system76 writes their own software, makes their own GNOME extensions, and tweak the kernel in their own way for better hardware support. Unlike others, they don't just pile other peoples' stuff on Ubuntu and call it good.

But wait - which Ubuntu-based distros out there don't add something significant to Ubuntu? Let's take a look at the more popular ones. I exclude the offical flavors from this for obvious reasons.

  • Linux Mint provides Cinnamon, their own desktop environment. They provide Timeshift, a very good backup program, and some great GUIs for doing complex packaging things that you can usually only do in the command line in Ubuntu. It's based on only Ubuntu's LTS, but they also maintain a Debian-based version. Not only that, they also partner with hardware vendors and make the MintBox, a fantastic computer. I think they're on 3, now. They've been doing this for years. These are significant additions.
  • ElementaryOS provides a much simpler GUI. However, they've written their own app store, created a consistent Human Interface Guidelines document for their platform, encourage and provide documentation for native development, support actually paying developers of FOSS, and are trying to adopt a way to bring this to other distros (search for "appcenter for everyone"). The curated experience, lack of desktop configurability, and overall UI is sometimes called very "Mac-like", but that's usually as a put down more than anything. The small team at Elementary is not only trying to make their OS better. They're encouraging native Linux Desktop development and encouraging users to pay devs for good stuff. These are significant additions, though less "program in your OS" and more "positive addition to the landscape of Desktop Linux".
  • System76's Pop!_OS (that hurt to type) is one of the few Ubuntu-based distros that doesn't stick only to LTS releases. Their additions are pretty straightforward: a Kernel that works bettery with their systems, a driver manager for their systems, useful tweaks to the GNOME UX via extensions they wrote, power profiles for laptops with hybrid Intel   Nvidia GPUs, Coreboot firmware for their laptops (technically not part of the distro) and an updater for said firmware (actually part of the distro), and now with 20.04 a custom tiling window manager extension for GNOME. They also support Flatpaks and Elementary's app store program. Their repos contain newer version of handy developer tools, nice addons like Lutris for gamers, new versions of the Arduino IDE, and some other stuff I haven't used. They make what I like to think of as "Ubuntu for Human Beings". Their tweaks make Ubuntu a much nicer place to live for the average user, especially if that user runs a system76 laptop.
  • Zorin OS has this badass looking systems management tool . Haven't used it so that's all I know about it. It looks like they're more focused on the enterprise, which is cool. Not something we see a lot of desktop distros doing. They also request you pay for their "Ultimate" edition, which seems like a viable business model, I guess. Gets a pass from me.
  • KDE Neon - basically Kubuntu + Backports PPA on the LTS instead of latest, minus a bunch of packages. The "KDE Experience Distro". Basically their proving ground. Basically a tool they made for themselves. Ok! Kinda an "Ubuntu + PPA" situation, but basically meant as an internal testing too, but they were gracious enough to give it to the masses. Gets a pass.
  • Linux Lite, Peppermint OS, other "lightweight" Ubuntu-base distros: they fill a nice by being super lightweight, but that niche is also served better by things like Lubuntu/Xubuntu.

Overall, the larger Ubuntu derivatives, while taking most (if not all) of their packages from Ubuntu proper, do actually seem to add some value. And the smaller ones, while probably not adding much outside of just how they're configured, they do offer value in the base configuration to their users. That's why the flavors exist - take a desktop environment, add some well-done configuration, set a custom theme, throw in some matching programs, and presto! you have yourself a "distro". When the alternative is Arch or bone-stock desktop environments, these smaller distros, based on a distro based on a distro, make sense.

After all, Ubuntu's just buiding on top of Debian's solid foundation, and they all need Linux and GNU. They're all a derivative of something - and there's nothing wrong with that.

by Adam Thiede
