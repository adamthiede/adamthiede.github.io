User-Friendly Privacy Advice

Hello there! You have a computer or two, and you want reasonable, understandable advice to make it and your activity on it more private? This in-depth guide is a good starting point.

First things first: computer security is a necessary prerequisite to privacy. If you lock your belongings in a transparent safe they are secure but not private. Leaving an opaque safe slightly ajar will not be secure or private.

Unlike with security, it's difficult to ensure that your activity can be truly unobservable on modern computers. Like security, it comes down to control - who has control over your devices, and how they could use that control to spy on your activity. If someone wants to spy on you, it is in their best interest for their infiltration to remain undetected for as long as possible. In order to avoid poisoning the well, you will likely need to obtain all-new hardware, or at the very least, wipe your disks clean and start from scratch, so you can avoid all potential spyware and malware. Removing everything from your personal devices and disconnecting as many things from the Internet as you can will give you a clean slate.

Physical Security

The number one way to keep your data private is to keep it only in physical locations you know about, and keep those physical locations secure.

Hardware/Operating Systems

Smartphones

The elephant in the room: smartphones. Everybody's got 'em, all the manufacturers prattle on about how theirs are the best for privacy and whatnot. In reality, there are only two ways to make a smartphone respect your privacy: somehow gain full control of the device, or throw it into the nearest large body of water.

If you must use a smartphone and want "best-in-class" privacy setup on a tracking device you carry with you, you must ensure that the SIM card and battery are trivially removable, and that you control the entirity of the software stack. So far, only two devices in recent history meet these critera: the Pinephone and Librem 5. These devices, even if compromised, have hardware switches you can use to turn off wireless communication, microphones, and cameras, so they can be assuredly isolated.

The "sane" option is to use an inexpensive Android phone like a Nokia-brand device, and apply a few fixes to it. (Nokia has a track record of delivering relativly quick security updates for a long time, and they run Android One in some cases, so you can ensure your device won't be insecure after 6 months)

  • Go through the initial setup of Android disconnected from wifi or cell networks. Don't even put your SIM card in.
  • Disable and uninstall all the apps you don't intend to use, and anything that's manufacturer-provided bloatware. Use adb from your PC to uninstall these packages.
  • Install NetGuard via adb. Enable it, set it to "whitelist" mode, and only allow apps to access the network that you trust. Kick the developer a few bucks if you want the advanced features, but it's fully usable without them.
  • Install F-Droid via adb. This is now your app store - it only shows free and open source software.
  • Install Signal via adb. It's not on F-Droid but will notify you when there are updates.
  • You're now allowed to connect to wifi, complete the system updates (you may have to temporarily disable NetGuard for that), and install other software.
If the above seems too daunting, then use whatever smartphone you want, but only use it as a communication tool, keep it up to date, don't install any apps, put tape over the cameras, don't under any circumstances use it for web browsing/media/maps etc, and turn it off and remove the battery (or stick it in a Faraday cage) when you don't absolutely need it.

The other option is a device running GrapheneOS, an Android-based operating system with additional hardening. You will have to purchase and manually reinstall the operating system on your pick of the supported devices. This is usually the most recent models of Google Pixel devices. Unfortunately, they do not have removable batteries, so you must also purchase something like a small pelican case that you can turn into a faraday cage lined with foam to neutralize the microphones.

Basically, you can't make modern smartphones private devices. You're better off avoiding them, placing them in locations where they can't listen to you or your other devices, and not entrusting them with any identifiable data.

Regular Computers

As with security, compartmentalize. Make sure your primary computer where you do your day-to-day tasks is running open source software that you trust. Keep it up to date and enable firewalls and security systems. Don't install any third-party programs that aren't also open source.

If you don't have or cannot obtain a second computer, you can have another computer as an application inside your computer via a program called VirtualBox. VirtualBox is a program that can run multiple full operating systems on Virtual Machines like a program on your computer. On Fedora and Ubuntu Linux systems, you can use software called Boxes to do the same thing, but faster and easier. If you want Windows in a virtual machine, obtain Windows installation media from Microsoft, go through the offline install of Windows in VirtualBox. This virtual computer will be your "sandbox".

Important note - you should not make your trustworthy system a virtual machine that resides on the untrustworthy system. The host system can view and manipulate the contents of the virtual machine. The host system should be trustworthy.

Virtual Machines use Virtual Hard Drives, and with that comes the concept of "snapshots". You can save the state of the virtual machine at any point in time, continue working, and if you decide to revert your changes, you can from any point in the future. When you complete the install and do all the updates, create a snapshot. It will be the "base" state. From here, any time you need to do something like open a questionable file, visit a sketchy website, or run a program that isn't trustworthy, you do it from within the virtual machine, and revert to the snapshot when you finish, to completely erase the evidence, or ill effects of any damage. If it's a program that requires access to a hardware device (say, iTunes for syncing an iPod) you can "pass through" the USB devices to the virtual machine.

The super-advanced way to do real compartmentalization is Qubes. It's what Edward Snowden uses. For most people, it's overkill on security and not usable enough.

Software

Software starts with the Operating System of your computer. Your OS should be trustworth and open-source to ensure it has no back doors and no spying built in. Use only Fedora, Ubuntu, or Debian as a normal computer OS. Don't install any software that's not included in the system's repositories. On a USB stick, you should keep a copy of Tails OS and use it as frequently as possible. During installation of your OS, encrypt the drive with a long passphrase.

Web Browsing

Modern computing is done mostly through the browser. It is necessary to keep your browser secure and up to date. Sadly, the safest browsers to use are the mainstream ones, so pick between Firefox and Chromium. Do not use smaller hobby projects like Waterfox or Pale Moon as they do not get timely security updates. Do not use Chrome. It cannot be treated as private as it is not Open Source. Chromium, the open source project on which Chrome is based, is a sensible choice on Linux as well. It must be configured properly to not interact with Google - install it, remove your network connections, and turn off all the Google bits in settings and chrome://flags.

Browsing the web in the modern world is not an activity that is secure by default. The reason most technical users block ads is not because they are opposed to seeing the ads - ads these days include embedded programs that track your browsing and behavior around the Internet, and can compromise security even on reputable pages, like big mainstream news sites.

Good browser add-ons, in order of importance, are below.

  • uBlock Origin, and not "uBlock" (a knockoff that does not keep you safe). In uBlock Origin's settings, enable as many of the filter lists as you feel comfortable. The default set is fine, but more is better.
  • HTTPS Everywhere encrypts all eligible sites and warns you when visiting an unencrypted non-HTTPS site. (Keep in mind HTTPS does not mean "trustworthy", it just means the page you see is verifiably the same page that the website put up, and there is no "man in the middle". Bad actors can have HTTPS too.)
  • Clean URLs strips tracking content appended to URLs and generally makes links more shareable.
  • Privacy Possum attempts to break commercial tracking and ad requests. Generally a good thing to have but not necessary.
Generally running with these on all the time will not break any websites. If sites do break, or ask you to turn off your adblocker, disabling JavaScript on those sites with uBlock Origin will fix that issue. In Firefox, make sure the uBlock Origin add-on is enabled when in Private Mode as well. More advanced users can try out the venerable NoScript which disables JavaScript (programs) from running in your browser. While the above add-ons will not break the functionality of websites, NoScript will. You've been warned.

Make liberal use of Private Browsing/Incognito Mode - it is useful for keeping sessions separate. Clear cookies and browsing data regularly. History is useful to have around, but cache, cookies, and active logins can be cleared frequently.

Tor Browser deserves a mention as well. It's good to separate out personally identifiable tasks (personal email, banking, paying bills) from your "anonymous" activity (general web browsing where you're not logging into anything). Do the anonymous activity in Tor Browser. Don't change any settings in Tor Browser, ever, and keep it up to date.

Passwords

Use an open-source password manager for storing passwords, like KeePassXC, a fully offline file-based password manager. This is non-negotiable, as proprietary solutions, especially cloud-based solutions like LastPass or 1Password, cannot be verifiably secure or private. To maintain security, your passwords must be accessible only to those trusted with the information they unlock. Remember, your password manager contains a list of all the sites you have logins for, with their users and passwords, so it's very dangerous to leave insecure.

Lock the password database with a strong passphrase. The only passwords you should remember are the three separate passphrases to decrypt your operating system's boot disk, log into your user account, and unlock your password manager. They should all be different.

Use different randomly generated passwords, or diceware passphrases, for every single online account you wish to keep secure. KeePassXC and Bitwarden can generate these for you.

Use two-factor authentication (2FA) for every account. Text-message based 2FA is not secure and should not be used. KeePassXC can also generate standardized TOTP 2FA codes. Smartphone apps can be used to generate 2FA code as as well, but these are less trustworthy systems.

Communication

The most important aspect of your communication is the metadata - the data about the data. The contents of your messages are less important than who you send them to, when you send them, and where you send them from. Good messaging systems should obscure that content as well. Use Signal, Briar, Matrix, XMPP+OMEMO.

Email

Email is common, but you can't make it truly private. Email can be read by the provider (Google, Microsoft, etc). At some point you will use email, so make it at least a litte more secure: use an email client like Thunderbird to store your emails offline. Email clients can be configured to both display and send plaintext email (no HTML formatting) and not download contents of emails from remote servers, unlike webmail. HTML-format email can download remote images or other files to trick you and can potentially run code on your computer; it's an attack vector you should close off. Email can be encrypted with PGP, but the complexity and drawbacks make it not worth it.

Phones

Chat clients, like WhatsApp, Telegram, and Signal, all require a smartphone, and should be used with hesitation, only in tandem with an appropriately secured smartphone. Any client requiring a phone number can potentially be hijacked by a bad actor - phone numbers are easy to steal. Signal is the only provider that guards against this with registration lock, so they're safe to use. Things like Telegram, WhatsApp, and other messengers should not be treated as secure. The service providers can view and alter the contents of the messages.

Phone calls and regular SMS text messages are not secure. Anyone with technical know-how and about $500 worth of equipment (not only governments and police) can intercept phone calls and text messages.

Matrix and Signal

Signal is a drop-in replacement for messaging and phone calls. It can do video calls, too. Everything is encrypted and secure, and it's incredibly easy to set up and use. The desktop application can do voice/video calls now too, but it must be linked to the smartphone in order to do so. Once that is done, the smartphone can be turned off and you can still use Signal on the desktop, unlike something like WhatsApp Web.

A better option than Signal is Matrix. It does not require a phone to use, which is why I recommend it over Signal. It is a decentralized system like email, but it's more like chat than serialized messages. The best client for Matrix is Element. A simpler client for smartphones is fluffychat. You can pick any server or host your own, but the default is just using the standard Matrix.org home server. The trick is getting those you communicate with to sign up as well, and since Signal is easier, has less opportunity for mistakes, and doesn't make normal people think about encryption keys, it's probably a better option for most.

Smartphones

I know I went over the hardware and OS setup earlier, but smartphones deserve their own section.

Whether Android or iOS, fundamentally, these devices cannot be controlled. They are automatically updated by the carriers, notable bad actors, and the user is given so little access to the system such that appropriate lockdown cannot be done. While iPhones sit in a much better position than Android devices, both are systems that should not be considered trustworthy.

However, people like to talk on the phone. People like to use messaging apps. In order to do this securely, you'll likely have to change your behavior regarding your smartphone. Here are a few general tips.

  • Only buy smartphones from notable good manufacturers that have a history of supporting devices with prompt security patches for a long time. This essentially means only buy Apple iPhones or Google Pixel devices. Samsung's Galaxy S and Galaxy Note lines are runners-up. Buying last year's model refurbished is a good option to save a buck.
  • Download as few third-party apps as possible. Whatever you do download should be verifiably open source. Smartphone apps (especially on Android) can escape their sandbox and steal your data, or trick you into doing things you should not. Most people should only have one or two apps aside from what is pre-installed. At any given time, your smartphone's data should be an acceptable loss.
  • Use the phone as little as possible. Smartphones can usually share their data connection via USB or Wi-Fi to a real computer. You are better off bringing a secured laptop with you and tethering it from your smartphone for mobile computing.
  • Only plug the phone into known-good cables and chargers. Harmless-looking cables can snoop on your device. Furthermore, only plug your phone into your charger - don't use a computer's USB port to charge.
  • Messaging apps that require a cell phone number should be carefully vetted. If you are being targeted, it is trivially easy for an adversary to steal your phone number and pretend to be you, so make sure to use something like Signal that has registration lock. Use services where you sign up with a username and password.
  • Don't use social media or email on the phone, and don't log into accounts unless they require the phone app as a companion (i.e. Signal). Don't put your password manager on the phone either.
  • Turn the phone off and leave it in another room away from your secure computer when it's not being used.
  • Do not use biometric authentication (fingerprint, face unlock) because they're "passwords" that are easy to steal and can't be changed.

Written out, these guidelines are simple enough, but likely requires a massive change in your behavior. However, examine the list and think about how close it is to your life pre-smartphone. Probably pretty close, right? It all boils down to "just use the phone as a communication tool, not a computer".

Cloud Services

Cloud services like Apple iCloud, Microsoft's OneDrive, and Dropbox should not be used the way they are intended. Normally, the user uploads visible, unencrypted "cleartext" data to the cloud, either to use on other devices or to have a backup. This enables the service provider to view the data put on their service, and this is not secure. For the security-minded individual, data should be fully encrypted with a key controlled by you, and only then should the encrypted data be sent to cloud services.

Obviously, the automatic backup and content upload (iCloud/Google Photos, iCloud/Google Drive, etc.) pushed on users by mobile operating systems should be disabled as well. Every "cloud" option should be off.

Services like Tarsnap do this automatically with an open-source client program, thereby proving themselves trustworthy.

Good tools to encrypt data, as mentioned in the article linked above, are 7-zip, VeraCrypt, and Microsoft's BitLocker. If you are using Windows 10 Home, you should upgrade to Windows 10 Pro to use encryption features. Microsoft does not allow Home users to be secure.

If data just needs to be synchronized between a few endpoints, Syncthing is an end-to-end synchronization program that only sends data between your devices without involving external servers.

Other Smart Devices (IoT)

Remember, the "S" in "IoT" stands for Security.

Do not use IoT devices, like Amazon Alexa, Apple's Homepod, the Apple TV, Roku and Chromecast devices, lights that connect to your wifi, and other "appliance"-type computers. (Raspberry Pis and similar devices are acceptable.)

This includes any device that connects to your network that a) does not run 100% Open Source software, and b) you can't get root access on. If the answer to these questions is not clear, don't use the device.

Etc

Avoid VPNs, because you likely don't need it. You're better off using Tor in most cases.

This goes without saying - do your updates, and don't delay them. Pick a point every week to manually do updates. This way you'll never be surprised.

Keep backups on encrypted media.

Extreme Paranoia

Instead of a normal Linux system, use an encrypted install of Qubes or OpenBSD as your operating system. Better yet, do not install an operating system on your internal disk, and use Tails on an encrypted USB thumb drive that you keep with you at all times.

Only use Open Source/Free Software. Only use secure, encrypted, third-party-audited protocols.

Only keep sensitive data on a completely offline PC. Only transfer data to and from the system by burning a CD or DVD. The security enhancements done by minifree (sadly now defunct) provide a great set of guidelines for more adventurous users. USB as a protocol is too dangerous - fill those USB ports with hot glue or de-solder them from the motherboard.

The End...

Hopefully this guide provides a good set of tools to assist you in keeping your digital life little more secure. You don't have to implement every single step in this guide to achieve decent security, but what you should now have is a setup that was better than before. Once you've learned to live without the convenience that you get from an insecure setup, come back here, and try to add another later to your armor. Seek out other resources as well, since I don't have the answers to everything.

So, while I may have nothing left to say here, this is just the beginning for you.
