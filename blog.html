New Laptop

[link—standalone]

After years of crappy craigslist machines and dying batteries, I finally decided to purchase myself a new laptop. (Yes, I did get a new-to-me T430 a few months ago, but that was free, and the battery doesn't work). I decided that since I was going to give money to a company, it should be a company that makes good things and cares about freedom. And, I couldn't buy a laptop and not have coreboot at this point.

I bought a System76 Lemur Pro. It is a lightweight 14-inch black laptop that is about the same size as my 12-inch X230. It has 8GB of RAM (with an extra slot available), a 10th-gen i5 U-series CPU, an Intel AC Wi-Fi card, and 2 NVMe drive bays. The embedded controller (the chip that handles keyboard and touchpad events to name a few) and the BIOS are all open source. They use Coreboot with UEFI.

Details out of the way, here we go. First, I had to open it up and add my extra NVMe drive. There's 12 phillips screws on the bottom. No tricks, it just pops off. 12 screws is a lot but I won't be doing that often. Second, I plugged in its power adapter. There's a light next to the plug that glows orange when charging, and green when charged. It also blinks green when the laptop sleeps. It can also charge via USB-C.

Opening the lid is not possible with one hand but the hinge is pleasant and the lid doesn't bounce much. The laptop has no logos internally, and the external logo is a sticker that I peeled off immediately, given it would probably have come off in my backpack. So, this is the matte-black no-branding laptop of my dreams.

The display is the first thing anyone notices. The bezels are slim, there's a webcam/mics up top, and the display is a matte 1080p IPS panel. Perfect for the size. It's clear, sharp, and gets both really bright and dim.

The touchpad is a relatively decent touchpad. It tracks well and clicks from almost anywhere on the touchpad. It's almost as good as my 2012 Macbook. The keyboard is good, but has much less travel than I'm used to on a thinkpad or desktop mechanical keyboard. It's backlit with 5 levels of backlighting the lowest being very low, which is great. The keyboard does not have a caps lock indicator light, which is fine-ish (I either remap or don't use caps lock anyway).

Given the port situation on many modern lapops I wasn't expecting Thinkpad-levels of connectivity, but it comes really close. No ethernet, but if you want that, System76 has a Galago Pro model for you. Otherwise the two models are quite similar (the Galago just has less battery and more ports).

Pop!_OS, System76's operating system, is installed on the drive in OEM mode, and lets you install to the rest of the disk when you boot up. It thoughfully provides a recovery partition, so if you want to blow away your install and start fresh, you don't have to boot from USB. I first ran the installer in attempt to put it on the smaller, secondary disk, but it instead put it on the first disk (during install I selected the smaller one, but an 'lsblk' showed it had set up the install targets on the larger one). (Possibly human error?) I download the ISO from their site, installed it to my second 256GB disk, and on the main 500GB disk, I put Fedora (my OS of choice).

Fedora works great on this machine. No complaints, no dmesg errors, no extra drivers, no weird fan noises. I run the sway window manager primarily (and GNOME3 when I need something more full-featured) - the only change over my previous install was I had to install the "light" package, which controls backlight a la xbacklight on xorg.

Overall, this is a fantastic laptop on which to run GNU/Linux. It was a bit expensive, but I couldn't imagine putting money toward a better purpose - a small black brandless laptop running coreboot and Linux. Only thing it's missing is a trackpoint. It almost fills the Thinkpad-sized hole in my heart!

Automate These Things

[link—standalone]
Friction in daily life is not good. Automation alleviates some friction, reducing stress, and creating more time for leisure and creativity. Many things can be automated. I have some opinions on what should ( and should not) be automated.
Here are a bunch of tasks where I've attmpted to reduce friction by automation.
  • Phone, text, email, and other alerts. I've set up filters, rules, and alerts that only really ping me when I want to be pinged.
  • News. My RSS reader (liferea) refreshes when I open it every morning, and I maintain a set of curated and low-volume feeds. I don't get caught up in infinite scroll and only see what I want.
  • Operating system updates. Follow this guide and it's pretty simple. On my servers, unattended-upgrades takes care of security updates, letting me choose when to apply the "regular" updates. My laptop running Fedora automatically downloads updates with dnf-automatic and lets me choose when to install them. Both OSs are very stable and haven't really ever broken irreparably on me, so I don't have to think about my computers much. Whenever they ask me to update, I do it, and it never takes more than 5 minutes and a normal reboot.
  • Backups. Once configured, backups should run forever and be reliable. I run Syncthing and Duplicity separately and back up to two locations. One is stored on a RAID, which is backed up to cold storage quarterly. My "cold storage" backups are not automated, but those I like to do on purpose, and they are stored in a secure location, locked up.
  • Software builds in testing/non-production environments. If you want testbox.local to have the latest version of your app every single day without you having to do it, Jenkins the heck out of it!
  • Monitoring and alerting. Cacti and Twilio make a great combo. Grafana and Nagios are also good monitoring applications.
  • OS installation. Chef, Ansible, JuJu, and Kickstart all provide GREAT ways to get a new box up and running without having to manually install software, partition your disk, or set up your network adapters.
Of course, automating things is almost as important as what NOT to automate. Some things need human intervention and oversight, for now.
  • Physical security. This includes your garage door. For obvious reasons, this shouldn't be a system that's easily fooled into unlocking itself while you're not at home. Presence detection via Bluetooth is sketchy and spoofable. If someone hacks your AD Domain Controller, you don't want a badge auto-provisioned and mailed to an address, and you don't want all your worker's badges not working if you screw up and delete an OU.
  • Cooking. Don't play with fire, kids!
  • Major software or operating system upgrades. Don't let your computers surprise you with a broken NTP service or 100% CPU utilization while you're on vacation.
  • Environmental changes. At my last job we had several air handlers that would rotate every Wednesday at 9AM. This frequently caused problems and interrupted someone's work day. It should have been a monthly (if that) process that was performed manually. Heating and cooling should operate within thresholds, but not make drastic changes without alerting you. A blown sensor could result in a 125F server room and you're none the wiser.
Of course, this non-exhaustive list is just my opinion based on experience. There are probably good arguments, and I'd love to hear them.

Toying with macOS

[link—standalone]

A mini-review of macOS from the perspective of a Linux user


A few years ago when I switched to using Linux on the desktop full-time, it was definitely not as complete and usable a desktop computing experience as the Mac. Windows 10 was easily worse (in the early days; it's become a little better, if you use the LTSC version) and the Mac was fantastic. I started using Linux more seriously after macOS 10.10 (the one with the iOS 7-like blurry flat redesign came out) and switched to it full time a couple years later. 10.9 was peak macOS for me, and it just got harder for me to stomach after that. Maximizing got taken away; I discovered tiling window managers and loved them, and even Windows has that; more settings got jumbled for no reason; iCloud became practically manadtory or forced in half the apps, and I didn't want to use it; and obviously I got super silly paranoid.
I've always kept a few spare laptops around (when I was using macOS full time, I kept a spare Windows machine for old games), and since 2017 the Mac has been in the pile of spares. I pulled it out since I'm going to be using a Mac for work soon, and wanted to see how it works nowadays.
I would give kudos to Apple for supporting recent macOS versions on an almost 8-year-old machine, but I regularly use the latest versions of Fedora and Ubuntu on machines that are older than that, and they perform better, so that's moot.
It does perform surprisingly well on a standard 750GB HDD, and save for long boot times, operates as smoothly as it does on an SSD. It's very cool and quiet, has decent battery life, charges fast, doesn't stutter and operates well doing daily tasks. All the hardware works and it has a plethora of ports, a little LED battery meter on the case, and a DVD drive in a very compact package. It's not really all that impressive, since that's the pretty low bar I expect things to clear, but it gets the basics right.
The GUI is fine. I really do like tiling and workspaces, and the Mac is okay at those. I have to use the trackpad instead of the keyboard, but I assume there are decent keyboard shortcuts I've not looked up for things like "go to workspace n" or "move application to workspace n". I hear it has picture-in-picture, but have not tried it yet. I wonder if I could do that with a terminal instead of a video. Overall, it's less configurable than GNOME3, even, and that's saying something. Every little app costs money (things that are just normal GNOME extensions or built-in features) and I don't want to pay for something that might not be good, or might be spyware.
The worst part is the lack of package management. I rely HEAVILY on dnf/apt/flatpak/snap to do automatic updates for all my programs/system at once, and lacking a good package manager built-in is a huge flaw of the platform. I've installed Homebrew since it's what I remember using prior, and it's still good. It'll take some getting used to. Nice to have an open-source way to install and update things like keepassxc, wireshark, ffmpeg, tmux, etc.
The best part is that it's a Unix system (I know this!) and the terminal application is REALLY GOOD. They've switched to ZSH by default (seriously? Why?) but it's easy to switch back to bash or fish. I could probably install a plethora of ncurses apps on a VPS and live in an SSH connection and be perfectly happy with macOS.
Conclusion: I'm a Free Software diehard, so I'll keep using Ubuntu/Debian/Fedora forever, but macOS is good if you don't have the option to use Desktop Linux. Since I don't have to pay for the inevitable keyboard repair (it being a work computer), I guess I'll be content with macOS!

Coreboot on the T430

[link—standalone]

Traditional computer BIOS programs are either spyware, featurless, or horrifically out of date. A few problems are evident with these systems:

  • The manufacturer never updates the BIOS and never cared about it. Critical features are missing and bugs are present.
  • The manufacturer injects programs or other spyware into the BIOS to change your operating system.
  • The manufacturer's BIOS either has glaring security issues or requrires specific versions of Windows to update, therefore rendering it unsuitable for use.

In order to solve these problems, you need a different BIOS. The manufactureres like to prevent modifications, but since most chips can just be read/flashed externally, that's not a huge problem if you're willing to do a lot of work for your freedom and security.

Coreboot is one of my favorite open soucre projects. It's a free replacement to commercial BIOS/UEFI systems. I've got it on my X220, X230 (courtesy of the Skulls project), and now the T430! In order to flash the T430, it needs to be totally taken apart. Since I just got a brand-new T430 and was going to repaste it and clean it anyway, I decided to flash Coreboot on it as well. In for a penny...

Anyway, now I have a coreboot family. Yay!

Special thanks to this blog bpost and of course all the guides out there for the T420 and X230. Splitting the flash into 2 separate chips was the trickiest part, along with figuring out which way to align the clip. Your mileage may vary with all that. My chips were different from his.

Amazon Story

[link—standalone] The Amazon Story I tell this little tale to my friends and family about how I got wrapped up in the whole technological privacy movement. I cared a little bit before, but mostly only from a technical interest perspective. Now I'm concerned and draw a hard line on this stuff. Here's why! This Wikipedia link has pictures of most models of Echo and a more detailed and objective set of descriptions for each.
2007 - Amazon launches the Kindle. This is widely seen as a good move. They still make these things and hardcore readers love them. I don't need to explain why Kindles (the e-paper black and white ones) are good.
2011 - In Feburary, Borders, a popular bookstore chain, files for bankrupcy. Amazon's eaten their lunch.
2011 - In September, Amazon launches the Kindle Fire, a tablet that's much cheaper (and smaller) than an iPad. Amazon markets it primarily as a way to read books (odd, given that half their Kindle marketing favorably compares the e-ink antiglare Kindle screen to the backlit LED glossy glare-prone tablet screens of the day) and it's a pretty good seller.
Shortly before the launch of this tablet, Apple decides that Amazon's Kindle app can no longer link to their ebook store, making the iPad a much worse choice for Amazon purchasers. These tablets are also still made to this day, having gone through several iterations with various features - high quality HD displays, good speakers, different sizes and aspect ratios, and enormous bubbly silicone cases for child-proofing. They are still marketed as reading devices, though they can also perform the function of an Echo, and they make it very easy to buy Amazon stuff - their primary function.
This was the beginning of Amazon's modern electronics manufaturing era. The Kindle still seems like a side business, but the "Fire" line and the Echos are more in line with Modern Amazon.

2014 - The Echo launches. It is a large black paper-towel-sized tube that is plugged into a wall outlet, connected to WiFi, and listens for commands on its highly sensitive seven-microphone array. It's basically an Orwellian telescreen, and people paid $150 for the privilege of being spied on. People said, "Okay."
Amazon said, "Okay!"


2016 - The Echo Dot launches. It's an Echo but much shorter (think hockey puck-sized), cheaper, and with a worse speaker. Amazon practically gives them away in frequent sales and bundles. They want one in every room in your house so you can always have a chance to ask Alexa (the name of the voice that talks from the tube/puck) to buy you paper towels or tell you how to make chicken soup.
People said, "Okay."
Amazon said, "Okay!"
(At the same time, the "Amazon Tap", a battery-powered bluetooth speaker version of the Echo, launches. It's not nearly as popular, but now you can take your spyware on the go. It just wasn't as useful.)

2017 - The Echo Look launches. It is an Echo (micrphones, wall power, WiFi) with a camera built in that Amazon tells you to place in your closet so it can watch you dress. It will offer outfit suggestions (and purchasing suggestions via Amazon.com) to complement your style. Most men are creeped out, because most men don't care about fasion. Women see a use for it.
People said, "That's a little creepy, but okay."
Amazon said, "Okay!"

2017 - Also in 2017, the Echo Show launches. It is an echo, but with a 7" tablet screen and a webcam. It cannot be used as a tablet since it has no battery, and requires wall power. The headliner feature was "Drop In", which enabled Echo Show owners to video call each other, but it doesn't ring, it just automatically starts the call. You can "Drop In" to your friend's kitchen at any time. This insane behavior was default-on.
People said, "That's super creepy, also why not just plug in your tablet and yell at it?" But it was still purchased and is a successful product.
Amazon said, "Okay, we'll roll back the Drop In feature and turn it off by default."
People accepted this and Amazon was not lynched that day.
2017 - Amazon buys Whole Foods in June.
2017 - The Echo Spot was released in September. It is a small sphere with the front chopped off and it looks like a tiny cute alarm clock. It is a miniature Echo Show, with a 2-inch screen that gets super dim. It is meant to be used as a bedside alarm clock, with a camera and microphones that you point at your bed.
Surprisingly no one found issue with this.
People said, "Okay."
Amazon said, "Okay!"

2017 - The nightmare never ends - in December 2017, Amazon partnered with various IOT Video Camera, Doorbell, and Door Lock manufacturers (several of which it later purchsed) to introduce Amazon Key, a service that lets Amazon delivery drivers open your door and throw your package inside your house.
A service that lets Amazon delivery drivers (NOT background-checked US Postal Service/UPS/FedEx employees, but regular folks with a station wagon and a smartphone app) enter your home.
People said, "Okay, hang on a second. This is SUPER creepy."
Amazon said, "Yeah, I know. I heard you talking about it this morning on the Echo in the bathroom as I watched you put on those new boots from the Echo Look that you bought from the Amazon app while you were laying in bed last night - also watched that on your Echo Spot. You snore a lot. I'll show you a suggestion for breathe right strips next time you're in the app."

Do you want any of this in your life? Other companies have only followed suit.
I won't tell you what to do, but know that in 2014 when these things started happening, I had already made my decision.

Ubuntu: Leaving Trusty

[link—standalone] As always, posts like these are always to be taken with a few handfuls of salt - they are basically a log of my own trials and tribulations for my own future perusal.
Trusty Tahr, Ubuntu 14.04, ends support next month. Here's what I've learned from the upgrade process, and tips on going to the next release, 16.04 Xenial Xerus.

First of all, for in-place upgrades, you need to go to 16.04 before you go to 18.04. That's the official in-place upgrade process, and it takes a little longer, but you're better off not trying to attach a disk and upgrade from there - it should be possible but you should go the supported way. 16.04 will be around for another two years, so if that's all you need, it's not a bad system to be on.

-Server or Desktop?
Let's agree a desktop is a non-critical user's terminal into network services. If your desktop computer (ATX motherboard in a normal consumer box) is running all the time and running high-uptime services, consider either a) transitioning that service to a server machine, or b) transitioning the box to a server role and using a different system as your desktop.
Desktop systems are either a) laptops that can be offline for long periods of time or b) desk- or floor-based boxes that are not perfomring any critical services provided to other users. These systems should be upgraded fairly frequently to keep up with the latest security patches and technologies. User-operated machines have the largest attack surface (the user, and the keyboard/monitor/mouse) and are important to protect.
All systems I run and administer at this point are on a later desktop OS. This was not an issue at this point.
Servers - find out when an acceptable time exists to turn the server off and break the service for users. It would be ideal to either have a redundant system. If that is not possible, inform the users of a time you're going to take action, and do it. You can't put this off and they have to understand these things need to happen.

-Pre-upgrade Steps
Backup backup backup! Make sure you have copies of all the relevant data from the system easily and readily restorable. For virtual systems, copying the virtual hard drive while the virtual machine is off to a different location on the same host is a good option. Snapshots, provided your systems can work together well, are also a great idea. Win2008R2 does not snapshot Linux machines very well. The snapshots only re-merge while the machine is off, so take that into consideration. VMWare is more Linux-aware, and later versions of Hyper-V (2012R2 and 2016) are very Linux-friendly.
If you cannot back up the system easily, and you don't necessarily know 100% of what is on it, copy /home, /etc, /opt, /var, and any directories that are common for your use case (i.e. if your organization places lots of custom scripts in /usr/local/bin, grab those). Tar them and copy to another machine. Your machine that you're performing the upgrade on is a good bet, if your connection is solid. Without grabbing the entire system, that should conain most of what you need.
Take an output from the package manager (apt list --installed) and look at what package sources are enabled (save the files /etc/apt/sources.list and the directory /etc/apt/sources.list.d/). Go through the output and resolve any inconsistencies before you move on - i.e. if there are old, custom, local-installed packages, make sure you note that and attempt to find the .deb files.
If you have redundancy, point any DNS aliases (box1.service.example.com) at your redundant system to it can take the load and your service doesn't break. That's always a bonus.
Check network interfaces (ifconfig) and MAC addresses if you are rebuilding. Make sure to record that information before you move on.
Check what's running in cron before you leave this old box too.

-Rebuild or Upgrade?
First, determine if your server performs a complex enough role that it needs to be rebuilt. Ubuntu's release upgrader is good enough to where it will ask you what to do with your old configs, but often for newer package versions,
If you are upgrading in-place: update to the latest version of the OS. apt update, apt upgrade, apt dist-upgrade, and apt autoremove. Reboot.
The upgrader will bomb out if you don't have enough space in /boot. You can always find what older kernels are on your system (dpkg -l |grep ^linux-image) and remove what's taking up space. Usually, it's good practice to keep the last two or three known-good old kernels around post-upgrade. That being said, if you're running just fine for weeks on 4.4.0-134, you can throw caution to the wind and remove all but that.

-The Upgrade
Ubuntu documentation starts here. Make sure to do your own research too.
Upgrading is complicated but is mostly handled for you. After you've run through apt dist-upgrade and rebooted, run do-release-upgrade. If you have access to run the upgrade through the hypervisor console, that's helpful - reconnections are easier. If many people access the console on a regular basis, don't do this - you could get interrupted. If you're running over SSH, use Screen/Tmux/Byobu if you want. Easier to reconnect that way.
It will ask you to restart services while running - that's fine; you've blocked out this time to break services anyway. It will show you what packages are being removed/kept/upgraded. This is helpful! Capture this output and save it for future reference, in case something goes wrong.
The upgrade from here on out is simple. The questions you get are along the lines of "should I take the config that is from the new package, or keep your old config?". If you have customized configs for something (say vsftpd or apt or smtpd) you should have done research about new configs and compatibility with your customized config of an old version. Usually these are fine to keep the old ones (with your changes for your environment), but take care to watch after and make sure things don't break.
After upgrading, several packages are listed for removal. That's fine - do that, and then let it reboot the system.
Check to make sure everything's up and running. Tail the logs for your services, and check dmesg. All good? Yay! Nope? Fix it. If you can't fix it, revert. That's why we back up.

-The Rebuild
This is much simpler. Roll out a new machine over top of the old one. Set up its networking, install packages, copy over home directories and custom script files, write in your old configs (don't just drop them in there - you are usually better off adding the custom lines to the new configs in the new format).
Right now, you have an opportunity to rebuild the system how you see fit. The less changes you make from stock, the easier you make it on yourself or anyone else in the future, so remember that and embrace minimal tweaking. On desktop systems, tweak away, but on servers, remember that you have to share the machine and that it must be easy to maintain by others.

-My Experiences
SMTPD
This one was very standard. I upgrade 4 mail relays. They didn't require any manual after-the-fact tweaking post-upgrade and took 16.04 like a champ.
apt-mirror
I have an apt-mirror server to serve packages to other servers behind a firewall in an isolated environment. Getting this server upgraded was easy, and again, took no messing about. I had to add more disk space to the /var disk and extend it, becuase I was going to begin mirroring 16.04 as well. This blog post helped me SO MUCH in getting this server ready to serve upgrades for other machines, though. It's impossible to run a do-release-upgrade normally if you can't hit archive.ubuntu.com, but if you mirror the right parts, in the right places, you can definitely do it right.
Cacti
HAHAHAHAAHA NO
But seriously - cacti 0.8.8f, in 16.04, works fine with PHP5, but not with PHP7, which is the only PHP in 16.04. Also, RRDTool, the backbone for cacti, is upgraded to 1.5 in 16.04, but cacti 0.8.8f only supports RRDTool 1.4.
This was a serious mess. We ended up installing an unsupported version of Cacti from some other Ubuntu version (0.8.8g, the last of the 0.8.8 versions) from the archive.ubuntu.org pool. Where macOS (formerly Mac OS X) excels in the aspect is settings configuration. For every application there is a global menu, and the Cmd+, shortcut works to open preferences in every default (and most third-party) applications. In each application there is a menu bar item called "Help", in which one can search the entire rest of the menus for commands and command shortcuts. This is, without a doubt, the best feature for newcomers to macOS - the ability to find any action, in any application, anywhere.

System preferences are in one location, which is nice - Windows 10's current mess of half-control-panel-half-modern-settings-app is a far cry from what Control Panel was in Windows 7. Many GNU/Linux desktop environments get it right as well - Gnome3, Cinnamon, and KDE Plasma all have great settings applications that are searchable and efficiently categorized.

------

My main complaint with macOS is application installation. There are too many different ways to perform this simple, regular, and necessary action.

In Windows, an installer is either a .exe or .msi, and both generally walk the user through a wizard with options. The installer can be deleted when finished, and the installed program shows up in a list of all the installed programs in a system settings window, where one would expect.

Many options available on GNU/Linux are even better. Package managers offer a command-line solution to application, library, and component acquisition - often, it's as simple as 'dnf in mpv', or 'pacman -S rhythmbox'. Dependency resolution is automatic (if you've ever seen a dialog pop up during a windows installation complaining about the lack of a .NET framework, this is a huge benefit!) and upgrades of all your system's programs at once is seamless.

In Apple's operating system, we'll break down application acquisition into five sections - Mac App Store, .zip file, .dmg file, .pkg file, and 'other'. 1. The Mac App Store (MAS) is the easiest way to obtain applications. Most users would likely prefer to go this route. However, many developers complain about the lack of control they have in the App Store, how sandboxing is an arbitrary requirement that doesn't really even work, how some updates may just get rejected for unclear reasons, and how long things take to be processed. Many applications (for now; this is changing very soon) like Microsoft Office and the Adobe suite aren't distributed through the MAS either. The revenue model is very greedy, the yearly fees are high for smaller developers, and many quality apps that do alter your system in helpful-but-non-sanctioned ways aren't available on the MAS. Development tools are often left out because of the control they require but can't get. 2. Many applications are distributed in .zip files. This is incredibly efficient, but messy - macOS applications can be entirely a single file, and this is quite nice. The file is a container file for other files, but is viewed by the shell as a launchable program. Many users will unzip an application download in their ~/Downloads folder, leave both files there, and run the application from there. This makes no difference to the system - the appropriate ~/Library folders are still put in place and all configs are stored there - but it creates a messy system filled with unnecessary data, especially with multiple users in mind. (Windows applications can come distributed in .zip files too, but this is much less common, and usually reserved for an alternatively distributed "portable" version that stores its configs in the same folder.) 3. Disk Images (.dmg files) are more or less the exact same. They do, however, come with the built-in ability to customize the background and include a shortcut to /Applications, which many do, in order to hint the user into doing the right thing. This is the case with many advanced or savvy users, but many inexperienced users simply launch the program from the disk image. Again, this is inefficient - effectively they're running a compressed application, and it will perform poorly. Many applications complain about being launched from a disk image. Some even prevent their being launched and instead serve a message about copying a file to their computer, which further confuses users - from their perspective, it's already on their computer! I've seen many Macs with a desktop full of mounted drives running various versions of Chrome and other programs. 4. Packaged application installers are the best way to go. They are a traditional wizard used to place the program into the correct location, run scripts to create folders or place other utilities, or download helper programs to assist with first runs. These are much like Windows installers and generally easy enough to use, but will require administrator access to place files in /Applications. 5. I promised an "other" section so here it is. I did not forget about homebrew and other command-line package managers. They're quite useful and have important features like key checking and unified updating, like many GNU/Linux package managers do. These are not officially supported and often cause more trouble than they're worth.

macOS has come a long way since Mac OS X 10.0. This problem has remained endemic for as long as I can remember, and shows no signs of slowing down - if anything, it will likely fragment further. GNU/Linux has gained Snap, Flatpak, and Appimage in the last decade as well, and these are all in addition to the native package management system. Many of these options seem to try and solve a problem that most people just don't seem to have.

There's no feasible way for Apple to fix this themselves, other than moving applications to /Applications immediately when they're run - there are security implications to this though, and non-admin users wouldn't have the permissions. They've put themselves into a tangle and can't really remove any of the options. In a time gone by when installers seemed like bulk and cruft, and many users just clicked through them without reading anyway, the "download zip, extract, and place app in /Applications" method seemed revolutionary. But people are lazy and skip steps. Where there's an idiot-proof system, there will always be a dumber idiot that breaks it. Fri, 21 Feb 2020 06:46:21 -0800

It's pretty neat. There's not much to say - it's not an opinionated distro. That's my main problem with Ubuntu and others - they've got their logo plastered all over the OS and it's got their colorscheme everywhere. Debian and Fedora tend to be my favorites, but Fedora's "I'm not a rolling release seriously trust me plz" model is really weird to me. Plus dnf is sloooooooow.


Arch is mostly what I want. I'll probably dabble with Parabola because ~FREEDOM~, but I'm really enjoying a rolling release with both current packages and no opinionated branding. Arch is basically 100% upstream with no tweaks and no changes. It doesn't feel like a cohesive unit, but more like a minimal system with the option of adding a bunch of extra packages on top. The installation just requires you to partition things the way you want, and pretty much does the rest for you, with a ton of really useful tools along the way. Pacstrap is like a good version of a Gentoo stage3, wifi-menu is like an easy version of wpa_supplicant, and pacman is...
well....


Pacman is the one area where arch falls down. It's fast and configurable and really easy to work with, but man is it a little sticky. When using dnf to remove a package, it removes its unneeded dependency packages at the same time. Debian's apt will mark them as such, and you can 'autormeove' them later. Here's three commands that all do the same thing:


1) apt autoremove
2) dnf autoremove
3) pacman -R $(pacman -Qtdq)


Seriously, I don't think it'd be that hard to write a package manager with syntax that uses actual words instead of switches. That is my one major complaint about Arch. But hey, at least it's fast, mirrors are easy to switch between, and its keyring system is very simple to understand!

------


Anyway, Arch is a great distro. No SELinux or AppArmor by default, but that's fine. Nothing but the barebones, raw, lean, simple setup that you need, and then you can add what you want after. I'm a tentative convert. I know it's not a useful system to be using, but I've cut my teeth on Debian- and Fedora-based systems for a while now, and I think it's fine to use this as long as it doesn't get in my way. The minute I have to struggle with something simple like printing or nework config, it's going in the trash and I'm going back to Fedora or Debian. But using a full DE like Gnome3 (it's really really fast now!) mitigates any issue I have. I encourage any readers to try it, becuase it lets you do what you want, instead of dumping a full set of gnome-games on you (debian) or preinstalling an office suite and mail/chat/calendar programs you won't use.


I'll add a script later to get things set up, but getting a new Arch system running to satisfaction is really easy. Just a few pacman, useradd, and systmectl commands, and you've got a full usable system, with wifi, printing, bluetooth, power management, and all that.
