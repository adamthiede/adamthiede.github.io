You Bought Your Phone Incorrectly

[link—standalone]

Americans are bad at buying cell phones. Always have been, and seemingly always will be. It's not everyone's fault, though - circumstances enabled the carriers to strong-arm individuals into bizarre and incorrect behavior that otherwise wouldn't be even considered. Let me explain.

The USA is one big country, with mostly shared laws and infrastructure throughout the whole place. That's not a surprise. Also not a surprise: our economic system has enabled was about three or four cell networks to dominate the entire country. We have basically only three carriers. Verizon has over 40% market share. The rest of it is split between AT&T and T-Mobile. There is a small sliver of independent providers but they are shrinking and being gobbled up by the big three. Heck, there used to be four - where did Sprint go? (The un-startling answer is: Sprint was bought by T-Mobile!)

Because of a serious lack of economic regulation, carriers have engaged in downright predatory and anti-consumer behavior for as long as they've been around, and we've just had to put up with it. There are a few points I want to bring up.

Buying Phones From Carriers

This is like buying your car from Shell or your lamps from the power company. It's ridiculous behavior. Nobody should do this. The phones that carriers sell are never less expensive in the long run (because they always come with trade-offs, contracts, and mountains of undisclosed fees) and are always critically impaired in some way.
You there. Promise me next time you buy a phone, you'll buy it used, or you'll buy it from the manufacturer? Please? Do everyone a favor and stop enabling this abuse by the carriers. Here's why!

Software Configuration

Android phones often ship with loads of carrier bloatware to this day. Verizon Android phones ship with a default text tone that is from the BREW java OS flip phone era. I guess it's an audio-branding thing? They also have a "verizon backup" app, which is totally worthless, given that it costs money and is worse than Google's backups, which are still bad but whatever, at least they're reliable and free. Carriers don't stop the bloatware at their own software, either. They also accept money from companies who make absolutely degenerate mobile games, riddled with advertisements, slot-machine-like dark patterns, and hundreds of megabytes of spyware, and they just pack them on. Of course this only really applies to Android devices, and a little bit to Windows Phone and Blackberries, but those don't exist anymore. AT&T, whose logo conveniently resembles the Death Star, forced Apple, Samsung, and other device manufacturers to lie, and label 4G networks "5Ge" (the "e" stands for "evolution" (I debated what to say the "e" stands for here but realized the truth was funnier than any joke I could make up)) in the status bar where it shows you the signal strength. But lying about signal strength isn't anything new for these providers or handset manufacturers anyway... moving on.

Bootloader Locking

Verizon will sell you a downgraded Google Pixel that is exactly like a real Google Pixel in many ways, except for the only reason that anyone would actually buy a Google Pixel - the unlocked bootloader. In fact, many Verizon devices are bootloader-locked, preventing a user from installing his own software on a phone he owns. This becomes more important after devices fall out of support. (No, of course Verizon will not offer to unlock the bootloader once the handset is fully paid off and the contract has ended - that would be too reasonable!)

SIM Locking

And here we get to the crux of the argument. SIM locking. This crap would never fly in places like Europe or Central America, because there are so many phone carriers, and people have to constantly bounce between then when they cross country borders, which is as common as crossing state borders for us. When you buy a phone you should be able to move it between carriers. You should be able to swap the SIM as you see fit. But many devices will refuse to accept working SIMs because of some shady backdoor deal between the handset manufacturer and the carrier has prevented it. It takes more effort to SIM lock a phone than to leave it unlocked. SIM-locked devices require more R&D, likely get security updates slower than normal devices, and are passed off as "normal" here. This is egregious. Verizon is likely just banking on the fact that everyone still assumes their phones are incompatible with the rest of the world, as they were back in the bad old days of 2G and CDMA. Since LTE happened, pretty much every device is compatible in some way with every other carrier. You might loose a few LTE bands but most phones work on most carriers. The worst part about this is users usually don't realize until after the fact - they've got a SIM, they've got their phone, and when they put the two together, expecting it to work like any normal person would, it'll yell "SIM Invalid" at them, or some other sort of weird phrasing like it's the user's fault, instead of maybe correctly stating that "Verizon invested time and money to make sure you couldn't use this otherwise widely cross-compatible device without paying them a monthly fee".

Because of the obvious market demand, a not-so-little cottage industry of sketchy Internet cell phone SIM unlocking services has cropped up, offering to send desperate users an unlock code for their phone for the low low price of roughly $25. How this works I'm not sure - where do they get these codes? What is the origin? How come I can't get them from the origin and instead have to go through a site that looks like one giant early-2000s pop-up ad? Sometimes these websites are legit and actually will unlock phones, but it's a roll of the dice every time, and I imagine desperate users aren't going to be too careful about how they vet these sites. There's lots of money to be made here if you don't care about morals. Don't get any ideas.

Anyway, I hope this has been somewhat helpful or at least entertaining. Cell phones are frustrating enough already, and you shouldn't do yourself the disservice of buying them locked and hobbled from a carrier. Buy unlocked devices from manufacturers, or places that sell unlocked phones. You can find a bunch on Amazon or Best Buy. Swappa is a great place to buy used and you can often filter unlocked devices. Things have gotten better over the years here in America, but we've still got a long way to go, and it looks like they'll be getting worse again soon. With a lack of strong regulation, and an apathetic user base, it's only a matter of time before the carriers invent some new scheme by which to bludgeon us.
Watch out.
