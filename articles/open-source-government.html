Proposal for a Transparent, Open-Source Government

America is the best country on the planet because our citizens are afforded the most freedom. We are not free from reasoable restrictions - that would be anarchy, the opposite of freedom. Innovation comes from America first because our people are the most able to explore new ideas and bring successful inventions to bear in our free market.

It is imperative in a Free society that the government should strive to be transparent to the people it serves. Records, budgets, meeting minutes, schedules, and voting records, if not senitive, should be easily accessible to citizens. This transparency would not harm the government, but help it serve its purpose better. Citizens would feel their personal ownership and trust in the government increase, would get more involved, and the government could do its job - representing the people - better.

On the flip side, the private lives of citizens should not be subject to undue scrutiny or surveillance by governments. We close our doors and blinds for a reason - not everything we do in our homes is secret, but it is private. We should have a meaningful expectation that our privacy is not compromised by our governments. The 4th and 5th amendments ensure we as private citizens are free to live private lives.

Today, we are at a tipping point. Over the last decade, it has become apparent that our privacy is being used as currency. We are under constant surveillance by massive monopolistic corporations, and through them, our government. This is wrong. Unwarranted surveillance, in all its forms, its antithetical to a free society, and should be rejected outright by all our leaders who value the principles of freedom and independence this country was founded on.

The American spirit of innovation is stifled by unnecessary restrictions on freedom. When we are shackled by undue demands to our time and attention, we can't thrive, and we can't do what makes our society great. When good people are ensnared in traps, they can't fulfill their potential. These days, it's hard to tell where the traps are. Time was, these traps were constructed of real-world objects, and could be easily detected and avoided. Things were more simple. Now a small set of enormous companies monopolize markets, convince the world that specific products are necessary to live, and ensnare most of humanity in a web of addiction and endless consumerism. The ones lucky or skillful enough to avoid this trap are seen as outcasts or weirdos, and shunned by the mainstream consumers.

Overwhelmingly, these traps are constructed of one thing: Proprietary Software. (What is proprietary software?)

The biggest trap of all is Proprietary Software. Proprietary software is often, if not exclusively, developed with the intent change the behavior of the user in order to increase its propagation. Although life without Proprietary Software is possible, it's pushed on consumers as if it's the only option, and there have been far too few vocal detractors. All but the smallest niche of people carry around Proprietary Software with them all day, every day in the form of their cell phone. Most people participating in society own smartphones[1], the biggest vector for Proprietary Software, and use them for everything from chatting with friends and family and unlocking their front door to paying for parking or rent and watching movies. The surface-level usefulness of Proprietary Software cannot be ignored, but there needs to be a more careful examination of the drawbacks.

The evidence[2] cannot be ignored - Proprietary Software is built with surveillance in mind. Apple computers know every time you open a non-Apple app, and have the ability to block any app on their platforms. Android phones have location tracking that cannot be turned off. Almost every app that a user downloads on their smartphone contains tracking code[3] that snoops on their behavior and reports back to the vendor - or an intermediary. The list goes on and on, and evidence isn't hard to find. If you pay attention, more violations to our citizens' privacy happen every day.

There is no question about it - Proprietary Software is built to harm and exploit users, rather than empower them. It is built to lock users into closed-off "ecosystems", preventing interoperability with other systems, increasing dependence on systems that extract data. Heck, the only thing the diametrically opposed Apple and Google can agree to standardize on is yet another way to track users![4]

While the US government takes a hands-off approach (mostly) to these companies, it uses their invasive behavior as a vector for secret surveillance on citizens. They shut down things they don't like, and try to block or backdoor encryption. This is the biggest problem with Proprietary Software - it enables and encourages surveilance of its users, and both the companies and the government behind them take advantage of this fact.[5]

All but the most technically inclined are stuck in this Orwellian techno-dystopia. It takes grit and determination to get out of this hole, and chances are you, dear reader, don't know anyone who is able to live without Proprietary Software.[6] There is hope, and it is easier than you may think. The knowledge is free, and it requires nothing but time.

In the face of all this, I have three radical proposals.

Ban or heavily burden the distribution of proprietary software at the highest level possible

Ban or heavily burden the usage of proprietary software at a government and public works level

Embrace the ideals of Free Software and integrate them into government operations

These proposals seek to ensure that citizens' freedom is respected, no one is forced to use proprietary software, and the practice of making proprietary software is discontinued.

The main argument against the first proposal focuses on money and so-called intellectual property. I do not believe that internally-used software should be forced out into the open. Private companies have every reason to keep their business private too. But if a company is developing software they intend to distribute to outsiders, the source code should be made available as well. Legacy proprietary software distributed without is source code is how we get in situations where businesses are still using insecure old operating systems like Windows XP because their old app breaks on later versions. This puts businesses, and their customers, in danger. If the source was available, they would be able to re-build the software and fix it for newer operating systems. It also increases trust and goodwill in the provider of the software.

Good support models can be constructed around Free/Open Source Software. The most successful software project in the world, Linux, which runs on 100% of all the world's supercomputers and most of the web servers you connect to every day, is completely Free. Billion-dollar companies like Red Hat and Oracle have large portions of their business selling support contracts for Free/Open Source Software. The core components of Apple's macOS are based on Free Software. Netflix and Facebook servers run Free Software because they need peak performance. Free Software is almost always better than the Proprietary alternative.

The second proposal focuses on the ethics of using taxpayer dollars to fund corporate surveillance (and through it, government surveillance). No one should be forced to pay for proprietary software - it is unethical to create, distribute, and purchase. It is always more expensive than the Free Software alternatives. Distributing documents in a Microsoft Word format enforces the purchasing of a Windows PC, and on top of that, Microsoft Office, whereas Libreoffice and Open Office formats are Free, and the programs can be downloaded for no cost and used on any operating system. Anyone knowledgable enough to know the difference between Free/Open Source Software and Proprietary Software would always choose Freedom, without question. No one chooses a fascist dictatorship or communist regime. Proprietary Software, much like non-deomcratic forms of government, must be forced upon the user.

Imagine a choice between two cars:

  • Car A is a normal car, you can take it to whichever mechanic you want, can drive everywhere, and takes standard fuel
  • Car B is a self-driving car, that only takes fuel from the manufacturer, can only be repaired with manufacturer-supplied parts, can only drive to places on an acceptable locations list, and has a camera in the car pointed at the passengers that you cannot cover up
Only the foolish would choose Car B, because there is no freedom. It facilitates arbitrary control by an obviously oppressive company and restricts the usage far beyond what one would consider "normal boundaries" for a car. On the surface, the manufacturer will only advertise the positive aspects, and the abuses to your freedom will likely only be found when you run up against the boundaries. By then it's too late.

That is Proprietary Software. The manufacturers of smartphones have done well getting consumers to divide into quasi-religious factions (Mac or PC, iPhone or Android) constantly at war with one another, because this prevents them from seeing the real issue - the slow but deliberate stripping away of their freedoms.

Finally, the third proposal is more nuanced and complicated, but I'd argue the most important. I want git, but for public policy. Look at the git repo for Signal, a popular messaging app. You can click around, view all the code that goes into making the app. That's not the important part, since public policy should already be visible. The important part is the commit history, or the list of all the changes that go into every file. Git, a version control system for software development, stores every single version of every single file, so we can see when changes were made, who made them, and why. This commit changes build versions, but you can see the differences between the old and new files. You can do this for every change, see the initial versions of the files, and run simple, accessible, free tools to do even deeper inspection. You see what I'm getting at?

Do that, but for laws, rules, regulations - all public policy.

You, a government official, a public servant, a defender of Freedom and the principles that make our nation great, cannot let this stand. We The People deserve better. We deserve transparency, interactivity, and openness. We deserve privacy. We deserve Freedom.
