Port Forwarding and Dependencies

[link—standalone]

Time for a tale of mystery and horror. Every so often my parents call me about computer/network/services problems, and I always solve them free of charge since because they dealt with me for 18+ years, and also I love them and stuff.

My dad has this thing in his house that's an internet-connected box that controls physical things in his house (so, already a bad idea; we've all seen that episode of Mr. Robot). It's got a bunch of outputs - CEC, IR, (and RS232 and Ethernet, neither used at this point) that control the entertainment center stuff and switch between inputs. He and my mom can control it on the iPad (which is what he likes). There's also a big physical remote with BlackBerry-style wheel and back button on it, which is AWESOME. Anyhow, the computer box portion of it broke.

So this internet connected mystery computer that has unlimited access to his home network (even though I gave him a guest network with isolation!) needs to get an update from the manufacturer to re-enable iPad control because that was the part that broke. So he calls the technicians and they say they can do it via teamviewer, since it's corona time, and nobody wants visitors. He calls me today, frantically asking for the admin password for his router (which I mysteriously have, and he even more mysteriously does not) so he can give it to a technician. I obviously tell him no.

But then he explains that the technician is going to remote into his mac, change his router settings to set up two port forwards into this mystery box, and then he'll update it from outside.

Anyway, my dad conferences me in with this guy, and since I can't drop everything and offer my dad a better solution in the next 20 minutes, I guess this thing has to happen. I insist the technician remove the port forwards after he is done with them, since there's little else I can do. He agrees. My dad already has his grill, doorbell, ancient home security system, Christmas lights, generator, and cooking temperature probes on his home network, so it's a lost cause at this point. I'll struggle against it when I have more time.

While we're on the call, the technician causally mentions that most of the time, they don't have to have port forwards, because they usually put VPN servers in these boxes. "One button, and then bang, I'm in your home network!" Excuse me?! This box doesn't need to be Internet connected or even network connected - it's just an IR repeater - but yet, here we are.

Anyway, moral of the story - given the least charitable explanation, how comfortable are you with the stuff in your home? You should strive for less dependence on technology, especially technology of the mysterious kind. If you don't know how it works and you can't repeat it, don't use it! If you can't service it yourself, and it's a proprietary box that has full access to something you'd be hesitant to give an adversary full access to, don't use it! If facts presented as writ make the thing sound creepy, it probably is. And if it's a lynchpin of your home networking or entertainment setup, you're dependent on it.

I strive to remove these things from my life while others these days try to add them in - and I'm supposed to be the "tech guy". Strange times we live in.

P.S. Also, if you must compromise your family's safety, make sure it's at least for something cool. This ain't it, chief.

by Adam Thiede
