Pointlessness of Appliance Computer Hacking

[link—standalone]

It's come to my attention recently that Nintendo stalks people distributing console hackware and tries to bully them into to sign an NDA. Stories on this also tend to bring up relevant history, like iPhone jailbreaking and PS3 OtherOS/Linux. Each time something technically interesting happens with these otherwise boring appliance computers, the manufacturer or legal entity involved in making money off of them screams "piracy!!!" and tries to sue the pants off the 19-year-old who is tinkering with a toy in his basement and telling his friends about it on the Internet.

As much as game game console hax was how a lot of us got interested in "computer stuff" early in our lives, shouldn't we, as adults, now understand that throwing time and effort at modifying appliance hardware is kind of a fool's errand? Recently there's been some effort involved in porting Linux to the Apple M1 chip - a proprietary custom Apple-designed ARM SoC that seems very cool but lacks support for a real operating system yet. This is effort in the wrong direction. It is not a thing we should support. We also don't want to encourage people to buy hardware with the promise of future software support. There are many examples of the software not being delivered.

The manufacturers of appliance computers always look to close the holes that hackers use to have fun and tinker. Every iPhone update closes off the previous jailbreaks. The Nintendo Switch had a flaw in the hardware so they released new versions and don't sell the old one anymore. Some consoles like the Xbox even have a limited number of hardware "efuse" devices that are burned out by a software update, ensuring you can't roll back to a previous version. These companies really don't like people tinkering with stuff that they bought! Even if it's well within your right to talk about cool software you wrote, the companies are going to try and make your life miserable if you do. Even if you do manage to create and distribute a good hack for an Xbox or something, chances are you won't be able to play online with your buddies since you can't update your console's software, you might get banned, you may not be able to buy new games, and you may not even be able to play games you've already bought! Congratulations, your Xbox is now a brick.

So, while modding the Switch to run Linux might be a cool trick, aren't we better off investing time in making open ecosystems better, not heading down a dead end, playing cat-and-mouse, getting applicances to do things they're not supposed to? As soon as you release any info about running Linux on the M1 or PS3 or Switch, the manufacturer will close that loophole, burn the efuse, and you can't ever do it again. Wasted effort. Instead, what about getting Linux to work on a RISC-V system? That's where we should be heading, instead of proprietary hardware run by litigious companies that don't want us anyway.

by Adam Thiede
