Pragmatism

[link—standalone]

- Inspired by Bring Your Own Client by Geoffrey Litt -


For a number of years I've been interested in "minimal" cell phones - alternatives to the duopoly of iOS and Android, like the Light Phone and similar devices. There is a draw there for me - I don't like using phones, I tend to carry a laptop wherever I go, I made people use Signal so I could text from my laptop, I'm not a fan of the way phones operate, and all I really need in that case is a data modem for the laptop. For me, this is an ideal way to operate and live my life - I get messages when I want to, the phone is effectively a data modem, and maybe it plays music, too. I would prefer not to communicate with it unless I'm under duress, since unlike real computers, it's not a device that can really be made secure without a lot of effort and modification. But nobody else is going to allow me to work like this.

If I want to own a stick-shift car, I can do that. If I want to have a left-handed soldering iron, I can do that, right-handers be damned. If I want to use a terminal-only Arch Linux setup and check my email in an esoteric command-line email app, I'm free to do that. Doesn't matter that you use Outlook on Windows or Gmail on an Android phone. We can still exchange emails.

But it all falls down when it comes to phones, and phone apps, which is just the way people work now. I don't really like Signal that much. It's the best thing I've ever done for my social circle, but it requires a phone number, depends on a smartphone app, and the desktop app is electron. The Signal people refuse to make their program federated or accept third-party clients because it would slow down their pace of innovation, or reflect poorly on their service. I get it, makes sense. They want to be free to innovate and quickly push new features to everyone.

With smartphones, smartphone apps, and things revolving around the smartphone sphere, I can't bring my own client. There is lock-in to one of the two awful platforms, with very few acceptable ways to alleviate the privacy and security concerns. I can't make the type of decisions I would want to revolving technology - making independent choices based on my personal values of Freedom, minimalism, privacy, and decentralization. This bothers me quite a bit.

I want to use cool technical stuff. Stuff that's more secure, private, interesting, and technically superior. I want to put my values first, much like many people refuse to buy products from China or only eat "organic" food. But the fact that people don't care about tech means I can't since I still have to talk to them. I tried it years ago, before Signal was a great solution, and people just ended up SMS or iMessaging my wife, using her as a middleman. That also puts a huge burden on her - she's not my secretary, and if my objective is "secure, private communication", this completely flaunts that. It makes my efforts useless.

What I really want is to bring my own client, everywhere, for everything. I want to bring my own weirdo phone that isn't like everyone else's iPhone or Android. I want to bring my own OS, my own desktop interface, my own software and hardware. I mandate that everything I use respect my freedom and privacy. I mandate that I can use all my software anonymously and without reservation. But as long as others are not willing to step up and do something a little more difficult, invest a bit of time into digital well-being, I can't do what I want, and it's mainstream Android phones and Electron apps for me. Pragmatism. I have to take what I can get from the things that work in ways I don't like. What other industry or area is like this? Is there another situation in which others can make a decision for you, and you're locked into something you don't like because of their poor choices?

by Adam Thiede
