Working From Home

[link—standalone]

While I've done my fair share of remote work in the past, working full-time from home is a new experience for me. I'm fortunate that my field makes remote work very easy. Even so, it can also put a lot of strain and confusion on a family's home life. Through trial and error I've found out what works and what doesn't. These are my findings.

As with everything, compartmentalization is critical. Space, time, and equipment are my most critical components.

Though this should go without saying, don't use your personal computer or phone for work. Unless you own the company and are making those decisions for yourself, your employer should be paying for your computer and phone if they expect you to use them for work. If you use your personal systems for work you won't ever get any down-time. Trying to watch Netflix? Here comes a Slack message. Playing some phone games? There's a flurry of work emails. (Also, don't install any corporate management spyware on your personal systems.)

Commuting to an office can feel different from working at home because of the initialization time. You get dressed and presentable, gather your things, get in a car and drive to work. (Remember driving? I kinda forgot how!) All these various tasks may not seem like much, but they're getting you into the work mindset. You're preparing for your day. When you work from home, the initialization time is zero - you roll out of bed, bleary-eyed, un-caffeinated, and pajama-clad, and you can start work. This is a bad habit to get into. I make sure to get up with ample time before my work day starts, so I can exercise, shower, and get some coffee before starting my work day.

When you work from home, don't just wear sweat pants and a t-shirt. Wear the same thing you'd normally wear to the office (maybe minus the shoes and tie) so that your body still feels like it's working. I used to keep a pair of work shoes in the office - that's now a separate pair of slippers under my desk. The small act of getting dressed in work clothes - and changing out at the end of the day - will help you keep your work and personal mindsets separated. It's a physical way of telling yourself, "I'm on the clock now, time to get working!"

It's also important to compartmentalize the time you spend. Begin and end your work day on a schedule, and set up some automation to help - I have a simple digital alarm clock that goes off a few minutes before work every morning, signaling to me to start my transition. When you take breaks, walk away from your work area. Set your status to "Away" or "Lunch" in your chat program, so that people know you're not at your workstation. Make sure you take adequate time for breaks.

Your workplace should ideally be isolated and comfortable. Your desk cannot be your bed, the kids' play room, or your wife's sewing area. Try and set aside a location where you do only work. If you can't have a dedicated space for it, then a shared space is fine, as long as you are able to make a clear transition at the end of the day. My first attempt at this was putting a tiny separate desk in my closet, hooking up some spare equipment to my work computer, and leaving it there. For isolation, it worked great - the desk was in the closet, so I could close it! Critically, though, it was incredibly uncomfortable and made work a lot less enjoyable. I was staring at the wall in the closet! It left me feeling drained and gave me headaches.

I bought some more equipment, moved my desk so I look out the window, and now I have docking station that works with both my work laptop and personal laptop. While I still end up using the same desk setup for both work and play, it doesn't bother me that much, since the work and personal computers are totally different environments. If your work is portable, try moving around to different places and see how it feels in each location. Working on the back porch in the spring or fall is quite lovely. It's important to keep morale up, and just being able to stare out a window while pausing for a thought is one of those small victories.

Most people have been FaceTiming for long enough that this is common sense, but I made some initial mistakes here. It's in your best interest to protect the privacy of your home, and to not display anything controversial or distracting in your video conferences. When using webcams, make sure your backdrop is a low-activity, neutral area, and doesn't feature things like violent movie posters, frequent changes in light level, or a frazzled wife chasing a naked baby. Webcam covers are great for preventing accidental activation.

Most offices will provide you with some good equipment - pens, notebooks, headsets, chairs... you likely won't have these items in the same quantity or quality at home. With my first few paychecks I got a better mouse, monitor, and headset. This drastically improved my ergonomic experience working at my desk all day, and helped me out in my frequent hours-long screen-share meetings. A comfy headset with a good microphone is a must if you're using it all day. You can usually get your employer to foot the bill for this sort of thing if it's standard equipment necessary for your position.

Activity is as important as comfort. Remember to get up and stay active - alarms will help here, too. A pull-up bar in your door frame, a walk around the block, or a small set of five-pound dumbbells will keep your blood flowing. Adjustable desks are expensive, but worth the cost if you're now home-bound full-time.

It all comes down to the same basic rules that also apply to being in the office: show up on time, prepared, and get your work done. If you can manage that without yelling at your family or tearing your hair out, you're doing great!
