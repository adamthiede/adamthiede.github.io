ditch your cell phone

[link—standalone] Most people who have a traditional computer these days have a laptop. Companies issue their employees laptops, and most students these days buy laptops. People who live in smaller spaces, or can't dedicate a whole desk to a tower and screen, get themselves a portable computer. Desktops aren't very popular these days, and usually those who have a desktop, which tend to be less flexible and more expensive, will have a laptop as well.

Even laptops are giving way to more portable devices. Tablets and smartphones are becoming increasingly productive, doing more and more every iteration. And everyone these days always carries a smartphone. The connected world, right there in your hip pocket. They can do amazing things: work email on the go, conference calls, mobile hotspot capability for your laptop, check in on your servers from afar, group chat with your international buddies, see your grandma's face from 1,000 miles away, get new music and podcasts on your way to class, and keep up with your family's photo stream all hours of the day. These seem like the perfect devices. Why wouldn't we want one device that can do everything for us?

You've been misled!

Your smartphone is your adversary. They're designed to pull you in, with bright colors, friendly sounds, notifications, and feeds. Their only purpose is to generate more data and revenue from your time, attention, and battery life. This little object doesn't serve you. These apps and feeds are avenues for advertisement, tracking your every move on the web and (sometimes not so) subtlety changing the way your brain works. You need to regain control.

We have so much functionality on these little computers, but if you don't curate your content, it will rule you. If it's not critical to how you work or communicate, get rid of it. Keep your notes app, maybe your music and podcasts, but get rid of news, social media, and games. Liberally apply do not disturb modes, silence your notifications, turn your ringer down low or off. Your phone should be a lifeline, not a television set or news device. The browser is all you should need in a pinch.

Break this little glass square's hold on your brain.

Another way to help you disconnect and retain your sanity with this attention hook in your pocket all day is a color filter.

Turn your phone to grayscale, and keep it that way for a week. You'll find the lack of bright colors make you a little less attracted after a while.

If you want to read, get a book or an e-reader. They won't distract you with incessant notifications about your sister's tweets or your second cousin's 34 photos of his meal. Write a lot? Grab a pen and journal. Want to watch some Netflix? Your laptop or desktop are much larger screens with leagues better speakers. For music, you can't beat a classic iPod. They sound great, and the ping of a text arriving won't interrupt you 15 minutes into Dark Side of The Moon.

My point is, dedicated devices may seem outmoded, bulky, or disconnected, but that's almost the point. Things that matter should have weight in your life. You should feel that journal stretching your back pocket. Your books should take up space in your bag. Your headphones and music player should be a world unto their own, and your camera should make you work for the shot.

The convergence of all this functionality into one tiny orb of distraction has helped us in many ways, and I'm not advocating getting rid of them. They're amazing devices that aid many of us in so many uncountable ways. Just loosen their grip. Be comfortable without them. Recall a time without them, and remember that going outside, being away from home, away from your telephone and internet connection, or even just in the other room, was also being free.
