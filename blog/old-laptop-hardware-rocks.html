Old Laptop Hardware Rocks

[link—standalone]

Some old-ish machines, opened up

I love old laptops. I like pretty much all old computers, because they're a snapshot in time of where we were, and it helps me realize how far things have come. (Or helps learn lessons about how far things have regressed.) But I especially enjoy old laptops, because laptops, in particular, have always been really interesting computers.

Inside each laptop is a unique solution to the same problem; the problem being of course "how do we cram all this in there and make it work safely". Different manufacturers (and even different models) can take a different approach to the same problems. Some open through the bottom, and some open on the top, making the keyboard an integral part of the disassembly. Some have hard disks externally accessible without full disassembly. Some pack every component on to the motherboard, while others modularize with ribbon cables and daughterboards. Some had many cute little doors for individual components, like the RAM or hard disk, while keeping the rest of the system locked away in a plastic coffin.

I like to think I've learned a bit from observing many machines of different vintages over the last however-many years I've been tearing apart laptops. What follows are some observations and "lessons".

Long ago, RAM was stacked, which made machines thicker. Then it became side-by-side, which took more space, then manufacturers only provided one SO-DIMM slot, then it became soldered. Batteries going internal helped save more space, the removal of the DVD drive more still. Many components have gotten smaller, but really all that means is laptops have gotten thinner and more fragile. I bet most folks wouldn't notice an extra few MM of thickness put toward device sturdiness.

Newer machines have a lot less empty space in them. Connecters aren't standing up, they're lying flat. Things are not multi-layered anymore. Those little doors for individual components often made other parts way harder to get at. A consequence of this is that newer machines tend to use the "whole bottom panel" strategy: 9-12 screws and the bottom cover of a machine is off, and you can access everything. I like this better.

Lots of Dell machines prioritized being able to remove the hard drive without removing the bottom panel. Smart. It was in its own slot, but it wasn't a "manylittle doors" situation (as above). This was really cool.

Captive screws are a godsend and plastic clips are an abomination. They will eventually break. Metal chassis stand the test of time.

If you want to see how repairable a laptop really is, see how many power connectors it has (USB-C has made this a little better), and see if they can be replaced individually. When a power connector gets loose or breaks, can you replace it for $15, or do you need to replace the whole darn motherboard, soldered CPU and all? Newer machines are not nearly as good at this.

Keyboards are now almost impossible to replace. On older machines, they often came off with very little fuss, and now it's either they have to come out last, after the battery and motherboard, or they don't come out at all.

Older, thicker fans made lower pitched noise, which sounds quieter. Older machines had way more space to put coolers, too. A modern 15W CPU will spin its thin whiny screamer of a fan more often than an older 35W laptop chip. Energy efficiency is cool, and it's led to fanless laptops, but fans have gotten worse. We're in a "middle period". Performance kind of stagnated because of the drop in power consumption and we're finally past it, but we're ripe for another efficiency gain. Soon all laptops should be fanless, or rarely use fans.

Models that get cute and creative with component placement are my favorite. The Lenovo X220/X230 palmrest is a super fun thing to open. The T430 has one SO-DIMM under the keyboard and one under the door on the bottom, and its Wi-Fi card underneath the keyboard. The HP in the picture has a swing-out smartcard reader resting with rubber feet on top of the hard drive, and the Dell keeps its Bluetooth card right under a little plastic piece by the hinge. It's so fun to think about how these things happened, and who made the decisions!

Socketed CPUs in laptops were awesome and they didn't have to go away. The last system I have with a socket isn't much thicker than one with a soldered CPU from the same year, and when laptops got thinner than those, they lost Ethernet and decent cooling anyway. Not worth it.

Vertical USB ports were super cool. Vertical fans were not, just funny. MXM cards and other weirdo daughterboards are also hilarious, but in a bad way.

I once took a pile of four Dell E6440 laptops, all with broken components, and made two working systems. It is my favorite laptop I've ever opened up because I didn't break anything during the surgeries, and all the components were really easy to get at and very understandable. HP EliteBooks from the same era come in second because of how they label screws. That's awesome!

Anyway. Kind of stream-of-consciousness, but I like disassembling these systems and seeing how they were put together. If you're a laptop maker or know one, please email me! I'd love to talk to you about your hardware and why you made the decisions you did. That's all. Have a good evening.

by Adam Thiede
