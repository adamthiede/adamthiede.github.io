Good Tools and Bad People

[link—standalone]

I have a love-hate relationship with technology/culture news site The Verge. On the one hand, the people who founded it are cool and interesting. They tend to cover stuff pretty factually in the realm of technology - their journalists are sharp and funny. On the other hand, they seem to ignore some basic facts in service of eradicating free thought, especially if it's thought they don't like. They've published a piece about Signal's "need" to moderate usage of their service. It is well written and offers real concerns about a real problem - bad people can use Signal. They can send whatever they want to each other and Signal isn't doing a damn thing about it!

But it's actually not a problem. Signal makes a tool. People use the tool. Signal can't monitor who is using it, how it's being used, and what's being shared there. The main reason people use Signal at all is because Signal can't see anything on their service, and that you can trust that this is the case, since it's fully Open Source. How would they "moderate"? How would they censor content? How do they even know at all what's being published on their messaging service without destroying the one and only reason people use and trust them?

Almost two years ago, I was concerned about Facebook's idealism and how that would eventually get them into trouble. Fast forward two years - last month they joined other social media sites in banning the President of the United States of America from suite of websites. Because of the way Facebook is constructed, they can see every word you type on their system. Because they have the ability to moderate, they have the responsibility to.

If you make a good tool, and that good tool also doesn't report back on its use case, then people are going to want to use it. A portion of the people in the world are bad people. I use my hammers and shovels and soldering irons as tools to get work done. There's probably someone in the world who uses hammers to murder people and soldering irons for torture. That's not how those tools are designed, but if you make a tool, and it's good at doing what's on the tin, people are going to misuse it. But that doesn't mean you shouldn't try.

There are probably more good people using Signal to chat with their family and have normal discussions than there are bad people plotting to overthrow their perfectly normal government. If bad people use the service, that's just a price you have to be willing to pay for making a high quality product that's also respectful of the user's basic human rights. Sometimes making the world a better place for good people also makes it a better place for bad people. Overall, it is worth it to help the good people. Sometimes bad people use good tools. That's just how it works.

by Adam Thiede
