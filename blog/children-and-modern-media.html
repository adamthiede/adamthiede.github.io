Children and Modern Media

[link—standalone]

Let's start here from the assumption that the majority of modern media aimed at children - television, video games, movies, books (in some cases) - is mostly bad. The quality of children's media is overall fairly low to begin with, since they don't have higher standards created by greater experience and more nuanced though. Truthfully, no adult consumes children's media after their teenage years, but most adults would consider going back to music, movies and books that they first encountered as a teenager, since the audiences are much closer in maturity level. Since the introduction of YouTube and automatic computer-generated content, the standards have gone down even futher.

When the standards for children's media are "the child must like it, and the parents must consent to buy it", it's already a pretty low bar. But when you remove the need for parental consent, and you remove any other limits to publication, like "must be on a DVD in a store", or "must be broadcast on television", the standard slides even futher. Kids will watch anything, and if you give them the most uncanny valley AI-generated 90-second clip uploaded to YouTube from a server farm in Belarus, you're gonna get kids that will watch anything.

When a parent hands a kid a smartphone or tablet, or any content-consuming device, they may belive that telling the kid "don't do X" will prevent them from doing X. They may be a little more technically inclined and set up things like Apple's Screen Time restrictions, and have faith in that. But kids are hackers. They will find a dozen ways around any roadblocks you set up, and before you know it your 4 year old is using your smartphone to watch unsavory Internet content without that was supposed to be blocked.
Bottom Line - don't hand a kid a computer with unfettered Internet access!

My primary fear about this whole thing is that while we're doing a decent job of it, 90% of these parents who are unaware of the ins and outs of the whole YouTube platform, or who just don't understand computers and networks, let their kids gaze at their smartphones all day with no thought to the long term effects. Then their kids who are 3 and have already seen Frozen seven times before breakfast will shame my kid when what we've been doing is the correct thing. And now I've got to explain to my kid for the tenth time this week that these smartphone things are bad, but who's she going to want to fit in with - her dad, or the other kids?

Parenting, I'm coming to realize, is frustrating because doing the right thing can be incredibly hard, and the wrong thing, the thing that'll get you a few more minutes of relaxation and peace, is easy. It's like a slot machine, or an addictive drug - it's easy to just keep doing it because you've been doing it, but very difficult to quit. Sometimes initially it'll seem unhealthy to quit. Things will get worse before they get better. And I'm no paragon of fatherhood either - I've done a pretty lousy job so far, I think. But I try my hardest everymost days, and I attempt to learn when I'm doing something wrong. What I do will have lasting effects on my child, and mistakes haunt me, as they should. But hey, at least I'm not gonna get the computer stuff wrong.

by Adam Thiede
