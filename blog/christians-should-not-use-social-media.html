Christians Should Not Use Social Media

[link—standalone]

Social Media - Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, Tik Tok, Snapchat, and whatever the hottest new social media platform of the day is - they're quite popular. Many people use them, and they're ostensibly useful for their purpose - allowing users to share text and media with a select group of other users, with relative levels of broadcast. There are several general purposes to services like this:

"show me what I want to see" (subscriptions, follows, friends)
"share how I want to share" (make public posts visible to all, keep private messages private)
"allow me to discover new things" (search for people, topics, and other data)

However, that's just surface-level. Everyone also knows that these services are built to be addicting on purpose. They collect every bit of data they can, even data users don't think they provide, and tailor services based on this data for maximum retention and engagement; simply put they learn from users and display content that keeps users on the site as long as possible. Not what the user actually wants to see, but what keeps him or her scrolling. This is a clear violation of the purpose of the service - "show me what I ask to see".

The service also retains copies of deleted data, has the ability to browse "private" messages and posts, and often hides the posts of a user to their fellow users if those posts won't keep users on the service. Censorship and message alteration is a normal feature of these types of services. A user's content may be flagged, leading to "shadowbans" or outright removal from the service. If a post intended for a small audience would be useful to the service for retention, it will be heavily promoted and "go viral". This lack of clarity about how things are actually shared is an obvious violation of another principle, "share how I want to share".

Social media services never fail to promote discovery, but it's usually only posts and accounts that are deemed "safe", not really in line with what the user wants to see. Worse, there's no way to tailor this discovery or put on reasonable restrictions, like "don't show me softcore pornography" or "no politics". For most social media services, the things users will be able to search for and discover are either massively popular already, or pre-vetted, not-yet-banned topics that drive engagement. With this the third goal, "allow me to discover new things", is also void.

Most social media sites don't make good on their surface-level promises. They pretend that they do, but instead fulfill their own goals of exploiting users for profit. If it's not clear by now, social media platforms are tools for evil. If a platform is built to do evil, and the operator is using it for evil deeds, should Christians even consider using it at all? I've heard people argue that posting good Christian messages on these platforms is "using the platform against itself" - seems like a noble goal. But continuing to use it at all will continue to drive more people to sign up, if that's where the message is available. All of this usage only benefits the social media service in the end. More users to addict, more data to gather, more ads to sell, more minds and hearts to change. And what are the chances that the platform will only promote other Christian messages to Christian users? How long will those truly Christian messages be allowed to stay around? Casting Christian pearls before the swine of social media platforms is arguably equivalent to preaching the gospel to Sodomites. Not even God could find ten good men there.

We're well beyond needing to provide evidence of the harm social media has done to the world. You, reader, could probably name more than I could. The injustice of this type of worldly attraction, despite its attractiveness, must not be something Christians succumb to. We are discerning when it comes to books, music, schools, dress, relationships, our words, our food and drink, consumer goods, and how we treat this planet. So why the blindness against technology, and technological services?

I call my fellow Christians to give up social media. Not just Facebook and Twitter, but all forms of this pox upon society. The surface-level use case of these services - communication and sharing of data on the Internet - can be achieved in many different ways using technology that is simpler, and not designed for evil.

by Adam Thiede
