Building Businesses on Proprietary Platforms

[link—standalone]

With the impending ban of Chinese spyware platforms TikTok and WeChat hanging over our heads in the US, I've been reading a bit about how the users on these platforms are lamenting the loss of their preferred social media site, as well as potential advertising/brand deals. I've got thoughts about building a business on a platform you don't control. (TL;DR: it's a stupid idea)

A few things must be addressed first though - if the United States is a free-as-in-freedom country, which it purportedly is, then our government can't feasibly ban a media platform. They'd have to convince every ISP to not route traffic outside the US's borders, effectively isolating us from the wider Internet (or, convince every other country to cut ties with China, but that far less likely). VPNs are stupid-easy to employ these days, and if those are banned, then we have cloud hosting, proxies, and Tor. There's no way that something that's basically just a single website can really be banned, especially if it's hosted outside the US. It's probably already functional as a Progressive Web App anyway, so it's not like removing the app from the platforms' stores will do anything meaningful.
Secondly, as much as this is a laughably unenforceable proposition (just like anything corona related), the US government can't not do it. The duty of our leaders it to protect us from threats, foreign and domestic, visible and invisible, and this is one of those fairly obvious threats to the freedom and liberty of Americans. This has been a long time coming, too; the US Army already banned it last year.

Given all that, does it really matter if TikTok gets banned? People will still find ways to use it, and like MySpace before it, if it truly dies, users will move. These days, as far as I'm aware, people use Facebook less and less, and have migrated over to things like Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok. Instagram's cloning TikTok's features - just move there. Or, even better, don't use a platform controlled by a third party in the first place.

Proprietary platforms like TikTok, WeChat, Instagram, and YouTube are usually about just one or two things - making money, and spying on their users. They go hand in hand - the data gathered from the spying is valuable to advertisers, and that's where it gets sold. They don't care about you or your content - it's not a platform for "creators", it's just spyware. And you want your livelihood to depend on this? You're way better off de-coupling as many things as possible, and having each individual layer of your Internet presence attached to each other with open standards, not proprietary platforms.

The Sisyphean task of constantly trying to figure out what unwritten rules you have to follow this week to be on these platforms is, it seems, all people talk about. If they're not helping you (and they're not, really) then leave! Build your own thing. Mastodon's source code exists. People got along just fine in the days before social media. Businesses rose and fell, and weren't beholden to the crack of the not-woke-enough whip, or the you-can't-blame-China ban-hammer.

Speaking of China - why would anyone want to give them any business, revenue, or anything positive at all these days? Living conditions are so bad that they created a global pandemic, they create tech products with backdoors, they're an economic powerhouse only because they treat their citizens so poorly, and they're actively interested in the destruction of free society. Of course Chinese citizens are wonderful and lovely human beings and deserve much better than they're getting, but the Chinese Government (and by extension, any company based in China, like ByteDance or Huawei or Lenovo) should not be trusted! Apathy about privacy and security is not ok. The bad guys offer some really delicious poisoned apples, but part of living a virtuous life is not enabling bad people to do bad things, even if it requires a small sacrifice on our part.

Don't help the bad guys - it makes things a little less convenient, but overall, your life (and the lives of future generations) will be better.

by Adam Thiede
