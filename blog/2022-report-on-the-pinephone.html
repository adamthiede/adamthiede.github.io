2022 Report on the Pinephone

[link—standalone]

"How do I call you with it?" "Can it do MMS yet?" "It just looks normal."

In 2022 I used the Pinephone as my main cell phone for the whole year, with the exception of one month where I was moving cross-country and needed something a little less finicky. If you're not a "must consume product" smartphone addict, then this thing is pretty much as ready for daily usage as Android was a few years ago. I fiddle with it a lot because it's my nature but one doesn't need to really fiddle with it.

On the functional side, I'm grateful that while the Pinephone looks like a smartphone, it has almost nothing in common with those things. Here's what it does well: It makes calls. It does SMS and MMS. It has an alarm clock and a camera for still images. You can play sounds out of its speaker, wired, or bluetooth headphones. Its battery lasts about 2 days if you're not constantly toying with it. It is a full Linux OS if you want to use that. But there's no "app store", no gamification of attention, and no proprietary software.

I hopefully will not buy another phone, ever, that doesn't behave like the pinephone does. It's the platonic ideal of a smartphone. It's actually the thing most people say they want. It's cheap at $150 or $200, but unlike cheap Androids, it's not being subsidized by spying on you. It has hardware killswitches to cut power to various components. The manufacturer has low-cost replacemnt parts available. The phone uses a standard size commonly available replaceable battery. The software support cycle is effectively infinite. You can install anything you want on it. It'll work as long as the modem continues to be supported by carriers.

But in spite of how much I like the simplicity and Linux Nerdery of the Pinephone, I'm beginning to think the dumbphone people are righter than I am. If I use the Pinephone around the uninitiated, it looks like a normal smartphone. When someone pulls out a flip phone, you know there has been a deliberate choice made to obtain one of those things instead of a cheap smartphone, which is the modern path of least resistance. On the Pinephone, there is an Internet browser, the screen is not black-and-white, and while the front-facing camera is pretty much useless, it still has one. The Pinephone is as much a resistance against modernism in technology as a flip phone, perhaps even more so, but it doesn't look like it, and it doesn't make anyone think about it when they see it.

I lied a bit before: it is possible to use the Pinephone like a smartphone. It can be used to "doom scroll" through Facebook and play some mobile games. It is not good at playing videos, but it can be used to do that. Dumbphones can't do these things.^1 On most of them there is only the most basic of networking and they won't have the option to install extra software. The user doesn't have control, but the limitations of most of these things (especially the silly-expensive modern dumbphones, like the Light Phone) are in service of the well-being of the user. The Pinephone has no externally imposed limits, and that is also in service of the user. They're both serving the user in different and opposite ways.

Honestly though the more informed I am about technology, the more disillusioned I become. Life's too short for smartphones. It's too short to worry intensely about what apps people are using - I am on record being highly concerned about how I do my communication, and I think that's unhealthy long term. Electronic communication should not be primary, it should be a catalyst to real-world communication. Smartphones should be avoided, and the dumbphone people are more correct than the Linux phone people (me included) are. Usually for some weird and kind of wrong reasons, but they're in the right place.

Linux phones are great, because they give the user control, but in the end the user's power should be exercised to limit the capability of the device. In the realm of smartphones, the less it does for (and to) the user, the better. The world will improve the less people see these things used, and the less they are depended on, and that includes the Linux or de-Googled smartphones! Free they may be but they can't escape being smartphones and those who care should use them less.

1: I'm aware of flip phones that run KaiOS, which is kind of a pseudo-smartphone OS that includes smartphone features. Those don't count.
