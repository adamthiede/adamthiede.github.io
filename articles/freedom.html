The Value of Freedom

Recently I've been grappling with defining true Freedom. It's talked about a lot these days in many circles and I think a distinction must be made between what is real, and what is fake, and why that matters.

When I talk about Freedom, I don't think people really understand what I mean. Defining terms #1 is an important precursor to any rational conversation, and since "freedom" is such an over-encumbered term, I'd like to make a clear definition of what it is and is not.

Many definitions of freedom are essentially "maximum available activities, minimum restrictions". This is what I call Freedom To. This does not provide true freedom. True freedom is Freedom From, which is freedom from restrictions and burdens imposed in less-visible ways.

Freedom To

Freedom To is essentially license. License is unrestricted total freedom. Do whatever you want, no regard for morals, the thoughts and feelings of others, religious beliefs, laws, tradition, forethought, or general good sense. Actions taken with license are not always bad, but usually inevitably license is used to hurt or take advantage of others. No restrictions, internal or external, give one the Freedom To do whatever one wants.

This total lack of rules and restrictions only works if everyone in its sphere of influence shares the same exact set of values and morals. Like Communism and Anarchy, it seems to work well on paper, and on very small scale. In reality, no one should have this license. We live in a world with other people, and obviously, one's freedom only extends up to where it infringes upon the rights of others.

Freedom To is a false freedom. It does not make one free. In the Catholic perspective, sin will make one more susceptible to further sin. Sin is an affliction that warps one's ability to make correct and moral judgments. This is a rational line of thought too - a drug addiction or a bad habit is hard to break. Bad deeds are permissible with Freedom To, because there are no rules. Sin and addiction make one slaves to the sin itself, and the changes it effects on one's well-being (physical, mental, or spiritual). Sin digs a hole that is hard to climb out of, but easy and comforting to keep digging. Habits are hard to break, and just because something feels good and easy does not make it the right path.

Freedom From

Freedom From is oft overlooked, because initially, its definition does not seem to fit. Freedom From is intentionally chosen, self-imposed discipline, learning, and through these restrictions, fulfillment. #2 In true Freedom, one is not free to do whatever one desires. (Desires are not inherently bad, but some desires are bad, and these need to be recognized, faced, and curbed with discipline and healthy habits.) Freedom From is the ability to filter the good from the bad and choose the good, with help from religion and philosophy. Freedom From has restrictions, but they are restrictions imposed to make further freedoms available!

The chains of sin and slavery of bad habits will bind one to a life of misery. Moralizing one's choices and living a life of discipline keep one free of regrets. A life of Freedom To will lead to short-term pleasure, but eventually results in a broken-down body, crippling addictions, and ultimately a lack of fulfillment. Freedom From frees one from all these things. Restrictions are not imposed arbitrarily, but for one's own good. Most adults can remember that a parent's discipline felt bad in the moment, but resulted in character building that had a butterfly effect of benefits for adult life. "Eat your vegetables," "don't stay up too late," and "wear a helmet when you ride your bicycle," are all good examples of things that may not be fun, or pleasurable, or ideal now, but build good habits that prepare a young man for a successful, happy, and fulfilling adult life, where he not only treats himself well, but holds others to this high standard as well.

Why I Care

Freedom is my favorite value. I apply it everywhere I can. Because I am an adult living in the United States, generally, I'm free to make my own decisions. My family situation, my life goals, my personal desires and interests, they all combine to make a very unique individual. That is what makes the experience of being human fun - we are all unique.

To that end, this uniqueness should be celebrated through freedom. Laws and regulations form a decent enough framework, but there is a lot of wiggle room and gray area there. Society can't make specific rules that will apply well to everyone, because we're all so different, so we much choose our own rules. Our morals and values must work in addition to laws - and against, when necessary.

I refuse to make choices that bind me to some arbitrary master who does not have my best interests in mind. My life is centered around these seemingly austere or ascetic choices. While they may not make sense to you, or to anyone else, the fundamentals are the same: hard work, ownership, continual learning, responsibility, and fulfillment. Having values and earnestly trying to lead a strict moral life has given me the freedom that pleasure-seeking license never could.
